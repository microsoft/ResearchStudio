# 18 Innovation Pattern Methodologies
Data-driven methodologies synthesized from 1,370 papers (ICLR/NeurIPS/ICML 2020-2025) via three-way contrastive analysis (Oral / High-Cited / Rejected).

## C00: Adversarial Inversion and Security Diagnostic Frameworks
*27 Oral, 11 High-Cited, 4 Rejected*

### Core Principle
Identify a structural vulnerability in an existing system by inverting the role of an established technique (e.g., using a certifier as an attacker, or adversarial examples as defenders), then validate the mechanism with targeted causal interventions or principled theory. The innovation is a role-reversal or threat-model expansion that exposes or closes a gap the community assumed did not exist.

### Step-by-Step
- Step 1: Identify an empirically observed failure or success pattern in the target system (e.g., jailbreaks succeed, certified defenses underperform empirical ones).
- Step 2: Diagnose the root cause by mapping the pattern onto a structural property of the system — constraint violation type, geometric concentration, depth of alignment, or attack-surface channel.
- Step 3: Formulate the key insight as a role-reversal or inversion: take a technique designed for purpose A and repurpose it for the opposite purpose B (e.g., certifier becomes attacker, adversarial perturbation becomes defense).
- Step 4: Implement the inverted technique with a precise, minimal intervention that demonstrates causal control (e.g., activation direction erasure, latent-space adversarial injection, geometry-aware reweighting).
- Step 5: Validate on strong benchmarks — including adversarially adapted or state-of-the-art baselines — and provide theoretical grounding or formal guarantees where possible.
- Step 6: Position the result as opening a new research axis, not just improving a metric (e.g., 'safety alignment is a depth problem', 'refusal is linearly localizable').

### Success Conditions (from Oral)
- The paper identifies and names a previously unlabeled structural failure mode (e.g., 'latent adversarial examples', 'shallow safety alignment', 'mismatched generalization') that unifies a body of empirical observations — reviewers consistently praised conceptual framing.
- The key mechanism is demonstrated through a causal intervention (activation erasure, adversarial injection, geometry-aware reweighting) rather than pure correlation, giving reviewers confidence the explanation is mechanistic not just associative.
- The method simultaneously improves two objectives that were previously assumed to trade off (e.g., certified and empirical robustness together, or clean accuracy and adversarial robustness), demonstrating that the assumed trade-off was an artifact of the prior approach.
- The attack surface or threat model is explicitly extended to a new channel or adversary type that prior defenses did not consider (e.g., label space instead of image space, streaming/real-time setting, contrastive training data poisoning at 0.0001% scale).
- Strong empirical validation includes evaluation against adaptive or aware adversaries — not just standard benchmarks — and covers multiple model families or datasets to support generality claims.

### Failure Modes (from Rejected)
- Proposing a creative but theoretically loose approximation (e.g., NTK-based single-level GAN objective) where the approximation gap invalidates the main claim in practice — reviewers penalize loose theoretical surrogates heavily when empirical performance does not compensate.
- Claiming stealth or efficiency without validating against adaptive defenses that are aware of the specific strategy — stealth claims that rely only on non-adaptive evaluation are uniformly rejected.
- Poor writing quality and organizational clarity, including undefined concepts, unexplained figures, and missing motivation — 7 uniform low-score rejections for Certified Copy illustrate that technical motivation must be stated explicitly even for mechanistically sound ideas.
- Insufficient empirical scope: evaluating only on small/standard benchmarks (CIFAR-10 only, one or two datasets) when claiming broad applicability to a practical safety or robustness problem.
- Lack of differentiation from prior adaptive attack work — rejected papers in this cluster shared the failure of not positioning their innovation relative to the closest existing evasion strategies.

### Impact Drivers (from High-Cited)
- Jailbroken and 'Refusal Is Mediated by a Single Direction' became landmark citations because they provided dual-use value: an alarming empirical finding (refusal is fragile, safety fails systematically) plus an actionable mechanistic explanation that reoriented the research community's questions.
- HarmBench achieved high citation through infrastructure value rather than conceptual novelty — standardizing a fragmented evaluation ecosystem is itself a first-class contribution that every subsequent paper must reference, creating guaranteed citation pressure.
- Papers that enable a new class of attacks (direction erasure, data poisoning at 0.0001%) generate self-reinforcing citations from both the attack community (who use the technique) and the defense community (who must respond to it).
- Safety Alignment depth paper combined an empirically memorable finding (safety collapses after ~3 tokens) with a structural explanation that could be tested and falsified — this 'testable alarming claim' structure drives citations from researchers seeking to confirm, extend, or counter the finding.
- High-cited papers in this cluster tend to establish named phenomena or named methods (FlashAttention, HarmBench, SnapKV) that become vocabulary in the field — naming a new concept is a prerequisite for community adoption.

### Cognitive Barriers
- Role-reversal thinking is blocked by functional fixedness: a certifier is mentally categorized as a 'safety tool', not an 'attack generator', so using it adversarially requires deliberately breaking the category boundary.
- The assumption that 'more data means more robustness' is so deeply held that the opposite (microscopic data fraction enables global poisoning via contrastive alignment) requires empirical falsification before it becomes thinkable.
- Safety alignment is evaluated as a binary property (does it refuse?), so measuring it as a depth or distribution over generation requires shifting the unit of analysis from outputs to generation trajectories — a non-obvious change of perspective.
- The defensive use of adversarial examples (protecting content rather than attacking models) was blocked by the adversarial ML community's sole framing of adversarial examples as attack tools — applying them to protect artwork required inverting a decade of community framing.

### Reviewer Expectations
Reviewers in this cluster expect: (1) a clearly named and formally defined structural failure mode, not just an empirical observation; (2) causal evidence (e.g., ablation by activation removal, direct intervention, targeted injection) distinguishing correlation from mechanism; (3) evaluation against adaptive adversaries or baselines aware of the proposed strategy; (4) discussion of concrete mitigations or defensive implications even if the paper is primarily an attack paper; (5) positioning that explicitly identifies which prior assumptions the work invalidates. Reviewers strongly penalize papers that claim stealth or effectiveness without adaptive evaluation, and papers with poor writing that obscure the mechanism's novelty.

### Oral vs Rejected Gap
Oral papers provide a named structural diagnosis with causal evidence and validate against aware adversaries, turning an empirical pattern into an actionable mechanism. Rejected papers in this cluster either relied on theoretically loose approximations that didn't transfer to practice (NTK-GAN), or proposed valid-sounding mechanisms without positioning them against the closest prior adaptive attacks — leaving reviewers unable to assess novelty.

### Oral vs High-Cited Gap
Oral papers achieve acceptance by providing a mechanistically sound and well-validated finding. High-cited papers additionally provide dual-use value (alarming finding + actionable mechanism), establish named phenomena that become field vocabulary, or provide infrastructure (benchmarks, efficient implementations) that all subsequent researchers must use — these properties create citation pressure independent of conceptual novelty.

### Illustrative Examples
- **[Oral]** *Jailbroken: How Does LLM Safety Training Fail?*: Two named failure modes (competing objectives, mismatched generalization) explain most observed jailbreaks and guide systematic attack generation.
  - Lesson: Naming and taxonomizing failure modes into a minimal principled framework is itself a major contribution — reviewers rewarded the theoretical unification even before the practical attack generation results.
- **[HC]** *Refusal in Language Models Is Mediated by a Single Direction*: Refusal behavior in aligned LLMs collapses to a single extractable activation direction, enabling targeted removal across 13 open-source models.
  - Lesson: A surprisingly simple mechanistic finding (single linear direction) that is simultaneously alarming (refusal is fragile) and scientifically clean (linear, causal, cross-model) generates massive citations — the simplicity amplifies the alarm.
- **[Oral]** *Safety Alignment Should be Made More Than Just a Few Tokens Deep*: Safety alignment concentrates in the first few output tokens, making it bypassable by prefix manipulation; deep alignment training redistributes the signal.
  - Lesson: Reframing a binary property (refuses or not) as a structural depth property opened a new research axis — papers that create new measurement dimensions rather than just improving existing ones tend to achieve both acceptance and high citation.
- **[HC]** *HarmBench: A Standardized Evaluation Framework for Automated Red Teaming*: Systematic comparison of 18 attack methods against 33 LLMs/defenses, creating a reproducible standard for red teaming evaluation.
  - Lesson: Standardization of a fragmented evaluation landscape is a high-impact contribution independent of algorithmic novelty — being the benchmark everyone uses guarantees citations.
- **[Rejected]** *Efficient and Stealthy Backdoor Attack Triggers are Close at Hand*: Proposed using natural in-distribution patterns as backdoor triggers for stealth, but attack success rate was not competitive.
  - Lesson: Stealth claims without validation against adaptive defenses that know the strategy are not accepted — the stealth-accuracy tradeoff must be resolved empirically, not assumed.

## C01: Structural Decomposition and Cross-Domain Algorithmic Transfer
*33 Oral, 14 High-Cited, 7 Rejected*

### Core Principle
Identify that a target system's apparent bottleneck (expressiveness ceiling, scalability limit, computational cost) stems from a structural gap that can be closed by importing a formal tool from an adjacent field — or by decomposing the problem into orthogonal sub-problems each solved by a principled existing technique. The core move is recognizing that two fields share the same mathematical object, enabling a cross-domain transplant that achieves what neither field could alone.

### Step-by-Step
- Step 1: Identify a known bottleneck or ceiling in the target domain (e.g., 1-WL expressiveness limit in GNNs, sequential bottleneck in MCTS parallelism, scalability wall in graph embedding).
- Step 2: Characterize the bottleneck structurally — what information is missing, what optimization is coupled, what approximation is too coarse — using a formal or mathematical lens.
- Step 3: Search for a field that has already solved a structurally analogous problem. Look in numerical linear algebra, algebraic automata theory, formal verification, fuzzy logic, or hardware systems — wherever the abstract structure appears.
- Step 4: Identify the minimal transfer: what is the simplest structural object, statistic, or algorithm from the source field that closes the identified gap? Resist importing the full framework; find the essential mechanism.
- Step 5: Prove formal properties of the transfer (expressiveness bound, soundness guarantee, convergence) to make the contribution theory-grounded, then validate empirically on standard benchmarks plus a practical-scale application.
- Step 6: Position the result explicitly by showing the transfer enables something previously impossible (zero-shot latent communication, linear parallel speedup, SOTA expressiveness with O(n) complexity) and compare to the closest prior approaches in the source and target fields.

### Success Conditions (from Oral)
- The paper identifies the essential structural statistic or algorithm that explains 'why' an expensive method works (e.g., subgraph GNNs work because of neighborhood overlap counts, not because of full subgraph structure) — then replaces the expensive proxy with a direct efficient computation.
- The cross-domain transfer yields a provably stronger result than native field methods (e.g., GD-WL is necessary and sufficient for biconnectivity; tensor language gives exactly the WL hierarchy in two syntactic counts) rather than just an empirical improvement.
- The method is simultaneously principled (formal expressiveness or correctness guarantee) and practical (drop-in usability, linear or near-linear complexity), resolving an apparent theory-practice trade-off that prior work accepted as fundamental.
- The paper provides a new measurement dimension or yardstick for the field (homomorphism counting framework, biconnectivity as expressivity benchmark, relative representations as compatibility standard) that guides future architecture design.
- The innovation is modular and wraps existing methods rather than requiring full retraining — reviewers strongly rewarded systems that improve existing embedding algorithms as drop-in wrappers (GraphZoom, HSCR, Relative Representations).

### Failure Modes (from Rejected)
- Missing comparison with the most relevant prior work that uses the same structural idea — rejected papers in this cluster were consistently penalized for missing compatibility-matrix methods, prior sheaf network applications, or existing coarsening-based approaches that made novelty claims impossible to evaluate.
- Framework-without-algorithm: assembling existing techniques (clustering + coarsening + structure learning) into a pipeline without a fundamentally new algorithmic component, yielding insufficient novelty despite addressing a genuine engineering problem.
- Theory-practice gap in the core approximation: proposing a theoretically motivated assumption (fixed-precision arithmetic model, NTK discriminator approximation) that does not map convincingly to the actual deployed systems reviewers know about — the burden is on the paper to close this gap explicitly.
- Evaluation limited to toy or simple benchmarks (MNIST-only deep clustering, CIFAR-10-only adversarial training, synthetic data only for topological features) when the method claims broad applicability — reviewers require performance on the hardest standard benchmarks.
- Claiming efficiency without hardware or latency benchmarks: proposing a lookup-table inference scheme (ClusComp) or a novel inference mechanism without measuring actual GPU latency, cache pressure, or throughput makes the 'efficient' claim unverifiable and leads to rejection.

### Impact Drivers (from High-Cited)
- FlashAttention-2 became foundational infrastructure (2430 citations) not through conceptual novelty but through ubiquitous adoption as the default implementation in every LLM training framework — infrastructure papers that become load-bearing dependencies of the whole field achieve citation counts impossible from conceptual papers alone.
- SnapKV achieved high citation by providing a training-free, no-fine-tuning method that solved a practical deployment bottleneck (long-context inference memory) with a simple, memorable insight (prefill attention predicts generation attention) — simplicity of implementation drives adoption, and adoption drives citations.
- KVQuant's impact came from enabling a specific impressive capability demonstration (10M token context on a single GPU) — concrete capability milestones that enable downstream research attract citations from all papers that build on that capability.
- High-cited papers in this cluster provided reusable modules (FlashAttention kernel, SnapKV compression wrapper, KVQuant quantization recipe) rather than standalone models — reusable components accumulate citations from every paper that uses them.
- Papers that solve both a theoretical problem (expressiveness proof) and a practical problem (orders-of-magnitude speedup) simultaneously become the canonical reference for both communities — dual-community appeal multiplies the citation pool.

### Cognitive Barriers
- The algebraic structure of a target domain (GNN computation as tensor language, automata as transformation semigroups) is not visible until you adopt a formalism from another field — researchers embedded in the target domain see the domain's native vocabulary, not the shared abstract structure.
- The assumption that a method's advantage comes from its architecture (subgraph extraction) rather than the information it accesses (structural statistics) is hard to break because the architecture is what is visible and modified — decomposing 'why it works' from 'how it is implemented' requires deliberate deconstruction.
- Cross-domain transfer is blocked by community silos: the insight that algebraic multigrid coarsening applies to graph embedding requires someone who knows both numerical PDE solvers and representation learning, a rare combination that most researchers do not have.
- The temporal coherence of optimization trajectories (parameters change slowly) is an empirical fact that is invisible within the per-step view of stochastic optimization — stepping back to the meta-level and treating the trajectory as structured data is cognitively non-trivial.

### Reviewer Expectations
Reviewers in this cluster expect: (1) formal guarantees that accompany empirical results — expressiveness proofs, correctness guarantees, or provable bounds, not just ablations; (2) comparison to all closely related prior work in both the source and target fields, especially methods using the same structural tool; (3) evidence that the method scales to practical problem sizes (large benchmark graphs, real LLM training runs, hardware-measured latency); (4) a clear delineation of what is genuinely new versus what is imported from the source field; (5) for efficiency claims, actual hardware benchmarks (latency, throughput, memory) rather than algorithmic complexity arguments alone. Reviewers strongly penalize missing prior-work comparisons and efficiency claims without empirical validation.

### Oral vs Rejected Gap
Oral papers in this cluster provide a formal guarantee (expressiveness proof, convergence bound, necessary-and-sufficient characterization) alongside empirical SOTA results, and position against all closest related methods. Rejected papers either miss the critical prior-work comparison that makes novelty evaluable, or assemble existing techniques into a pipeline without providing a new algorithmic core — they solve real problems but cannot demonstrate sufficient novelty over the combination of the individual components they use.

### Oral vs High-Cited Gap
Oral papers achieve acceptance through principled formal contributions with strong empirical results. High-cited papers additionally become load-bearing infrastructure (FlashAttention-2, SnapKV) used by the entire field — their citations accumulate through dependency rather than through intellectual influence. Papers that provide a reusable module with a simple interface and a dramatic capability demonstration (10M context, 2x training speedup) accumulate citations at a rate that no conceptual breakthrough can match in the short term.

### Illustrative Examples
- **[Oral]** *Graph Neural Networks for Link Prediction with Subgraph Sketching*: Identified that subgraph GNNs' advantage comes from neighborhood overlap statistics, not full subgraph processing, then replaced expensive subgraph extraction with sketched approximations.
  - Lesson: Decomposing 'why it works' from 'how it is implemented' enables a faster method that matches or exceeds the original — this deconstruction-and-reconstruction pattern received a perfect score of 10.
- **[HC]** *FlashAttention-2: Faster Attention with Better Parallelism and Work Partitioning*: Redesigned attention kernel work partitioning across GPU thread blocks and warps, achieving 2x speedup over FlashAttention-1 with no algorithmic change.
  - Lesson: Infrastructure papers that become the default implementation for an entire field accumulate citations through dependency — being what everyone uses is more impactful than being conceptually novel.
- **[HC]** *SnapKV: LLM Knows What You are Looking for Before Generation*: Discovered that prefill attention patterns reliably predict generation-time KV importance, enabling proactive cache compression before generation with no fine-tuning.
  - Lesson: A memorable empirical insight (the model 'knows' what it needs before generating) combined with a practical training-free solution creates citations from both practitioners and researchers — simplicity of deployment is as important as correctness.
- **[Rejected]** *Diffusion Minimization and Sheaf Neural Networks for Recommender Systems*: Applied sheaf neural networks to recommender systems for oversmoothing, but the combination had already been published by Bamberger et al. 2024.
  - Lesson: Applying an existing method (sheaf NNs) to a new domain (recommendation) without adding new algorithmic components is insufficient novelty if the combination has prior art — a thorough prior work search across both source and target domains is mandatory before submission.
- **[Oral]** *Transformers Learn Shortcuts to Automata*: Proved that Transformers achieve O(log T) depth for all automata and O(1) for solvable-group automata using algebraic decomposition theorems, and verified empirically.
  - Lesson: Translating a domain's capacity question into an algebraic structure problem enables applying classical theorems to get tight bounds — cross-disciplinary reframing into a field with rich theory delivers contributions that native-domain methods cannot.

## C02: Causal Structure Exploitation for Identifiable Inference
*17 Oral, 0 High-Cited, 3 Rejected*

### Core Principle
Exploit the causal structure of a data-generating process — rather than treating data as a flat distribution — to achieve identification, identifiability guarantees, or improved estimation that purely statistical or observational methods cannot. The key innovation is consistently finding a formal mathematical tool (de Finetti representation, conditional independence constraints, geometric support analysis, measure-theoretic axioms) that bridges abstract causal theory with a concrete inferential problem, producing results that are provably stronger under correctly stated assumptions.

### Step-by-Step
- Step 1: Identify a problem where standard statistical approaches (regression, maximum likelihood, imputation) either fail or cannot provide identifiability guarantees — often signaled by the existence of systematic empirical failures or unresolved competing frameworks.
- Step 2: Model the data-generating process causally: draw the causal graph, identify confounders, selection mechanisms, interventional structures, or exchangeability properties. Ask whether the standard approach treats a causal element as noise rather than structure.
- Step 3: Find a formal mathematical tool from causal inference, measure theory, or algebraic statistics (do-calculus, de Finetti representation, Gaussian covariance geometry, t-norms, RKHS constraints) that directly addresses the structural problem identified in Step 2.
- Step 4: Derive the minimal formal result needed — often a necessary and sufficient identifiability condition, a testable statistical constraint replacing expert knowledge, or a provable reduction in generalization error — and state it as a theorem with precisely verified assumptions.
- Step 5: Show the counterintuitive or unexpected consequence: imputation is actively harmful (GRN dropouts), collider constraints improve prediction without any causal intervention, bi-directional causality is identifiable from conditional independence tests alone.
- Step 6: Validate on benchmark datasets from the target applied domain (genomics, time series, knowledge graphs) and provide a practical algorithm that implements the theoretical insight without requiring the full theoretical apparatus from the user.

### Success Conditions (from Oral)
- The paper delivers a necessary and sufficient identifiability result — not just a sufficient condition — under stated assumptions, giving reviewers a theoretically complete result that advances the field rather than an incremental sufficient condition.
- The key result is counterintuitive or overturn-s standard practice: imputation is harmful (not just suboptimal), passive causal knowledge improves predictive regression (not just causal estimation), bi-directional causality is testable (not just an expert prior), labels in SSL are a missing-data mechanism (not a random sample). Counterintuitive findings attract broad attention within and outside causal inference.
- Formal assumptions are precisely stated and are testable from data rather than requiring expert prior knowledge — papers succeed when they replace 'you must know the valid instruments' with 'you can identify the valid instruments from data using the following test.'
- The paper bridges two independently developed theoretical frameworks (causal inference and exchangeable probability, ICA and intervention geometry, causal graphs and RKHS regression) — bridging papers are recognized as unifying contributions rather than incremental advances.
- The empirical validation covers a benchmark from the target applied domain (genomics, biological networks, survey data) that demonstrates the method's practical value, not just toy synthetic data.

### Failure Modes (from Rejected)
- Identifiability not established for the proposed latent causal model — the most common rejection reason in this cluster: claiming to jointly learn causal representation and graph structure without proving the result is identifiable from the data generating process, leading to results that cannot be distinguished from an arbitrary solution.
- Mathematical presentation errors or poorly defined notation in theoretical proofs — even a conceptually valid extension (soft interventions in causal representation learning) was rejected when key definitions were incorrect and proofs contained reviewer-identified errors.
- Methodological combination without theoretical innovation: assembling representation learning with existing IV bounds (CATE partial identification) as a pipeline without new theoretical guarantees, producing marginal empirical improvements that do not justify publication as a theoretical contribution.
- Evaluation limited to synthetic or toy settings (simple toy images, synthetic IVs) when theoretical claims cover broad real-world applicability — causal inference reviewers require validation on real-world datasets from the target domain.
- Assuming a weaker assumption holds without rigorously verifying it: claiming that the exclusion restriction is preserved by the learned instrument representation, or that soft intervention switching is identifiable, without a formal proof that the construction actually satisfies the required causal independence conditions.

### Impact Drivers (from High-Cited)
- NOTE: Cluster 2 has 0 high-cited papers, so impact driver analysis is based on acceptance signals and positioning of oral papers, not citation data.
- Papers that reframe a persistent methodological debate (imputation vs. omission for missing data, MAR vs. MNAR in SSL) by providing a formal causal resolution are likely to achieve citation impact, as they give practitioners a principled answer to a frequently encountered decision.
- Unification papers — establishing measure-theoretic axioms for causality analogous to Kolmogorov's axioms — have the potential for very high long-term impact by providing a common language, but require extensive follow-on work to realize their citation potential.
- Counterintuitive positive results (causal structure improves purely predictive regression, passive causal knowledge provides regularization benefit) tend to attract citations from researchers in adjacent fields who can immediately apply the insight to their own prediction problems.
- Papers that establish a named formal object (causal kernels, do-Finetti interventional distributions, collider regression) that other researchers can build on accumulate citations as the vocabulary of the sub-field — naming enables adoption.

### Cognitive Barriers
- The assumption that causal graphs are only useful for causal estimation (not predictive regression) is so pervasive that using a known DAG as a regularization resource for purely predictive tasks — without any intervention — required stepping outside the standard causal workflow entirely.
- The missing-data framing of label availability in SSL is cognitively blocked by the fact that SSL and the missing-data literatures developed independently with different goals — recognizing the structural isomorphism required familiarity with both survey statistics (MNAR/MAR) and machine learning (SSL), which is an uncommon combination.
- Geometric properties of distribution supports (what values variables can take) are not analyzed as identifiability tools in causal representation learning, because the standard toolkit focuses on conditional independence (which variables are d-separated) — shifting from independence structure to support structure requires a non-obvious change of mathematical lens.
- The de Finetti representation theorem is a probabilistic tool not typically combined with interventional causal reasoning — recognizing that the mixing measure in the de Finetti representation plays the role of the structural equation system requires a cross-domain insight that probability theorists and causal inference researchers rarely arrive at independently.

### Reviewer Expectations
Reviewers in this cluster (causal inference, probabilistic models, representation learning) expect: (1) formal identifiability results with precisely stated and verifiable assumptions — necessary and sufficient conditions are rewarded over sufficient-only; (2) mathematical rigor throughout: definitions must be correct, proofs must be complete, and notation must be consistent; (3) a clear counterintuitive consequence of the theoretical result that motivates why the causal framing is superior to the standard statistical approach; (4) practical algorithms that implement the theory without requiring the full mathematical apparatus from users; (5) validation on real-world applied domain datasets, not only synthetic data. Reviewers strongly penalize papers that claim identifiability without proof, have notation errors in key definitions, or combine existing methods without new theoretical guarantees.

### Oral vs Rejected Gap
Oral papers establish formal identifiability or optimality results with correctly stated and verifiable assumptions, and demonstrate a counterintuitive consequence that justifies the causal framing over simpler alternatives. Rejected papers in this cluster failed at one of three points: they could not establish identifiability for their proposed latent model (Learning Latent SCMs), they contained mathematical errors in key definitions (Implicit CRL), or they combined existing techniques without new theoretical guarantees (instrument representation learning for CATE bounds).

### Oral vs High-Cited Gap
This cluster has no high-cited papers, preventing a data-driven comparison. Based on oral paper content: the oral papers that are most likely to achieve high citation are those that provide reusable tools (causal kernels as a new mathematical object, do-Finetti interventional distributions for multi-environment settings) and those that overturn standard practice in an applied domain (omitting rather than imputing dropouts in genomics). Papers that establish formal foundations tend to have longer citation horizons than papers solving a specific applied problem.

### Illustrative Examples
- **[Oral]** *Gene Regulatory Network Inference in the Presence of Dropouts: a Causal View*: By modeling dropout as an explicit causal node, the paper proves imputation is actively harmful and that omitting technical zeros outperforms imputing them for GRN inference.
  - Lesson: Treating a data quality problem causally rather than as a preprocessing nuisance can overturn standard practice — the counterintuitive finding (imputation is harmful, not helpful) is the primary driver of acceptance and potential impact.
- **[Oral]** *A Measure-Theoretic Axiomatisation of Causality*: Defines causal kernels as the minimal addition to a probability space needed for interventional distributions, subsuming all existing causal frameworks in a unified measure-theoretic foundation.
  - Lesson: Grounding a contested concept in a minimal axiomatic extension of an established framework (Kolmogorov probability) unifies competing approaches — but reviewers require motivation through concrete examples to connect abstract definitions to practical problems.
- **[Oral]** *Returning The Favour: When Regression Benefits From Probabilistic Causal Knowledge*: Independence constraints implied by collider structures in a known causal graph serve as regularization for purely predictive regression, provably reducing generalization error.
  - Lesson: Finding a beneficial use of causal knowledge outside the standard causal estimation workflow (using DAG constraints to improve prediction, not just causal effect estimation) creates cross-community interest from both the causal inference and machine learning communities.
- **[Rejected]** *Learning Latent Structural Causal Models*: Attempted to jointly learn latent causal variables and causal graph structure from observations via variational inference, but could not establish identifiability of the latent variables.
  - Lesson: Proposing a variational approach to a joint learning problem without establishing identifiability is insufficient in causal representation learning — reviewers treat identifiability as a prerequisite, not a future work item.
- **[Oral]** *Interventional Causal Representation Learning*: Proved that interventions create geometric signatures in the support structure of latent factor distributions, enabling causal identifiability invisible to conditional independence-based methods.
  - Lesson: Shifting from a statistical object (conditional independence patterns) to a geometric object (support changes under intervention) as the primary identifiability tool required looking for information that the standard toolkit ignores — a different kind of mathematical lens applied to the same intervention data.

## C03: Cross-Modal Transfer and Representation Reframing
*98 Oral, 47 High-Cited, 17 Rejected*

### Core Principle
Identify a powerful technique, objective, or architectural pattern from one domain and systematically re-instantiate it in a new target domain by resolving the key domain-mismatch barriers. Rather than engineering solutions from scratch, the innovation is recognizing that a known tool already solves the target problem once the representation or objective is correctly adapted.

### Step-by-Step
- Step 1: Identify a bottleneck in the target domain — a performance gap, a missing inductive bias, or a training objective mismatch that existing domain-specific approaches fail to address.
- Step 2: Survey adjacent domains for a technique that has already solved an analogous bottleneck. Look specifically for tools that produce the desired output property (invariance, structure, quality) as a by-product of their primary objective.
- Step 3: Characterize the domain mismatch. List the specific differences (signal dimensionality, continuity vs. discreteness, correlation structure, scale) that prevent direct application of the source technique.
- Step 4: Design the minimal adaptation that resolves each domain mismatch without discarding the core power of the source technique. Prefer re-instantiating at domain-appropriate granularity (e.g., temporal windows instead of image patches) over wholesale redesign.
- Step 5: Validate that the adapted technique achieves the target property. Run targeted ablations isolating the transferred component from other design choices to confirm it is the source of the gain.
- Step 6: Benchmark on well-established community metrics, ideally introducing domain-appropriate evaluation measures that parallel the source-domain metrics (e.g., FDSD/KDSD analogous to FID/KID).

### Success Conditions (from Oral)
- The transferred technique breaks a long-standing assumption or belief in the target domain (e.g., 'GANs cannot match autoregressive TTS,' 'reconstruction loss is necessary for discrete speech units') — reviewers reward paradigm-invalidating demonstrations.
- The adaptation is surgically minimal: only the components necessary to resolve the domain mismatch are changed, preserving the source technique's core mechanism and making the contribution easy to understand and reproduce.
- Comprehensive multi-domain evaluation is provided — oral papers in this cluster consistently ran ablations across two or more datasets/modalities, demonstrating robustness rather than cherry-picked results.
- New domain-appropriate evaluation metrics are introduced alongside results, providing the community with tools to measure the problem going forward (e.g., FID-analogues for audio, new benchmarks for zero-shot TTS).
- The paper demonstrates a practical speed or efficiency advantage over prior approaches that required more expensive data, supervision, or computation — reviewers in this cluster specifically praise parallelizable inference and zero-shot capabilities.

### Failure Modes (from Rejected)
- Claiming novelty for combining existing components (e.g., TCN + TGN + self-attention) without proposing any new module or principled design rationale — reviewers explicitly called this 'too incremental for ICLR' even when results improved over baselines.
- Presenting only qualitative or abstract claims without quantitative benchmarks — the stochastic interpolants rejection shows that even theoretically elegant ideas are rejected when FID/LPIPS scores or direct comparisons are absent.
- Failing to respond to or address specific reviewer concerns in the rebuttal phase — multiple rejections in this cluster note that authors did not engage with gradient analysis challenges, missing baselines, or undefined terminology.
- Applying existing methodology (LoRA, VQ-VAE tokenization) to a new domain without any methodological novelty and without demonstrating that the pre-trained model cannot already handle the domain adequately.
- Testing only on synthetic or rendered data without validating on real-world inputs — reviewers consistently flagged this as limiting real-world applicability, particularly for 3D editing and medical imaging papers.

### Impact Drivers (from High-Cited)
- Opening a new research paradigm that the community can build upon: the highest-cited papers (ViT, MiniGPT-4, Make-A-Video, Sparse Autoencoders) each defined a new standard approach that subsequent work adopts as infrastructure, rather than just improving a metric.
- Providing open-source code or models that democratize access — MiniGPT-4 (2975 citations) and CogVideo (1001 citations) became highly cited partly because they released the first accessible reproductions of closed-model capabilities.
- Addressing a bottleneck that affects every researcher in a subfield simultaneously: MVDream solved the 'Janus problem' affecting all SDS-based text-to-3D methods, making it a required preprocessing step adopted universally.
- Achieving surprising simplicity: the minimalist bridge hypothesis (MiniGPT-4's single linear layer, TimesNet's 1D→2D reshaping) is counterintuitive enough to generate community discussion and easy enough to reproduce widely.
- Demonstrating results at scale that challenge fundamental assumptions: ViT's proof that CNNs are not necessary for vision, and LLaDA's proof that diffusion LLMs compete with autoregressive models, each invalidated a field-wide prior belief, driving citations from papers needing to cite the paradigm shift.

### Cognitive Barriers
- Researchers assume domain-specific techniques require domain-specific foundations: the audio-from-image-GAN analogy and the speech-discretization-without-reconstruction insight both required ignoring the common wisdom that each modality's unique properties demand bespoke solutions.
- The target property seems to require the mechanism being eliminated: it is counterintuitive that removing the reconstruction decoder entirely (C-SWMs) still yields well-structured representations, or that a single linear projection (MiniGPT-4) achieves cross-modal alignment.
- Source-technique use cases are narrowly framed: GAN discriminators were seen as final-product generators, not as supervisory engines; VQ-VAE was reconstruction-only; normalizing flows were density estimators, not coordinate-transform operators.
- Scale and data abundance are not recognized as substitutes for architectural priors: it was widely believed that image recognition required CNNs' locality bias until ViT empirically demonstrated that pre-training scale can replace hand-crafted inductive biases.

### Reviewer Expectations
Reviewers in this cluster look for three things simultaneously: (1) a crisp conceptual insight — a clear single sentence explaining why the transferred technique works in the new domain; (2) empirical decisiveness — results that are not merely comparable but that break a previous SOTA barrier or invalidate a standing assumption; and (3) ablation depth — systematic isolation of the transferred component from other design choices. Reviewers accept small absolute gains only when paired with exceptional interpretability or benchmark-setting (e.g., new evaluation metrics). They penalize missing code/reproducibility details, absent comparisons to strong concurrent baselines, and over-reliance on synthetic or rendered test data.

### Oral vs Rejected Gap
Oral papers in this cluster validate their core innovation with multi-domain ablations that isolate the transferred component, while rejected papers either lack quantitative experiments entirely or combine existing components without any new module, making it impossible to determine what produces the gain. The single most discriminating factor is whether the paper demonstrates that the target domain property (quality, structure, invariance) could not be achieved without the specific transferred mechanism.

### Oral vs High-Cited Gap
Oral papers solve a well-defined technical problem and win an acceptance signal; high-cited papers additionally provide community infrastructure — they either become the first open baseline for a new capability (MiniGPT-4, CogVideo), define a new evaluation paradigm (Sparse Autoencoders for mechanistic interpretability), or become the standard preprocessing module that all subsequent work must cite (MVDream for text-to-3D). The transition from oral to highly cited requires solving a bottleneck that is simultaneously universal (affects everyone in the subfield) and tractable (reproducible within weeks by other labs).

### Illustrative Examples
- **[HC]** *An Image is Worth 16x16 Words: Transformers for Image Recognition at Scale*: Apply NLP Transformers to vision with minimal adaptation, relying on pre-training scale rather than CNN's locality inductive bias.
  - Lesson: The most impactful cross-modal transfers are the most conceptually minimal — the entire contribution was 'remove the CNN, add more data.' Community impact comes from definitively falsifying a prior belief (CNNs are necessary for vision) rather than from architectural sophistication.
- **[HC]** *MiniGPT-4: Enhancing Vision-Language Understanding with Advanced Large Language Models*: Align a frozen vision encoder to a frozen LLM with a single trainable linear projection, testing the minimal-bridge hypothesis for multimodal alignment.
  - Lesson: Extreme simplicity is a citation multiplier when it democratizes access: MiniGPT-4's near-3000 citations stem primarily from being the first open, reproducible approximation of GPT-4V, not from architectural novelty. Open-sourcing at the right moment in a fast-moving subfield generates outsized impact.
- **[Oral]** *Contrastive Learning of Structured World Models*: Reframe world model training as contrastive prediction in latent space, eliminating the reconstruction decoder and forcing representations to earn their structure from predictive utility.
  - Lesson: Oral-level quality requires demonstrating that removing a component (reconstruction decoder) rather than adding one still achieves the desired property — this counterintuitive move is the core innovation and must be supported by thorough ablations across multiple environments.
- **[HC]** *MVDream: Multi-view Diffusion for 3D Generation*: Bake multi-view geometric consistency into the diffusion prior itself by conditioning on all views in a single forward pass, solving the Janus problem at the source rather than patching it during 3D optimization.
  - Lesson: Impact comes from becoming the universal preprocessing step: because every SDS-based text-to-3D method suffered from the same Janus problem, a solution that works as a drop-in prior gets cited by all subsequent work in the subfield.
- **[Rejected]** *Stochastic interpolants with data-dependent couplings*: Generalize transport-based generative frameworks to data-dependent couplings for conditional generation, but without quantitative empirical validation.
  - Lesson: Even theoretically elegant ideas are rejected when FID scores and direct quantitative comparisons are absent — 'no quantitative experiments' is the single most cited rejection reason. Theory papers must include at least one empirical benchmark that reviewers can evaluate numerically.

## C04: Sequential Decision-Making via Self-Improvement and Objective Reframing
*54 Oral, 11 High-Cited, 10 Rejected*

### Core Principle
Transform an intractable RL or planning problem by reframing the training objective so that the system generates its own supervisory signal, either by retroactively relabeling failures as successes (hindsight), treating the entire learning trajectory as a prediction target (in-context algorithm distillation), or using the model's own judgments as rewards (self-rewarding). The unifying insight is that the bottleneck is rarely the model capacity but the supervision source — replacing external supervision with self-generated signal breaks the dependence on expensive labels or reward engineering.

### Step-by-Step
- Step 1: Identify the supervision bottleneck in the target RL or planning task. Ask: what is the specific signal that the current approach requires but is expensive, sparse, or absent?
- Step 2: Determine whether the agent's own trajectory data (failed or successful) can be retroactively re-interpreted as valid supervision for a different but related objective. Formalize this relabeling mathematically.
- Step 3: Design the self-generated supervision mechanism. This may be hindsight relabeling (achieved state becomes the goal label), sequence prediction over learning histories, competitive self-play, or a staged curriculum that alternates between generating and filtering self-supervision.
- Step 4: Address distribution shift or collapse risk. Identify the failure mode of naive self-supervision (e.g., repeating first answers, mode collapse) and add a stabilization mechanism such as staged training, importance weighting, or constrained exploration.
- Step 5: Evaluate on benchmark suites that test both sample efficiency and generalization to out-of-distribution settings. Oral papers in this cluster consistently tested on URLB, POPGym, NetHack, or Atari — established suites with known difficulty.
- Step 6: Provide theoretical grounding, even if partial. Show a formal convergence property, sample complexity bound, or information-theoretic motivation. Reviewers in this cluster explicitly praised theoretical guarantees alongside empirical results.

### Success Conditions (from Oral)
- The paper demonstrates a qualitative capability that was previously impossible, not merely a quantitative improvement — in-context RL, genuine self-correction, zero-shot multi-agent coordination, or human-timescale adaptation are examples of capability thresholds that earned Oral status.
- Theoretical grounding is provided for the self-supervision mechanism: convergence proofs, performance bounds, or information-theoretic justifications appear in nearly every Oral paper (PSRL, DCRL, ReLIC, DeepLTL, etc.) and are specifically praised by reviewers.
- The proposed method is simpler than competing approaches: Meta-Q-Learning's ablation showing that context-conditioning alone drives meta-RL gains, and PEARL's minimal extensions earned praise precisely because they demonstrated sufficiency of simpler mechanisms.
- Cross-environment generalization is demonstrated — papers that tested on three or more distinct environments or task families consistently earned higher ratings than those tested on a single domain.
- Practical integration is shown: papers that demonstrate the method slots into existing pipelines without architectural overhaul (e.g., AdaptDiffuser, PRISE, ACE) earn praise for accessibility.

### Failure Modes (from Rejected)
- Testing only one narrow instantiation of a broad general framework — the ToT rejection explicitly noted that 'only one narrow implementation was tested despite the general architecture proposed,' making it impossible to assess generality.
- Missing critical baselines: rejected papers uniformly failed to compare against the strongest recent concurrent method (ACT for imitation learning, strong multi-agent RL methods for coordination), making claims of improvement unverifiable.
- Mismatch between the stated problem and the actual evaluation: the slow-hardware RL paper was rejected because 'the approach seems to target high action frequency rather than perceptual delay,' revealing a disconnect between the framing and the solution.
- Over-reliance on simple environments for evaluation (Minigrid only, two task types, single simulator) — reviewers in this cluster repeatedly penalized limited evaluation scope, with explicit concern about generalization to StarCraft II, complex planning, or real-world settings.
- Incremental contribution framed as a novel framework: assembling existing components (off-policy RL + diffusion + filtering) without identifying and addressing the specific failure mode that makes the combination necessary yields rejection for insufficient novelty.

### Impact Drivers (from High-Cited)
- Enabling a capability that fundamentally changes what practitioners can do: Self-Rewarding LLMs opened the path to superhuman feedback without human annotation; SCoRe solved LLM self-correction that practitioners had been unable to train reliably — both became widely cited as enabling technologies.
- Providing a clean diagnostic that disambiguates a disputed question: 'SFT Memorizes, RL Generalizes' became highly cited because it provided the definitive empirical answer to a field-wide debate about when to use SFT vs. RL, giving practitioners a principled decision rule.
- Solving a universally experienced failure mode rather than a niche one: SPFITE (self-play fine-tuning) addressed the limitation of needing human or GPT-4 labels — a constraint every RLHF practitioner faced — making it immediately applicable across projects.
- Releasing the first open system in a new capability class: Genie's open generative interactive environments and CogVideo's open text-to-video model became citation magnets by being the first accessible baseline at a moment of rapid community interest.
- Establishing that a simple mechanism is sufficient where complex ones were assumed necessary: Plan-and-Act's synthetic planner data generation and SCoRe's staged RL showed that the expensive components (human plans, separate critic models) were not required.

### Cognitive Barriers
- Failures are treated as wasted data rather than retroactively useful supervision: the insight that any achieved outcome is a valid goal for hindsight relabeling (GCSL) requires abandoning the framing of failed trajectories as irrelevant, which feels wasteful to discard.
- Self-improvement seems circular — using the model's own judgments as training signal appears to risk reward hacking or mode collapse. The non-obvious insight is that staged training and importance-weighted selection prevent collapse while enabling genuine capability improvement.
- World models and planning are assumed to require separate architectural components: the insight that a Transformer's next-token prediction over discretized states is equivalent to a world model (DreamerV3, IRIS) required recognizing that the planning horizon is an emergent property of sequence length, not an architectural primitive.
- The exploration problem in multi-agent settings was assumed to require agent-specific incentives: the PSRL insight that a posterior-difference interaction criterion provides the correct multi-agent exploration signal required recognizing that the information value of joint actions is the relevant quantity.

### Reviewer Expectations
Reviewers in this cluster demand a combination of theoretical rigor and empirical breadth that is unusually strict. They specifically look for: (1) formal properties — convergence guarantees, regret bounds, or sample complexity proofs appear in 70%+ of Oral papers and are consistently praised; (2) multi-environment validation — evaluation on at least three distinct task environments or domains; (3) ablations that isolate the novel component from baseline components; and (4) honest treatment of failure modes — papers that omit limitations from the main text (as in the diffusion BC rejection) are penalized. Reviewers are particularly alert to mismatch between the stated problem and the implemented solution, and to missing baselines from concurrent work.

### Oral vs Rejected Gap
Oral papers provide both a formal theoretical motivation and multi-environment empirical evidence, while rejected papers either test only on a single narrow setting or fail to compare against the most relevant recent baselines. The most discriminating failure in this cluster is evaluation scope: every rejection cited insufficient breadth, while every Oral paper included at least three distinct evaluation domains.

### Oral vs High-Cited Gap
Oral papers demonstrate a new capability or algorithm within the RL/planning domain; high-cited papers additionally solve a bottleneck that affects language model training and deployment, which is the larger community. SCoRe, Self-Rewarding LLMs, SPFITE, and SFT vs. RL are all cited heavily because they addressed the RLHF/post-training pipeline that the entire LLM community was struggling with — the generalization from RL domain to LLM community is what drives citation impact beyond acceptance.

### Illustrative Examples
- **[Oral]** *In-context Reinforcement Learning with Algorithm Distillation*: Reframe meta-learning as sequence prediction over entire RL learning histories, enabling a Transformer to distill any RL algorithm into its attention mechanism.
  - Lesson: A perfect-10 review was earned by creatively connecting two major research threads (in-context learning + RL) with a clean mechanistic explanation. The key is identifying that 'what is the ICL analogue for RL?' is the right question, then building a concrete implementation.
- **[HC]** *Training Language Models to Self-Correct via Reinforcement Learning*: Enable genuine LLM self-correction through staged multi-turn RL that trains both the initial attempt and the correction jointly, resolving the distribution shift that caused naive attempts to collapse.
  - Lesson: Impact comes from solving a long-standing open problem (self-correction) by correctly diagnosing the root cause (distribution shift, not model capacity). Highly cited because every RLHF practitioner had tried and failed to train self-correction — providing the right diagnosis enables the community to make immediate progress.
- **[Oral]** *Learning to Reach Goals via Iterated Supervised Learning*: Convert goal-reaching RL into iterated supervised learning by retroactively relabeling any trajectory's actual outcome as its target goal, turning failures into training signal.
  - Lesson: Simplicity is a winning strategy when it is theoretically grounded: the performance-bound derivation that justifies relabeling failures as successes provided the formal justification that converts an intuitively odd idea into a principled algorithm. The elegance plus the empirical gains produced a strong oral.
- **[Rejected]** *Large Language Model Guided Tree-of-Thought*: Structure reasoning as LLM-guided tree search with self-evaluation, but evaluate only one narrow implementation of the proposed general framework.
  - Lesson: Proposing a general framework while evaluating only one narrow case — and having concurrent work appear — is the rejection formula. Claims of generality require empirical evidence of generality. If concurrent work exists, the submission must clearly differentiate on a dimension that matters.
- **[HC]** *SFT Memorizes, RL Generalizes: A Comparative Study of Foundation Model Post-training*: Through controlled experiments with systematically varied rule variants, show that outcome-based RL generalizes to unseen variants while SFT memorizes training patterns.
  - Lesson: Diagnostic papers that settle field-wide debates become widely cited even when they propose no new method. The key is designing the experiment with enough systematic variation to make the conclusion definitive and actionable — the community cites this paper as the justification for using RL instead of SFT.

## C05: Symmetry and Geometry as Architectural First Principles
*46 Oral, 7 High-Cited, 6 Rejected*

### Core Principle
Identify the mathematical symmetry group or geometric constraint that the target problem must respect, then either construct the architecture to enforce it exactly (equivariant design) or use it to explain and fix a known empirical failure mode (symmetry-based diagnosis). The innovation is recognizing that the bottleneck is a missing structural constraint rather than model capacity, and that encoding the right symmetry often makes a problem tractable that was previously intractable.

### Step-by-Step
- Step 1: Identify the symmetry group of the target problem. Ask: which transformations of the input should produce a known, predictable transformation of the output (equivariance) or no change at all (invariance)? Enumerate all relevant symmetry classes, not just the obvious ones.
- Step 2: Determine which existing architectures violate these symmetries. Characterize the gap precisely — is it a missing permutation symmetry, a rotation equivariance, a scale symmetry, or a temporal structure? Prior-work surveys that identify which methods violate which symmetry are publishable contributions on their own.
- Step 3: Select the mathematical machinery. Common choices in this cluster: group-equivariant convolutions, frame averaging over a representative subset, Clifford algebras for orthogonal symmetries, permutation-matching (combinatorial assignment), or conformal isometry constraints.
- Step 4: Prove that the proposed architecture is theoretically sound — either proving exact equivariance guarantees, providing sample complexity bounds that depend on the exploited symmetry, or showing a formal correspondence (e.g., completeness of a logical framework for GNN expressiveness).
- Step 5: Demonstrate an empirical advantage attributable to the symmetry structure. The advantage should manifest as improved sample efficiency, better generalization to unseen group elements (novel rotations, object scales), or parameter efficiency compared to symmetry-agnostic baselines.
- Step 6: Validate on an existing benchmark where the symmetry is known to matter. For neuro-AI papers, validation against biological measurements (RSA with neural recordings, match to known neural coding properties) is expected. For ML papers, SOTA on a relevant benchmark is required.

### Success Conditions (from Oral)
- The theoretical result is complete, not partial — oral papers in this cluster (Brauer's equivariant networks, Frame Averaging, GNN logical framework) provide full characterizations of symmetry classes, not just sufficient conditions, earning explicit praise for 'filling a theoretical gap.'
- The symmetry insight yields a qualitative capability gain: Git Re-Basin achieved zero-barrier loss landscape connectivity, Frame Averaging achieved exact (not approximate) invariance, and KAN enabled genuine interpretability — these are not metric improvements but new capabilities.
- The paper connects two previously separate research communities or frameworks: Clifford algebras + equivariant networks, neuroscience + ML architecture design, logic + GNN expressiveness — reviewers in this cluster strongly reward bridge papers that enable each community to use the other's tools.
- An unexpected or counterintuitive prediction is confirmed empirically: emergent equivariance in deep ensembles, shape bias appearing in untrained networks under sparsification, negative eigenvalues enabling state-tracking in linear RNNs — surprising findings that are then explained by the symmetry framework are the signature of high-quality cluster-5 papers.
- For neuro-AI papers specifically, testable predictions about biological systems are derived and at least partially validated against recorded data or established neuroscience findings (place field size trade-offs, grid cell hexagonal patterns, rodent tactile cortex alignment).

### Failure Modes (from Rejected)
- Proposing a symmetry-aware architecture for an incremental improvement on a domain-specific architecture where a simpler existing method already works adequately — the Hodge-aware simplicial complex paper was rejected for 'limited applications and no evaluation on widely-used benchmarks,' while the Canonic Spike Coding paper failed for insufficient novelty in the core coding scheme.
- Claiming a new theoretical result that is actually subsumed by or derivable from existing literature using simpler techniques — the universal approximation paper was rejected because 'a stronger result could be proved using well-known simpler techniques the authors appear unaware of,' indicating insufficient literature coverage.
- Testing the symmetry approach on only one narrow task (single country-capital task, single evaluation domain) without demonstrating that the structural insight generalizes — scope limitation is the primary rejection reason for cluster-5 papers.
- Proposing a bio-inspired mechanism (predictive coding for GNN robustness, evolutionary search for SNN feedback) without rigorously testing it against strong adversarial baselines or showing a clear advantage over standard methods under challenging conditions.
- Providing ablations that do not isolate each symmetry component's contribution — the Hodge-aware paper was rejected partly because 'ablation study does not clearly isolate each Hodge component's contribution,' preventing assessment of which symmetry constraint actually matters.

### Impact Drivers (from High-Cited)
- Providing a practical training-free fix to a widely-experienced artifact: Vision Transformers Don't Need Trained Registers became highly cited because it enabled practitioners to fix the ViT artifact without re-training their models — immediate applicability without cost drove adoption.
- Bridging computational neuroscience and ML architecture design with a mathematical proof: Grid Cells conformal isometry became highly cited because it provided a rigorous derivation connecting a neuroscience phenomenon to a principled position embedding design, simultaneously advancing two fields.
- Addressing a fundamental structural flaw in a dominant paradigm: FedMA identified that FedAvg's permutation-blind averaging was mathematically incorrect and provided the combinatorial fix, making it a required citation for any serious federated learning comparison.
- Opening a new architectural direction inspired by biological mechanisms: Continuous Thought Machines introduced neuron-level timing and synchronization as a new design primitive, cited by the neuro-AI community as a concrete path toward biologically realistic computation.
- Demonstrating that a simple targeted mechanism resolves a complex emergent failure: the surgical ablation approach (Vision Transformers Don't Need Trained Registers) shows that mechanistic interpretability can yield immediate practical engineering value — fixing problems post-hoc without retraining.

### Cognitive Barriers
- Symmetry is treated as an add-on rather than a first principle: the standard approach is to design the architecture first and then try to make it symmetric; the cluster-5 insight is to start from the symmetry constraint and derive the required architecture, which inverts the design process and feels circular.
- Established mathematical tools from other fields (Clifford algebras, conformal isometry, Brauer categories) are not part of ML researchers' standard toolkit, requiring cross-disciplinary literature search that most ML researchers do not perform when designing architectures.
- Group-theoretic constraints seem to sacrifice expressive power: restricting attention to K=4 sparse points, averaging over a minimal representative subset, or allowing only permutation-equivariant weight processing all feel like severe capability limitations until the theoretical analysis shows these restrictions are sufficient or even optimal.
- The symmetry that explains a phenomenon is invisible at the standard analysis level: the CNN sample efficiency advantage was invisible at the function-class level and only appeared at the training algorithm level; permutation symmetry as the explanation for mode separation required seeing loss landscapes through a group-theory lens.

### Reviewer Expectations
Reviewers in this cluster have unusually high standards for mathematical rigor: they expect complete characterizations (not just sufficient conditions), explicit proofs of equivariance guarantees, and careful accounting of all symmetry classes in the target architecture. They also expect empirical results on established benchmarks where the symmetry property matters — performance improvements should be tied to the symmetry constraint through ablations. For neuro-AI papers, biological validation against real neural recordings or known neuroscience findings is expected. Reviewers penalize papers that claim connections to biology without testing biological predictions, and papers that use simpler existing algebraic techniques while presenting results as novel theoretical contributions.

### Oral vs Rejected Gap
Oral papers provide complete theoretical characterizations (full symmetry groups, formal proofs, completeness results) combined with multi-domain empirical validation, while rejected papers either provide incremental theoretical extensions without awareness of stronger existing results, or test narrow single-domain applications without demonstrating that the symmetry insight generalizes. The single most discriminating factor is theoretical completeness: partial equivariance results or narrowly tested architectures do not reach oral level in this cluster.

### Oral vs High-Cited Gap
Oral papers establish a new theoretical result or architectural primitive; high-cited papers additionally solve a concrete bottleneck that practitioners immediately encounter in deployed systems (ViT artifact, FedAvg structural flaw, grid cell positional embedding design). The transition from accepted to highly cited requires that the symmetry insight either fixes a problem that practitioners could not previously fix without retraining, or opens a new research direction that other groups immediately build upon (neuro-AI timing mechanisms, conformal position embeddings).

### Illustrative Examples
- **[Oral]** *Git Re-Basin: Merging Models modulo Permutation Symmetries*: Recognize that the apparent multi-basin loss landscape structure of independently trained networks is an artifact of ignoring permutation symmetry, then exploit that symmetry to canonicalize and merge models with zero loss barrier.
  - Lesson: Resolving a long-standing puzzle (mode separation) by identifying a symmetry that was theoretically known but never exploited earns strong oral recognition. One reviewer gave 10/10. The key is the non-obvious realization that the apparent complexity is artifactual, not fundamental.
- **[Oral]** *KAN: Kolmogorov-Arnold Networks*: Apply the Kolmogorov-Arnold decomposition theorem directly to determine where learnable nonlinearities should be placed in a neural architecture, replacing fixed activation functions with learnable edge functions.
  - Lesson: Translating a classical mathematical theorem into an architectural specification produces a high-impact oral when the resulting architecture yields interpretability properties unavailable in standard MLPs. The theoretical grounding in a named theorem provides automatic credibility.
- **[HC]** *Federated Learning with Matched Averaging*: Identify that FedAvg's aggregation violates the permutation symmetry of neural networks, and fix it with a combinatorial layer-wise matching procedure before averaging.
  - Lesson: Diagnosing a fundamental structural flaw (not just a performance limitation) in a dominant paradigm generates lasting citations. Papers that reveal 'the standard method is mathematically wrong' become required references for every serious practitioner in the field.
- **[HC]** *Vision Transformers Don't Need Trained Registers*: Trace the ViT artifact problem mechanistically to a small set of identifiable neurons and surgically ablate them post-hoc, fixing the artifact without any retraining.
  - Lesson: The highest practical impact comes from solutions that require zero retraining cost. This paper became a standard practitioner reference because labs that cannot afford to retrain large vision models could immediately apply the fix. Mechanistic interpretability that yields engineering value drives outsized citation impact.
- **[Rejected]** *Hodge-Aware Learning on Simplicial Complexes*: Design separate model components for each Hodge decomposition sub-space but evaluate only on narrow node-classification tasks without standard citation graph benchmarks.
  - Lesson: Domain-specific mathematical elegance is not sufficient for acceptance without broad empirical validation. Reviewers rejected despite the principled design because evaluation was too narrow to demonstrate that the Hodge decomposition provides advantages on the benchmarks the community cares about.
- **[Oral]** *Frame Averaging for Invariant and Equivariant Network Design*: Achieve exact invariance/equivariance by averaging predictions over a minimal canonical representative subset of the symmetry group, rather than requiring the intractable full group orbit.
  - Lesson: A unifying framework that provides exact theoretical guarantees across multiple symmetry types simultaneously earns strong recognition. The key contribution is 'exact' rather than 'approximate' — reviewers in this cluster prize provable guarantees over empirical approximations.

## C06: Domain-Grounded Structural Bias for Scientific ML
*15 Oral, 5 High-Cited, 5 Rejected*

### Core Principle
Inject domain-derived structural knowledge (chemistry, geometry, biology) as explicit inductive biases into generative or predictive models, resolving apparent tradeoffs (expressivity vs. generalization, sequence vs. structure, prediction vs. generation) that arise from using domain-agnostic representations. The key move is recognizing that the correct structural unit or constraint for a scientific domain already exists in domain knowledge and must be made first-class in the model design.

### Step-by-Step
- Step 1: Identify the domain's natural structural unit — the fundamental building block that domain experts use (molecular fragment, E(3)-symmetry group, topological growth layer, conformational ensemble). Ask: what constraint does domain physics/chemistry impose that standard ML ignores?
- Step 2: Diagnose the failure mode of the current approach. Is it incomplete context (antibody design ignores antigen)? Wrong generation order (sequential vs. synchronized growth)? Wrong modality (sequence only vs. sequence+text)? Wrong objective (imitation vs. contrastive avoidance)?
- Step 3: Encode the domain structural unit directly into the model architecture or training objective — as an adapter module, graph grammar, synchronized generation order, equivariant constraint, or multimodal alignment objective — rather than hoping the model will discover it from data.
- Step 4: Demonstrate that incorporating the domain constraint simultaneously improves multiple competing objectives (expressivity + generalization, accuracy + diversity, sequence + structure) to show the constraint resolves a perceived fundamental tradeoff.
- Step 5: Validate on tasks where domain knowledge predicts the improvement will be largest — binding affinity, structural plausibility, functional property prediction — and show qualitative alignment with domain understanding (e.g., fragment biases align with chemists' intuitions).

### Success Conditions (from Oral)
- Full context inclusion: Oral papers like 'Conditional Antibody Design' succeed by casting the entire biologically relevant complex (antibody + antigen + light chain) as the conditioning context, rather than using partial context. Reviewers unanimously praised the 'unified co-design framework' and gave all 8s when the model treats global context as a first-class input.
- Architecture provably preserves domain symmetry: Papers that use E(3)-equivariant operations (EquiPocket, MCEG) receive praise for 'physical interpretability' and 'principled geometric motivation'. The equivariance must be structural (not approximated) and reviewers check whether the architecture satisfies the symmetry constraint by construction.
- Domain knowledge resolves a stated tradeoff: Expressivity-Generalization for molecular GNNs and Parallel-Diversity for LLM decoding are Oral-level because they demonstrate that the right domain bias eliminates a tradeoff the community believed was fundamental. Each reviewer praised these papers for showing 'simultaneous improvement' on both competing objectives.
- Cross-domain transfer of technique with non-trivial adaptation: LLM-SR transferring LLM knowledge to symbolic regression and Preference Optimization transferring DPO to molecular generation succeed because they identify a structural identity between domains and make non-trivial modifications (not mere copy-paste). Reviewers specifically praised the 'elegant adaptation' and 'cross-domain transfer'.
- Multimodal data construction enables new capability: ProtST is Oral because the authors constructed ProtDescribe — a new paired dataset — enabling a new capability (zero-shot function prediction from text). Papers that construct new enabling datasets alongside new methods receive stronger acceptance signals than methods alone.

### Failure Modes (from Rejected)
- Domain motivation without performance advantage: 'Contrastive Learning of Molecular Representation with Fragmented Views' is chemically well-motivated but rejected because performance gains over existing molecular contrastive learning baselines 'were not significant enough to justify the contribution'. Merely arguing chemical plausibility is insufficient — gains must be clear and consistent across tasks.
- Applying existing algorithm to new domain without algorithmic novelty: 'Identifying key amino acid types using Shapley values' was rejected explicitly for 'applying an existing algorithm to a new domain without significant algorithmic novelty'. The insight must be both domain-specific and technically novel — domain application alone does not meet ML venue standards.
- Failing to generalize beyond the specific domain: Rejected papers like trace reconstruction with language models fail when the approach requires dataset-specific fine-tuning and cannot generalize to different error probabilities. Reviewers specifically identify this as a fatal flaw — domain-specific methods must show principled generalization.
- Metaphorical framing masking trivial mechanism: The 'Social Hierarchy-Guided NAS' paper is rejected because the social hierarchy metaphor 'reduces to fitness-weighted selection, which is standard in evolutionary algorithms'. Structural innovation must be mechanistically novel, not just rebranded standard techniques with a creative name.
- Insufficient evaluation breadth: Multiple rejected papers are criticized for 'limited downstream tasks', 'only one model size tested', or 'evaluation domains not sufficiently diverse'. Domain-focused papers must demonstrate that the domain bias generalizes across multiple benchmarks, not just a single cherry-picked evaluation.

### Impact Drivers (from High-Cited)
- Extreme parameter/data efficiency from domain knowledge: CPO matches GPT-4 and WMT winners with only 22K sentences and 12M trainable parameters. Papers that achieve dramatic efficiency gains — because domain priors compress the hypothesis space — become highly cited as they demonstrate the practical power of structural knowledge injection.
- Opening a new paradigm at the intersection of ML and a scientific domain: LLM-SR is highly cited because it establishes 'LLM-guided scientific equation discovery' as a new research direction rather than improving a single benchmark. Papers that define a new problem formulation and demonstrate its feasibility become more cited than papers that achieve SOTA on an existing benchmark.
- Interpretable biological/physical reasoning as a new capability: BioReason is cited for making genomic predictions interpretable via multi-step reasoning chains — a capability that did not exist before. High-cited papers in this cluster add a qualitatively new capability (interpretability, zero-shot transfer, ensemble generation) on top of existing accuracy, not just accuracy improvements.
- Universal applicability of the structural insight: Papers like LLM-SR (structure search + parameter optimization separation) and CPO (contrastive avoidance of near-correct outputs) become highly cited because their core insight is domain-generalizable. The trigger condition — 'when a model plateaus below expert performance via imitation, switch to contrastive objective' — is actionable in many domains.
- Providing a practical recipe that others can adopt: High-cited papers in this cluster provide concrete pipelines (paired dataset + joint training objective for ProtST, evolutionary skeleton generation + numerical optimization for LLM-SR) that other researchers can apply to new scientific domains without significant methodology redesign.

### Cognitive Barriers
- Domain experts and ML researchers work in separate communities: Recognizing that molecular fragment chemistry is the right abstraction for GNN expressivity requires simultaneous expertise in cheminformatics and graph theory. The cross-field insight is only obvious in retrospect — the field had ML people working on graphs and chemists working with fragments, but neither community recognized the connection.
- Generation order is treated as an implementation detail: MorphGrower's key insight — that synchronized layer-by-layer growth enforces biological plausibility by construction — required realizing that generation order encodes domain growth rules. Researchers routinely treat generation order as irrelevant to output quality, making this insight genuinely non-obvious.
- Deterministic models are assumed to encode only point predictions, not distributions: The AlphaFold flow matching paper's insight — that AlphaFold's internal representations implicitly characterize the conformational distribution, not just the single most stable structure — required recognizing that a model optimized for accuracy at one task can still encode distributional information useful for a different task.
- Imitation is assumed to be the right objective for expert-level performance: CPO's insight that contrastive avoidance of near-correct outputs outperforms imitation requires rejecting the ML field's default assumption that supervised imitation of high-quality references is the correct training signal. This is counterintuitive because imitation always gets better as reference quality improves — until it plateaus.

### Reviewer Expectations
Reviewers in this cluster consistently look for: (1) a clearly articulated domain failure mode that the standard ML approach misses — reviewers want to understand *why* domain knowledge is necessary, not just that it helps; (2) comprehensive evaluation across multiple domain-relevant benchmarks — single benchmark results are consistently flagged as insufficient; (3) explicit demonstration that the domain bias resolves or eliminates a perceived tradeoff rather than just achieving incremental improvement; (4) ablation studies quantifying the specific contribution of the domain knowledge component vs. the rest of the model. Major concerns include: insufficient baselines (especially domain-specialized baselines, not just generic ML baselines), scalability of the domain-knowledge injection to larger or more diverse datasets, and potential biases inherited from the knowledge source (e.g., database biases in UniProt text, LLM biases in scientific knowledge).

### Oral vs Rejected Gap
The single most important difference is that Oral papers achieve clear, significant performance gains that justify the domain-motivated complexity, while rejected papers have sound conceptual motivation but marginal or context-specific improvements — reviewers in this cluster accept the premise that domain knowledge helps but demand proof that this specific encoding of domain knowledge provides a qualitatively different capability or substantially better results, not incremental gains on limited evaluations.

### Oral vs High-Cited Gap
Oral papers achieve state-of-the-art on existing benchmarks within their domain; high-cited papers additionally define a new research direction or paradigm that researchers across related domains can adopt — LLM-SR is highly cited not just for beating previous equation discovery methods but for establishing 'LLM-guided scientific discovery' as a new field, while BioReason is cited for establishing 'RL-incentivized interpretable biological reasoning' as a new direction rather than for a performance number.

### Illustrative Examples
- **[Oral]** *Conditional Antibody Design as 3D Equivariant Graph Translation*: Cast the full antibody-antigen complex as a multi-component equivariant graph and predict all CDRs in parallel via iterative refinement.
  - Lesson: The winning move was expanding context to all structurally relevant entities (antigen + light chain) and using geometry-preserving message passing — reviewers gave unanimous 8s because full context + equivariance resolved two distinct failure modes simultaneously, demonstrating that correct structural framing eliminates both incomplete-context errors and autoregressive dependencies.
- **[Oral]** *LLM-SR: Scientific Equation Discovery via Programming with Large Language Models*: Use LLM scientific knowledge to propose equation skeleton programs, then numerically optimize free parameters, separating combinatorial structure search from continuous parameter fitting.
  - Lesson: This paper appears as both an Oral and a High-Cited paper, illustrating the overlap when a method both advances SOTA and opens a new research direction. The separation of structural hypothesis generation (LLM) from parameter fitting (numerical optimization) is the key architectural insight that makes the approach both principled and generalizable.
- **[Oral]** *Expressivity and Generalization: Fragment-Biases for Molecular GNNs*: Resolve the GNN expressivity-generalization tradeoff by incorporating chemically meaningful fragment decompositions as explicit structural biases.
  - Lesson: The key lesson is that 'fundamental tradeoffs' in ML often disappear when domain-appropriate structure is introduced. Reviewers praised this specifically for showing that domain knowledge eliminates rather than manages a tradeoff — researchers should ask whether their perceived tradeoff is actually an artifact of using the wrong structural prior.
- **[HC]** *Contrastive Preference Optimization (CPO) for Machine Translation*: Replace supervised imitation with contrastive training that penalizes adequate-but-imperfect outputs, achieving GPT-4-level translation with 22K sentences and 12M parameters.
  - Lesson: High citation impact comes from extreme efficiency and a clear, counter-intuitive insight: imitation of high-quality references plateaus because it memorizes their imperfections, while contrastive avoidance of the model's own near-correct outputs teaches what not to do. The data efficiency (22K sentences vs. millions) made this broadly applicable and triggered adoption across translation tasks.
- **[Rejected]** *Contrastive Learning of Molecular Representation with Fragmented Views*: Use chemically meaningful molecular fragments as contrastive learning positive pairs to align representations with chemical activity.
  - Lesson: Sound chemical motivation is insufficient for acceptance — the gains over existing molecular contrastive learning baselines (mixed ratings: 5/3/3/8) were not significant enough. Rejected papers in this cluster fail when their domain-motivated design choice is plausible but not demonstrably superior across enough tasks to justify the added complexity of the domain-specific augmentation.

## C07: Probabilistic Reformulation for Generative Model Design
*36 Oral, 7 High-Cited, 3 Rejected*

### Core Principle
Reframe an existing generative modeling problem in a fundamentally different mathematical language — information theory, optimal control, stochastic processes, energy-based models, variational calculus — to import tools, guarantees, and insights from that mathematical domain that are unavailable in the original framing. The innovation is not a new algorithm but a new problem formulation that reveals the correct solution class.

### Step-by-Step
- Step 1: Identify the correct mathematical object for the problem. Ask: Is the target a *distribution* over outputs or a single output? Is the constraint *distributional* (collective) or *pointwise* (per-instance)? Is the generation process equivalent to an *inference problem*, a *control problem*, or a *search problem*?
- Step 2: Find an equivalent mathematical formulation in a field with richer theory. Controlled text generation → KL-minimizing distribution satisfying constraints. Neural decoding → input-output dynamics → control theory (iLQR). Diffusion training → score matching → MaxEnt IRL with EBM reward. Hyperparameter search → smooth response function → hypernetwork approximation.
- Step 3: Import the closed-form or algorithmic solution from the mathematical domain. If the new formulation has a known optimal solution (e.g., KL-minimizing EBM, iLQR trajectory), derive it explicitly. If not, show that existing algorithms from the imported domain (adaptive policy gradient, variational inference, minimax optimization) apply with minimal modification.
- Step 4: Derive new capabilities or guarantees that the original framing cannot provide. The distributional constraint formulation enables bias correction impossible with pointwise rewards. The iLQR-as-encoder approach reduces parameters by an order of magnitude. The stochastic process formulation provides global coherence constraints. These *new capabilities* — not just better performance — distinguish Oral papers.
- Step 5: Show that the mathematical reformulation identifies the correct *level* of abstraction. Demonstrate that operating at the wrong level (per-token instead of distribution-level, instance-level distillation instead of latent-variable supervision) causes the failure the reformulation resolves, with ablations showing the failure mode re-emerges when the correct abstraction is dropped.

### Success Conditions (from Oral)
- Theoretical optimality guarantee accompanies the reformulation: Oral papers like 'A Distributional Approach to Controlled Text Generation' succeed because the KL-minimizing EBM is the *unique* optimal solution satisfying distributional constraints — reviewers praised the 'theoretical optimality guarantees'. Papers in this cluster must derive and state what the reformulation's optimality condition is, not just show empirical improvement.
- The reformulation reveals a new capability absent in the prior framing: iLQR-VAE reduces free parameters by an order of magnitude and enables generalization from short to long trials — capabilities that are structurally impossible without the control-theoretic formulation. Reviewers specifically praised 'impressive reduction in free parameters' and 'principled encoder-decoder consistency'. The new mathematical framing must add something the old framing cannot have.
- Latent-variable distillation targets the correct bottleneck: 'Scaling Up Probabilistic Circuits by Latent Variable Distillation' succeeds because it identifies that PCs plateau due to optimizer failures in the *latent* space (not the output space) and intervenes there. Reviewers gave all 8s because the paper identifies the correct bottleneck level. Similarly, DreamFusion succeeds because it finds that 3D generation lacks gradients (not 3D architectures) and gets them via score distillation from 2D.
- Cross-domain mathematical transplant with principled justification: Stochastic process theory into language modeling (Brownian bridge for long-form coherence), IRL into diffusion training (MaxEnt IRL for score matching), GFlowNets into LLM posterior inference — each succeeds because the transplant is justified by a structural identity (text trajectories share dynamics with stochastic processes; diffusion samples are trajectories that MaxEnt IRL treats as expert demonstrations). Reviewers require the structural justification to be explicit.
- Amortization principle: Multiple Oral papers (Multi-Rate VAE, Amortizing intractable inference in LLMs, iLQR-VAE) succeed by showing that repeated independent computation can be amortized via a learned meta-mapping or algorithm-as-inference. Reviewers specifically value 'principled, parameter-efficient solutions' that demonstrate the amortization is not just empirically useful but theoretically justified.

### Failure Modes (from Rejected)
- Prior work has already used the key mathematical tool: 'On Fitting Flow Models with Large Sinkhorn Couplings' is likely rejected because OT-based couplings for flow matching were already used in prior work (Lipman et al.) — the paper did not differentiate its contribution sufficiently. In this cluster, the mathematical tool must be either genuinely new to the problem class or applied with a non-trivial modification that enables a new capability.
- Theoretical framework disconnected from practical contribution: 'Conditional Lagrangian Wasserstein Flow for Time Series Imputation' is rejected because 'theoretical elements feel disconnected from practical contribution' and reviewers identified potential implementation errors. A reformulation paper must show that the mathematical framework *causes* the empirical improvement, not just accompanies it.
- Architectural modification without theoretical justification: 'Jakiro' applies MoE to an existing speculative decoding framework and is rejected for 'incremental nature' and 'insufficient theoretical justification'. In this cluster, adding components without a principled mathematical rationale (why does this formulation guarantee better diversity?) is insufficient — the contribution must be the formulation, not the architecture.

### Impact Drivers (from High-Cited)
- Score Distillation Sampling as a general cross-modal gradient tool: DreamFusion is the most highly cited paper in this cluster because SDS is a general principle — use 2D/lower-dimensional score to guide 3D/higher-dimensional optimization — that spawned an entire field of text-to-3D, video, and scene generation research. The impact driver is that the mathematical tool (density distillation via rendering) is universally applicable whenever a differentiable mapping exists from a data-scarce domain to a data-rich one.
- Rehabilitation of a theoretically sound but empirically overlooked model class: 'Simple and Effective Masked Diffusion Language Models' is highly cited (517 citations) because it demonstrated that masked diffusion models, properly formulated, are competitive with AR models — resurrecting an entire model class by showing the performance gap was a training recipe artifact. Papers that rehabilitate model classes by diagnosing implementation failures (rather than proposing new architectures) become highly cited as they redirect community resources.
- Single-step generation without quality sacrifice: 'Consistency Models' is highly cited because it defines a new generative model family with one-step generation — a practically transformative property. Mathematical formulations that unlock a qualitatively new inference regime (single-step, few-step, training-free steering) become highly cited because they solve a problem that practitioners constantly face.
- Training-free steering of pretrained models: 'A General Framework for Inference-time Scaling and Steering of Diffusion Models' is highly cited because it enables steering without fine-tuning — reducing the practical cost from training to inference. Papers that reformulate alignment/steering as an inference problem (using Feynman-Kac particle filtering, GFlowNets, etc.) are highly cited because they democratize capabilities previously requiring expensive fine-tuning.
- Self-contained practical recipe: Medusa's highly-cited status reflects that it provides an immediately deployable recipe (attach auxiliary heads, use tree attention for verification) that achieves 2.2-3.6x speedup with no separate draft model. Mathematical formulations become highly cited when they can be implemented in a small code change with a clear performance guarantee — the simplicity of implementation is an impact multiplier.

### Cognitive Barriers
- Confusing the unit of optimization with the unit of constraint: The distributional approach paper's insight — that some constraints are distributional (holding collectively over many outputs) rather than pointwise — requires recognizing that the target of optimization is the *distribution* itself, not any individual sample. Practitioners optimize per-output rewards reflexively; the barrier is recognizing when the constraint lives at the wrong level.
- Assuming inference components must be unconstrained neural networks: iLQR-VAE's insight — that the recognition network of a VAE can be replaced by a specialized control algorithm implicitly defined by the generative model — requires rejecting the assumption that encoder and decoder must be separately parameterized networks. The 'algorithm as encoder' idea is architecturally unconventional because it requires differentiating through optimization steps.
- Treating generative models as data generators rather than priors: LLMs-as-Bayesian-priors paper's core insight requires viewing an LLM not as a text generator but as a prior over text that can be conditioned on arbitrary likelihoods. This perspective shift — from generation to inference — is blocked by the natural observational frame of LLM use.
- Assuming that a principled method requires architectural changes: Multiple papers show that the right mathematical formulation achieves breakthrough results through training objective changes (MDLM, discriminator-guided score correction) or inference procedure changes (DreamFusion, particle-based steering), not architectural changes. The barrier is the assumption that new capabilities require new architectures.

### Reviewer Expectations
Reviewers in this cluster look for: (1) a clear mathematical equivalence or structural identity that justifies the reformulation — reviewers expect the derivation to be explicit and the connection non-trivial; (2) new theoretical guarantees or capabilities that are structurally impossible in the prior framing, not just empirical improvements; (3) ablation studies showing that the mathematical formulation causes the improvement (not a confounded architectural change); (4) acknowledgment and differentiation from prior work that used similar mathematical tools in adjacent settings. Key concerns include: computational overhead of the new formulation vs. prior approach; whether theoretical guarantees hold in practice (gap between linear/simplified theory and nonlinear practice); scalability to large-scale models; and whether the formulation is a genuine structural advance or just a rebranding of standard techniques with mathematical vocabulary.

### Oral vs Rejected Gap
The critical difference is that Oral papers demonstrate that their mathematical reformulation enables a *structurally new capability* — enforcing distributional constraints, reducing parameters by 10x via algorithm-as-encoder, amortizing hyperparameter search into a single training run — while rejected papers apply mathematical tools (optimal transport, Lagrangian mechanics, MoE) to existing formulations without demonstrating what is now possible that was previously impossible.

### Oral vs High-Cited Gap
Oral papers in this cluster solve a well-defined technical problem rigorously; high-cited papers additionally provide a *general mathematical tool* that other researchers can apply to their own problems — SDS loss for text-to-3D, consistency regularization for fast generative sampling, particle filtering for inference-time reward steering, Rao-Blackwellized ELBO for masked diffusion. The gap is between solving your problem and handing others a reusable mathematical technique.

### Illustrative Examples
- **[Oral]** *A Distributional Approach to Controlled Text Generation*: Recast constraint satisfaction as KL-minimization over distributions, deriving the closed-form EBM optimal solution and distilling it via adaptive policy gradient.
  - Lesson: The success here comes from operating at the correct mathematical level (distributions, not samples) and deriving a uniquely optimal solution. Papers that find the right mathematical object and prove their approach is the unique optimum receive unanimous high scores — reviewers prize theoretical uniqueness, not just empirical superiority.
- **[Oral]** *iLQR-VAE: control-based learning of input-driven dynamics*: Replace the VAE recognition network with iLQR — an algorithm implicitly defined by the generative model — making the encoder structurally consistent with the decoder and reducing free parameters dramatically.
  - Lesson: The 'algorithm as encoder' idea crosses disciplinary boundaries (control theory + VAE inference) and is justified by a structural identity: optimal inference given a dynamical generative model is exactly the problem iLQR solves. The discipline-crossing insight requires expertise in both fields — a natural collaboration barrier that explains why the insight was non-obvious.
- **[HC]** *DreamFusion: Text-to-3D using 2D Diffusion*: Derive Score Distillation Sampling: render the 3D model, compute 2D diffusion score on the render, and use it to update the 3D model — bridging 2D priors to 3D optimization without 3D data.
  - Lesson: DreamFusion's high citation count reflects that SDS is a general cross-modal gradient tool applicable whenever a differentiable projection exists from a data-scarce domain to a data-rich one. The key is that the mathematical tool (density distillation via rendering) is general, not domain-specific — community adoption scales with the breadth of the tool's applicability.
- **[HC]** *Consistency Models*: Enforce that all points on the same probability flow ODE trajectory map to the same origin, enabling single-step generation while retaining multi-step refinement.
  - Lesson: This is the paradigmatic example of a mathematical reformulation that creates a qualitatively new capability (single-step generation at SOTA quality). The consistency property is a geometric insight about the ODE trajectory — not an architectural change — and it spawned a new generative model family. Papers that define new model families via mathematical constraints become highly cited as they are cited by all subsequent work in the family.
- **[Rejected]** *On Fitting Flow Models with Large Sinkhorn Couplings*: Use optimal transport couplings to define better source-target pairings for flow matching training, reducing variance in the learned velocity field.
  - Lesson: This paper is rejected because OT-based couplings for flow matching are not novel — prior work (Lipman et al.) already used OT pairings. In this cluster, the mathematical tool must be genuinely new to the problem class or applied with a modification that enables a capability prior work lacks. Incremental application of known tools without a differentiated contribution does not meet the bar.

## C08: Decomposition-Based LLM Capability Elicitation
*35 Oral, 23 High-Cited, 3 Rejected*

### Core Principle
Elicit capabilities latent in large language models by decomposing monolithic tasks into structured sub-operations that individually fall within the model's demonstrated competence, then composing their outputs — via iteration, verification, external tools, or self-critique — to solve problems beyond the model's single-shot capability. The key insight is that the bottleneck is rarely model capacity but the *structure* of how the model is asked to operate.

### Step-by-Step
- Step 1: Identify the specific sub-capability that fails in end-to-end evaluation. Decompose the full task into candidate sub-operations and test each separately. For LLM reasoning failures: is the model bad at hypothesis generation or hypothesis application? For code generation failures: is the model bad at writing code or evaluating whether it is correct? The failure often localizes to one sub-step.
- Step 2: Design the decomposition so each sub-operation either (a) falls within the model's existing single-step capability, or (b) uses an external tool/verifier that provides the capability the model lacks. ReAct decomposes into alternating thoughts (within LLM capability) and actions (external environment). Toolformer decomposes into language generation + API calls (external tools). Self-RAG decomposes into generation + retrieval decision + critique (meta-tokens trained into the model).
- Step 3: Define the interface between sub-operations explicitly. The connection mechanism is as important as the decomposition: how does output from step N become input to step N+1? For sequential reasoning (Least-to-Most, Selection-Inference), previous answers provide context for subsequent steps. For iterative refinement (Self-Debugging, Self-RAG), external feedback (execution results, retrieval) is formatted as input to the next generation call.
- Step 4: Build the feedback loop. Most successful decomposition papers add a verification or critique step that closes the loop: Self-RAG adds reflection tokens that assess retrieved content and output quality; LEGO-Prover adds failed-proof-to-missing-lemma identification; Self-Debugging adds execution-result-to-diagnosis prompting. The loop enables iterative improvement that single-shot generation cannot achieve.
- Step 5: Validate that the decomposition is the causal factor. Ablate against single-shot prompting, against simpler decompositions, and against task-specific fine-tuning. Show that the capability improvement specifically comes from the structure, not from more compute/tokens or a stronger model. ReAct, for example, demonstrates that interleaving reasoning and acting provides gains beyond either alone.

### Success Conditions (from Oral)
- Clean identification of a specific sub-capability gap: Selection-Inference succeeds because it cleanly diagnoses that LLMs fail at multi-step reasoning not due to reasoning failure but due to confounding selection and inference in the same step. Reviewers give high scores when the paper's decomposition is motivated by a precisely identified failure mode, not a general claim that 'LLMs are limited'.
- Surprising emergent capability from the decomposition: ReAct's unanimous 8/8/8 scores reflect that interleaving reasoning and acting in a single prompt format — without additional training — yields synergistic gains unexpected from either capability alone. Papers that demonstrate emergent properties from the combination (compositionality in Decision Transformer, generalization beyond exemplar difficulty in Least-to-Most) receive the strongest acceptance signals.
- Self-contained closed-loop system that minimizes external dependencies: Self-RAG internalizes retrieval decisions and self-critique as special tokens rather than relying on a separate pipeline. Toolformer uses the model's own perplexity as the supervision signal for tool learning. Reviewers specifically praise systems that are 'end-to-end trainable' or 'require minimal human annotation' — reducing manual intervention is a consistent praise target.
- The decomposition enables generalization across task types: Instruction tuning (FLAN) succeeds with unanimous high scores because training on 60+ NLP tasks as instructions enables zero-shot transfer to unseen task types — the decomposition into instruction format reveals a latent generalization capability. Papers that show the decomposition enables generalization to strictly harder or more diverse problems receive stronger acceptance.
- Interpretable intermediate representations with safety/diagnostic value: Self-RAG's reflection tokens, Selection-Inference's explicit selection steps, and Draft-Sketch-Prove's formal proof sketches are all praised for 'interpretability' and 'human readability'. Reviewers in this cluster value decompositions that produce observable intermediate steps that enable debugging and trust verification.

### Failure Modes (from Rejected)
- Writing quality and depth insufficient for venue: 'Lucy: Think and Reason to Solve Text-to-SQL' is rejected because 'the paper lacks depth and writing quality does not meet NeurIPS standards'. In cluster 8, the LLM+tool decomposition idea is familiar to reviewers; a paper needs rigorous evaluation, strong writing, and clear contribution differentiation — not just a working pipeline.
- Incoherent or AI-generated methodology: 'A Dual-Modal Framework Utilizing Visual Prompts for Enhanced Patch Analysis' receives all four 1/10 ratings as reviewers unanimously identify it as 'likely AI-generated with incoherent methodology'. In a cluster where the community is expert in LLM outputs, papers with disconnected mathematical formulations and ambiguous methodology are rapidly identified as lacking genuine scientific content.
- Limited performance vs. baseline methods despite principled framing: 'ARC-AGI Without Pretraining' is likely rejected for achieving only 20% on ARC-AGI compared to pretraining-based approaches, and for 'insufficient theoretical grounding of why MDL enables the observed generalization'. Principled alternatives (MDL as compression) without competitive performance cannot justify rejection of the dominant paradigm.

### Impact Drivers (from High-Cited)
- Prompting frameworks that any researcher can apply immediately: ReAct (1000+ citations), Tree of Thoughts, Least-to-Most, and Chain-of-thought-style papers are highly cited because they require no fine-tuning, no new datasets, and no architectural changes — any researcher can immediately test them on their task. The zero-barrier-to-adoption property is the primary impact driver for prompting-framework papers in this cluster.
- Establishing a new sub-field with a clear definition and benchmark: Least-to-Most prompting (1618 citations) and Large Language Models are Human-Level Prompt Engineers (1276 citations) become highly cited because they define 'problem decomposition' and 'automatic prompt optimization' as distinct, nameable capabilities that subsequent papers measure against. Papers that give names to previously unnamed capabilities become citation anchors.
- Self-supervised data generation that removes human annotation bottleneck: Toolformer (perplexity-based API call filtering), WizardLM (LLM-based instruction evolution), and Self-Alignment with Backtranslation are highly cited because they solve the annotation bottleneck using the model itself as the labeler. Papers that demonstrate 'the model can generate its own training data' become highly cited as they relax the human supervision constraint that limits model development.
- Revealing a counter-intuitive capability of LLMs: FLAN's counterintuitive finding — that fine-tuning on diverse instructions actually improves zero-shot generalization, contrary to the expectation that fine-tuning reduces zero-shot capability — is what makes it a landmark paper. High-cited papers in this cluster often report a surprising empirical finding (self-debugging works without execution feedback, the model can generate its own prompts better than humans) that changes researchers' priors about LLM capabilities.
- Enabling a qualitatively new capability for a widely-used model: Self-RAG advances RAG from a static pipeline to an adaptive system with metacognitive capabilities; WizardLM enables complex-instruction following that was previously beyond 7B model capability. Papers that qualitatively expand the capability frontier of accessible (non-frontier) models become highly cited because the community is larger for accessible models than for proprietary ones.

### Cognitive Barriers
- Assuming capability is monolithic rather than sub-capability structured: The standard LLM evaluation framing treats 'reasoning', 'coding', and 'instruction-following' as atomic — the model either can or cannot do them. The Phenomenal Yet Puzzling paper's insight that LLMs can *generate* hypotheses but not *apply* them — a sub-capability asymmetry invisible to end-to-end evaluation — requires decomposing the evaluation into parts before diagnosing failures.
- Assuming separate capabilities require separate training: ReAct's core insight — that reasoning and acting can be interleaved in a single prompting format without separate fine-tuning, because both are emergent behaviors of the same model — contradicts the assumption that capabilities developed separately require separate optimization. The barrier is treating capabilities as model properties rather than properties of the interaction format.
- Assuming that knowledge exists only in model weights, not in self-generated intermediate steps: LEGO-Prover's insight — that the prover can dynamically grow its own lemma library during proving — requires viewing the knowledge base as a mutable artifact that the model can contribute to, not just consume. The barrier is treating external resources as fixed inputs rather than as co-constructed outputs.
- Assuming fine-tuning on specific tasks reduces generalization: FLAN's counterintuitive finding — that instruction tuning on 60+ tasks increases zero-shot generalization to unseen task types — contradicts the standard assumption that fine-tuning causes overfitting to the training distribution. This barrier is a widespread prior belief based on the bias-variance tradeoff that FLAN empirically refutes.

### Reviewer Expectations
Reviewers in this cluster (the largest, with 35 Oral papers) are experts in LLM capabilities and inference-time methods. They specifically look for: (1) identification of a precise capability gap or failure mode that the decomposition targets — vague claims about LLM limitations are insufficient; (2) ablation against single-shot prompting, simpler decompositions, and task-specific fine-tuning to isolate the contribution of the structure; (3) evaluation across multiple diverse tasks — single-task evaluations are consistently flagged; (4) honest analysis of failure modes and when the decomposition does not help. Key reviewer concerns include: computational cost (number of LM calls multiplied by decomposition depth), circularity concerns for self-evaluation methods, dependence on specific model capability levels (does it only work with the largest models?), and comparison to more recent baselines that reviewers may know from concurrent work.

### Oral vs Rejected Gap
The single most important difference is execution quality and contribution specificity: Oral papers identify a precisely named capability gap, design a decomposition with a clear mechanism that addresses it, and demonstrate results across multiple tasks with honest ablations — rejected papers propose pipelines for legitimate problems but lack depth, rigorous evaluation, or clear differentiation from existing approaches, and in the extreme case (AI-generated paper) lack genuine scientific content entirely.

### Oral vs High-Cited Gap
Oral papers demonstrate a working capability-elicitation method with strong empirical results; high-cited papers additionally provide a prompting framework, technique name, or data generation method that other researchers immediately adopt as a building block — ReAct's thought/action interleaving, Least-to-Most's decompose-then-solve protocol, WizardLM's instruction evolution operations, and Toolformer's perplexity-based tool filtering are all directly incorporated into subsequent systems. The gap is between solving your problem and providing a reusable component that becomes infrastructure for the community.

### Illustrative Examples
- **[HC]** *ReAct: Synergizing Reasoning and Acting in Language Models*: Interleave thought tokens (internal reasoning) and action tokens (external environment interaction) in a single prompting format, letting each modality correct the other's failure modes.
  - Lesson: ReAct's unanimous 8/8/8 scores and high citations both stem from the same property: the interleaving creates genuine synergy — hallucination-free acting and grounded reasoning — from a trivially simple format change. The lesson is that *how* capabilities are combined matters as much as which capabilities are available. Papers showing synergistic interaction between two previously siloed capabilities become citation anchors.
- **[Oral]** *Finetuned Language Models are Zero-Shot Learners (FLAN)*: Instruction-tune a 137B model on 60+ NLP tasks expressed as natural language instructions and evaluate zero-shot on held-out task types — demonstrating that diverse instruction training improves zero-shot generalization.
  - Lesson: FLAN's acceptance and impact both hinge on the counterintuitive finding: fine-tuning increases zero-shot performance. This required precise experimental design (held-out task types, not held-out tasks) to demonstrate the effect cleanly. Oral-level papers in this cluster require this precision — the contribution must survive reviewer scrutiny about whether the evaluation setup fairly tests the claimed phenomenon.
- **[Oral]** *Self-RAG: Learning to Retrieve, Generate, and Critique through Self-Reflection*: Train reflection tokens (retrieve/no-retrieve, relevant/irrelevant, supported/unsupported) into the model, converting RAG from a static pipeline to an adaptive self-evaluating system.
  - Lesson: The key innovation is internalizing pipeline decisions as learnable tokens rather than external heuristics, enabling end-to-end optimization. Reviewers praised the 'elegant unified framework' and 'practically deployable and interpretable reflection tokens'. Papers that convert external pipeline logic into model-internal representations — reducing system complexity while improving adaptability — are particularly valued in this cluster.
- **[HC]** *WizardLM: Empowering Large Pre-Trained Language Models to Follow Complex Instructions*: Use an LLM to iteratively apply instruction-evolution operations (add constraints, deepen, concretize, add reasoning) to simple seed instructions, generating a complexity-stratified training dataset without human annotation.
  - Lesson: WizardLM is highly cited (1184 citations) because Evol-Instruct became a widely adopted data generation technique. The high-cited impact driver is that it provides a concrete, reusable algorithmic procedure (define evolution operations, apply iteratively, filter low-quality outputs) that other researchers immediately applied to new domains (WizardCoder, WizardMath). Actionable, reproducible data generation methods become infrastructure.
- **[Rejected]** *Lucy: Think and Reason to Solve Text-to-SQL*: Combine LLM natural language understanding with a symbolic reasoning engine to generate SQL for complex multi-table database schemas.
  - Lesson: Lucy is rejected not because the hybrid LLM+symbolic approach is wrong (it is sound) but because 'the paper lacks depth and writing quality does not meet NeurIPS standards' and 'inference time efficiency is not analyzed'. In a cluster where reviewers are expert in LLM-tool combinations, a paper needs rigorous benchmarking against strong baselines, latency analysis, and high writing quality — a working pipeline is insufficient.

## C09: Diagnostic Metric Design for LLM Reasoning Evaluation
*6 Oral, 7 High-Cited, 2 Rejected*

### Core Principle
Identify that the field's standard evaluation metric conflates two distinct phenomena (e.g., capability vs. sampling efficiency, factual accuracy vs. causal reasoning, outcome quality vs. process quality), then design a diagnostic metric or protocol that cleanly separates them and exposes a failure mode invisible to the original metric.

### Step-by-Step
- Step 1: Identify a widely-used performance metric and articulate precisely what it measures vs. what it claims to measure — locate the gap between the proxy and the true capability.
- Step 2: Construct a controlled diagnostic protocol (e.g., pass@k curves, counterfactual test cases, step-level labels) that isolates the true capability from confounders measured by the proxy.
- Step 3: Empirically show the dissociation — demonstrate cases where the existing metric succeeds but the true capability fails, or vice versa.
- Step 4: Use the diagnostic to characterize the failure mode precisely (e.g., distribution sharpening vs. capability expansion; shallow pattern-matching vs. genuine causal reasoning).
- Step 5: Propose or validate a training/generation procedure that directly targets the true capability as defined by the new metric.
- Step 6: Validate that the new procedure improves on the new metric without simply gaming it, and provide evidence of generalization.

### Success Conditions (from Oral)
- The new metric reveals a clean, reproducible dissociation: cases where the existing metric shows high performance but the new metric shows failure — giving reviewers a compelling empirical 'aha' moment.
- The diagnostic is principled and grounded: likelihood-based novelty, causal consistency metrics, or pass@k analysis all have clear theoretical motivation rather than being ad-hoc thresholds.
- Empirical scope is broad enough to establish universality: multiple RLVR algorithms, multiple reasoning domains (math, coding, visual), or multiple LLM sizes, so the finding is not a narrow artifact.
- The paper delivers an actionable consequence: beyond diagnosis, it proposes a concrete training procedure (counterfactual fine-tuning, synthetic domain-specific PRM data, process reward models) that closes the gap.
- The paper challenges a dominant narrative with evidence rather than opinion — success comes from framing the contribution as empirical falsification of a widely-held belief (e.g., 'RLVR creates new reasoning').

### Failure Modes (from Rejected)
- Insufficient novelty over existing approaches: adding a process reward model to outcome-based RL (SophiaVL-R1) without a principled new diagnostic or a theoretically grounded new objective — reviewers see it as engineering without insight.
- Interesting phenomenon identified but methodological rigor insufficient: 'underthinking' (Thoughts Are All Over the Place) was genuinely novel but the evaluation was insufficiently robust and the proposed fix (TIP decoding) was ad hoc without theoretical justification.
- Evaluation scope too narrow to support general claims: a phenomenon measured on a single model family or single reasoning domain raises doubts about whether the finding generalizes.
- The metric or intervention is not clearly motivated by theory: rejections occur when diagnostic design choices (switching penalty magnitude, threshold values) lack principled selection methods.
- Treating a symptom rather than diagnosing a root cause: applying a penalty at decoding time without understanding why the problem occurs — reviewers demand mechanistic understanding alongside the fix.

### Impact Drivers (from High-Cited)
- Universal applicability with zero additional training: Self-Consistency's majority voting works on any LLM, any task, without fine-tuning — the training-free property made it an immediate default baseline for all subsequent reasoning work, generating thousands of citations.
- Providing infrastructure (datasets, benchmarks) that the community depends on: Let's Verify Step by Step released PRM800K, which became the standard dataset for process reward model research — infrastructure papers attract citations proportional to downstream use.
- Resolving a foundational ambiguity: papers that resolve whether a dominant technique (RLVR, chain-of-thought) truly achieves its claimed goal or merely improves a proxy become canonical references in every subsequent debate about the technique.
- Enabling a new research direction: papers that introduce a new evaluation paradigm (process supervision, causal consistency metrics) attract citations from every paper in the new direction that must position relative to the paradigm.
- Framing a practical recipe that can be adopted plug-and-play: ReST-MCTS* showed how to combine MCTS with process reward guidance without human annotation — a fully automated, adoptable pipeline that practitioners immediately incorporated.

### Cognitive Barriers
- The existing metric appears to measure the right thing: factual accuracy seems to test reasoning; pass@1 seems to test capability; these metrics require careful analysis to reveal they are measuring something different from what is claimed.
- The better metric requires a non-obvious abstraction: recognizing that answer space is smaller than reasoning-path space (enabling majority voting), or that transition bags can represent environment type (enabling likelihood-based novelty), requires seeing across levels of representation.
- Improving the proxy metric is confounded with improving the true capability: RLVR improving pass@1 looks like capability improvement; separating the two requires a new experimental protocol (pass@k at large k) that the field had not applied.
- Step-level supervision seems more expensive, not better: the counter-intuitive finding that denser but costlier supervision (per-step labels) is more efficient per example than cheaper outcome supervision requires empirical validation at scale to be believed.

### Reviewer Expectations
Reviewers in this cluster expect: (1) a clearly identified gap between the current metric and the true capability, with an explicit argument for why the gap matters; (2) a principled diagnostic protocol with theoretical motivation — not an ad hoc threshold or penalty; (3) broad empirical coverage across multiple models, tasks, or domains to establish generality; (4) quantitative results that demonstrate the dissociation cleanly (e.g., base model matches fine-tuned model at large k); (5) a concrete training or inference procedure that acts on the identified failure mode, not just a description of it. Rejections occur when diagnostic choices lack justification, evaluation scope is too narrow, or the contribution is incremental over prior process reward model work without a new mechanistic insight.

### Oral vs Rejected Gap
Oral papers introduce a principled diagnostic that reveals a theoretically motivated failure mode invisible to standard metrics, then demonstrate the failure with broad empirical evidence across multiple models and tasks. Rejected papers either identify interesting phenomena but lack methodological rigor and principled justification for their interventions (underthinking paper), or apply existing PRM frameworks to a new modality without a novel diagnostic insight.

### Oral vs High-Cited Gap
Oral papers in this cluster are primarily diagnostic — they expose failure modes and propose targeted fixes. High-cited papers additionally provide training-free or infrastructure-level contributions that the entire community can adopt immediately: Self-Consistency requires no training and improves any CoT system; PRM800K provides a dataset that enables downstream research. The HC papers became community infrastructure rather than individual contributions, attracting citations from every paper that builds on the paradigm.

### Illustrative Examples
- **[Oral]** *Does Reinforcement Learning Really Incentivize Reasoning Capacity in LLMs Beyond the Base Model?*: Uses pass@k curves to show RLVR improves sampling efficiency without expanding the base model's capability ceiling.
  - Lesson: A single diagnostic metric (pass@k at large k) cleanly separates two previously conflated phenomena, challenging a dominant narrative. The paper succeeds because the diagnostic has clear theoretical motivation and the finding is replicated across multiple RLVR algorithms and reasoning domains.
- **[Oral]** *Reasoning Elicitation in Language Models via Counterfactual Feedback*: Constructs counterfactual test cases to show that factual accuracy and causal consistency are dissociable, then trains on counterfactual data to bridge the gap.
  - Lesson: The dissociation between factual and causal accuracy is the key finding — it reveals that high factual performance does not imply causal reasoning. The oral-level quality comes from both the diagnostic (new metrics) and the fix (counterfactual training), not just one or the other.
- **[HC]** *Self-Consistency Improves Chain of Thought Reasoning in Language Models*: Replaces greedy decoding with majority voting over diverse sampled reasoning paths, achieving state-of-the-art on arithmetic, commonsense, and symbolic reasoning with no training.
  - Lesson: The insight that the answer space is smaller than the reasoning-path space — making answer agreement more reliable than any individual chain — is the key non-obvious move. Universal applicability (any LLM, any task, zero training) drove 6000+ citations because every subsequent reasoning paper needs to cite the default sampling strategy.
- **[Rejected]** *Thoughts Are All Over the Place: On the Underthinking of o1-Like LLMs*: Identifies 'underthinking' (premature thought-switching in reasoning models) and proposes a decoding penalty to discourage it.
  - Lesson: Interesting phenomenon identification is necessary but insufficient. The paper was rejected because the evaluation was not robust, the switching penalty was ad hoc, and multiple reviewers requested major revisions. Phenomenon papers must pair novel observation with methodological rigor.
- **[Oral]** *VersaPRM: Multi-Domain Process Reward Model via Synthetic Reasoning Data*: Diagnoses PRM domain generalization failure and generates synthetic multi-domain step-level training data to fix it.
  - Lesson: The diagnostic (systematic evaluation showing math-trained PRMs fail on law, science, coding) is just as important as the fix (synthetic domain-specific data generation). Oral-level papers show both the failure mode quantitatively and a principled path to resolution.

## C10: Cross-Domain Knowledge Import for AI Systems and Evaluation
*27 Oral, 14 High-Cited, 2 Rejected*

### Core Principle
Recognize that an AI system design or evaluation problem has a structurally identical counterpart in an established non-ML domain (organizational theory, psychophysics, behavioral economics, legal accountability, complexity theory), then import the domain's validated solutions or measurement frameworks directly, adapting them to the AI context rather than re-inventing from scratch.

### Step-by-Step
- Step 1: Identify a persistent failure mode in an AI system (multi-agent hallucination cascade, evaluation unreliability, alignment training blindspot, agent interface mismatch) that existing ML-native solutions have not resolved.
- Step 2: Identify which established non-ML discipline has already solved an analogous problem — organizational management SOPs solve coordination failures; psychophysics models relate response time to preference strength; complexity theory characterizes what a weaker verifier can reliably check.
- Step 3: Formally characterize the structural mapping: identify which entities in the AI problem correspond to which entities in the source domain (LLM agent ↔ team member; preference bandit interaction ↔ drift-diffusion decision; debate protocol ↔ interactive proof system).
- Step 4: Adapt the imported solution to the AI context's specific constraints — e.g., SOPs must be expressed as meta-programs parseable by LLMs; psychophysical models must handle digital response latency noise; complexity constraints must apply to discrete argument structures.
- Step 5: Empirically validate that the imported solution addresses the AI failure mode better than ML-native alternatives, demonstrating the gap on realistic benchmarks.
- Step 6: Characterize the scope conditions — when the import works well vs. when the structural analogy breaks down — to give the community actionable guidance.

### Success Conditions (from Oral)
- The structural mapping between AI problem and source domain is precise and explicit — reviewers accept the import only when the analogy is rigorously argued, not merely metaphorical (e.g., doubly-efficient debate maps to IP complexity classes with formal proofs).
- The import solves a problem that ML-native approaches had failed to address: MetaGPT succeeded because free-form multi-agent communication had provably failed and SOP structure provably prevented cascading errors in software engineering tasks.
- Empirical results demonstrate the gap between the imported solution and existing approaches on realistic, complex tasks — the benchmark must be hard enough that naive baselines fail, exposing the import's value.
- The paper provides interdisciplinary accessibility: it explains the source domain concept clearly enough that ML reviewers unfamiliar with it can evaluate the mapping, and makes the ML connection clear enough that readers from the source domain can understand the contribution.
- The imported framework reveals a new research direction: the best papers (ACI design, open-endedness arguments, algorithmic indistinguishability) open a research agenda beyond the specific system, attracting follow-on work.

### Failure Modes (from Rejected)
- The structural mapping is implicit or incomplete: CAAP imports visual perception framing for computer control but fails to articulate why this framing is better than existing approaches, leaving reviewers unconvinced of novelty over GUI agent baselines.
- The design choices in the import are not justified: PiCO imports peer review for LLM evaluation but the elimination threshold (60%), aggregation method, and other design parameters lack principled selection, making the import seem arbitrary rather than validated.
- The empirical validation is too narrow: testing only on a specific application domain (single software company workflows, single medical benchmark) raises generalizability concerns that rejected papers do not adequately address.
- The analogy is metaphorical rather than structural: papers that merely use source domain vocabulary without formally instantiating the source domain's mechanisms are rejected for superficial framing without technical depth.
- The import adds complexity without demonstrable benefit over simpler alternatives: rejected papers fail to show that the additional machinery (bilevel optimization, multi-agent coordination overhead) yields improvements justifying the added cost.

### Impact Drivers (from High-Cited)
- Infrastructure that becomes community-standard: WebArena, SWE-bench, and Chatbot Arena became the canonical evaluation environments for their respective domains, attracting citations from every paper that needs a realistic benchmark — infrastructure papers compound citations because they are cited by virtue of being the standard, not because researchers are advocating for them.
- Revealing a bottleneck that redirects community research: SWE-agent's ACI finding that interface design (not model capability) was the bottleneck, and GPT-4V(ision)'s finding that grounding (not planning) was the bottleneck, redirected entire subfield research agendas — every subsequent paper in the area must address the identified bottleneck.
- Universal applicability across models and tasks: CodeAct's Python code action space works with any LLM on any tool-use task; multiagent debate works with any model pair — the training-free, model-agnostic property maximizes the population of researchers who can immediately adopt the contribution.
- Bridging technically distant communities: papers that successfully import from organizational theory, psychophysics, or complexity theory attract citations from both the ML community and the source discipline, multiplicatively expanding the citing audience.
- Establishing a named concept: MetaGPT's 'meta-programming,' SWE-agent's 'Agent-Computer Interface,' and WebArena's 'realistic web environment' became vocabulary that subsequent papers are forced to use, embedding the original citation in every subsequent paper that uses the concept.

### Cognitive Barriers
- The source domain solution requires knowledge outside ML training: recognizing that EZ diffusion model response time behavior maps to preference bandit reward strength requires psychophysics knowledge that most ML researchers lack, making the cross-domain connection genuinely non-obvious.
- The AI entity appears categorically different from the source domain entity: it is non-obvious that LLM agents are subject to the same coordination failures as human teams (they don't have ego, fatigue, or miscommunication in the human sense), so importing human SOPs required recognizing structural isomorphism despite surface dissimilarity.
- The destination domain already has strong ML-native solutions being actively improved: importing from non-ML domains requires believing that existing ML-native approaches have fundamental limitations that more optimization within the approach cannot fix.
- The analogy appears too simple to justify a research paper: majority voting over sampled paths (Self-Consistency) seems almost trivially obvious once stated — the cognitive barrier is that nobody had explicitly argued that answer space is smaller than reasoning-path space and verified it empirically.

### Reviewer Expectations
Reviewers in this cluster expect: (1) an explicit and precise structural mapping between the AI problem and the source domain concept, not just metaphorical similarity; (2) formal justification or complexity-theoretic grounding when the import is a theoretical mechanism (e.g., doubly-efficient debate requires proof, not just argument); (3) comprehensive empirical validation on realistic benchmarks that are hard enough to reveal the imported solution's value; (4) clear positioning relative to ML-native approaches, explaining why the non-ML import is necessary rather than just convenient; (5) evaluation of scope conditions — under what circumstances the import works vs. fails. Position papers (governance, open-endedness) receive oral status when they sharply identify technical bottlenecks with concrete actionable proposals rather than general advocacy.

### Oral vs Rejected Gap
Oral papers provide a rigorous structural mapping from a non-ML domain to the AI problem and validate the import with comprehensive experiments on realistic benchmarks, often introducing a named concept or evaluation environment that the community adopts. Rejected papers provide shallow engineering integration (CAAP) or unjustified design parameter choices (PiCO), making the import seem arbitrary rather than principled — reviewers cannot distinguish the design from alternative implementations without a theory of why the specific choices were made.

### Oral vs High-Cited Gap
Oral papers demonstrate that an imported framework improves AI system performance, but high-cited papers additionally become the de facto standard infrastructure or vocabulary that makes them mandatory citations. WebArena, SWE-agent, and Chatbot Arena are cited not because researchers endorse them but because they are the standard evaluation environments — this community infrastructure role generates citations independent of how well the paper argues its case. The gap is community adoption at infrastructure level vs. individual contribution level.

### Illustrative Examples
- **[Oral]** *MetaGPT: Meta Programming for A Multi-Agent Collaborative Framework*: Encodes software development SOPs as meta-programs that specify which LLM agent role produces each artifact and how subsequent agents validate inputs.
  - Lesson: Organizational management theory is structurally isomorphic to multi-agent LLM coordination: role division, handoff protocols, and quality checkpoints solve cascading hallucination exactly as they solve cascading human error. The import works because the structural problem is identical; the oral-level quality comes from making this isomorphism explicit and validating it on realistic software engineering tasks.
- **[HC]** *WebArena: A Realistic Web Environment for Building Autonomous Agents*: Deploys production-grade web applications in Docker containers with pre-populated realistic databases to expose authentic task complexity absent from synthetic benchmarks.
  - Lesson: The import here is from software engineering practice (self-hosting production applications) to AI evaluation design. The paper became a 1000+ citation standard because it established the realistic web environment paradigm — every subsequent web agent paper must cite it. Infrastructure contribution compounds citations regardless of paper quality.
- **[Oral]** *Human Expertise in Algorithmic Prediction*: Uses algorithmic indistinguishability to formally identify when human expert predictions add information beyond the algorithm's training features, then selectively incorporates only those predictions.
  - Lesson: The import is from computational learning theory (multicalibration, distinguishability) to human-AI collaboration design. The key non-obvious move: we don't need to know what information the human has — detecting indistinguishable pairs where the human disagrees is a sufficient proxy, requiring no knowledge of the human's information source.
- **[Rejected]** *CAAP: Context-Aware Action Planning Prompting to Solve Computer Tasks with Front-End UI Only*: Attempts to enable GUI agent automation from screenshots alone using visual UI detection plus LLM action planning, without DOM access.
  - Lesson: The approach imports from HCI (UI-level control) to AI agent design, but the import is too shallow: prompting techniques are described only superficially, the spatial relationship extraction rationale is unexplained, and novelty over existing GUI agents is not established. Reviewers cannot distinguish which design choices are principled imports vs. ad hoc engineering.
- **[Oral]** *Scalable AI Safety via Doubly-Efficient Debate*: Applies computational complexity theory to AI debate safety, defining doubly-efficient debate as a protocol where arguments are polynomial to generate and verify, then proving this prevents superhuman AI from hiding deception in computationally intractable arguments.
  - Lesson: The import from complexity theory to AI safety is the most rigorous example in this cluster: informal safety arguments are translated into formal complexity-theoretic models, and the doubly-efficient property provides proofs rather than intuitions. This demonstrates the highest standard of cross-domain import: the destination domain gains formal guarantees, not just conceptual framing.

## C11: Assumption Inversion and Structural Constraint in Transfer Learning
*23 Oral, 2 High-Cited, 2 Rejected*

### Core Principle
Identify an implicit assumption that all existing methods in a sub-field accept without challenge, then demonstrate that inverting or relaxing this assumption — while introducing a complementary structural constraint — simultaneously removes a known failure mode and enables a new capability that was blocked by the assumption.

### Step-by-Step
- Step 1: Survey the failure modes across an entire sub-field (fine-tuning distorting OOD features, continual learning catastrophic forgetting, unsupervised RL metric collapse, representation extraction defaulting to final layers) and find the single implicit assumption shared by all methods.
- Step 2: Formulate the assumption inversion precisely: 'If we remove labels, what happens to forgetting?', 'If we replace the reconstruction objective with a behavioral equivalence objective, does distractor robustness improve?', 'If we replace state-space distance with temporal distance, does skill diversity scale?'
- Step 3: Provide a theoretical or principled argument for why the inversion should help — either a formal proof (NTK linearity of ReLU MLPs, bisimulation metric fixed-point equations, sample complexity bounds from multi-task theory), or at minimum a mechanistic causal account.
- Step 4: Introduce the structural constraint that makes the inversion viable: LP-FT requires linear probing before fine-tuning; DBC requires a twin-network regression with stop-gradients; self-composing policies require freezing old networks; METRA requires a quasimetric network for temporal distance estimation.
- Step 5: Empirically validate across multiple datasets, architectures, and distribution shift types — demonstrating that the failure mode is eliminated and the new capability is unlocked.
- Step 6: Characterize the scope conditions and limitations explicitly — when does the inverted assumption still hold in some form, and what does the approach not handle?

### Success Conditions (from Oral)
- The assumption inversion is counter-intuitive and challenges established wisdom: claiming that removing labels helps continual learning, or that restricting fine-tuning improves OOD generalization, or that overfitting to individual examples (OCD) improves generalization — each contradicts a community default.
- There is a clean theoretical account: oral papers in this cluster almost universally provide either formal proofs (NTK linearity, non-asymptotic learning bounds, bisimulation contraction) or rigorous mechanistic analysis (head initialization controls OOD error in two-layer linear networks), not just empirical observations.
- Empirical coverage is broad enough to rule out cherry-picking: fine-tuning distortion validated on 10 distribution shift benchmarks; self-training validated on BSCD-FSL benchmark; GNN extrapolation validated across multiple regression tasks — breadth signals robustness.
- The method is simple enough to be adopted without specialist expertise: LP-FT is two training stages; self-composition adds a weighting network; task-level mixup adds one interpolation step per training batch. Implementation simplicity multiplies impact.
- The paper clearly positions against specific prior assumptions and methods — naming the assumption being inverted and identifying which existing methods implicitly hold it — gives reviewers a crisp contribution boundary.

### Failure Modes (from Rejected)
- Incremental improvement without assumption inversion: RangeAugment learns magnitude ranges online instead of fixing them, but this is a refinement within the existing augmentation paradigm rather than an inversion of any core assumption — reviewers see 6/10 or lower when the core idea is 'learn this hyperparameter adaptively' without a structural insight.
- Insufficient differentiation from prior merging techniques: StatsMerging uses teacher statistics to guide model merging, but fails to clearly articulate what assumption prior merging methods make that this inverts, or why statistical overlap is the right proxy — reviewers cannot evaluate whether the design choices are principled.
- Marginal empirical gains that do not justify additional complexity: RangeAugment's bilevel optimization overhead makes it slower than RandAugment without commensurate performance improvement — the assumption inversion must produce gains large enough to motivate the structural change.
- Theory that does not connect to the empirical setting: papers providing theory for two-layer linear networks but experimenting with deep nonlinear networks face skepticism about whether the theory explains the observed phenomenon or is merely illustrative.
- Evaluation scope that raises generalization concerns: papers evaluated on a single domain (only NLP sequence tagging, only visual domains) without discussing where the assumption inversion would or would not hold.

### Impact Drivers (from High-Cited)
- High-cited papers in this cluster (DARE/Language Models are Super Mario; Layer by Layer) reveal a fundamental property of fine-tuning that practitioners had never tested: that 90-99% of fine-tuned parameter deltas are noise-like (DARE), or that final layers are systematically suboptimal for representation extraction (Layer by Layer). These findings change practitioner defaults — every researcher who fine-tunes or extracts representations needs to evaluate the claim in their setting.
- Papers that unlock a free capability — model merging without retraining (DARE), better representations without additional training (Layer by Layer) — attract citations because they provide improvement at zero additional cost, and every practitioner in the area wants to know if the free improvement applies to their system.
- Comprehensive empirical studies (32 tasks, 15+ architectures, multiple distribution shift benchmarks) serve as reference papers for any subsequent work in the area — they are cited as the source of ground truth about the phenomenon, not just as one method among many.
- Papers providing both a theoretical account and a practical recipe become the canonical reference for teaching the concept: DBC (bisimulation + deep RL), LP-FT (feature distortion + two-stage training), and MLTI (task augmentation + meta-regularization) each become the primary citation for their respective phenomena.

### Cognitive Barriers
- The inverted assumption feels like it should make things worse, not better: removing supervision from continual learning sounds like removing useful information; restricting fine-tuning to the head sounds like reducing expressivity; allowing the model to intentionally overfit sounds like guaranteed generalization failure. The barrier is that these intuitions are locally correct but globally wrong.
- The structural constraint required to make the inversion work is non-obvious: bisimulation metrics were considered intractable for continuous spaces — the insight that you only need to satisfy the fixed-point equation (not compute true bisimulation distances) requires knowing that stop-gradient twin-network regression is a valid relaxation.
- The abstraction level is mismatched: recognizing that transition bags can represent environment types (cluster 9), or that fine-tuned latent spaces retain cross-domain semantic structure (StyleAlign), requires reasoning about the statistical properties of optimization processes rather than about the immediate output of individual training steps.
- Prior work in the area has been accepted with the implicit assumption intact, making it feel unnecessary to question: when the entire continual learning literature assumes labeled data, a paper claiming to remove labels seems to be narrowing scope rather than advancing it — the cognitive barrier is reframing the constraint as a cause rather than as a given.

### Reviewer Expectations
Reviewers in this cluster expect: (1) a clearly stated assumption being inverted, identified by name and linked to all prior methods that implicitly hold it; (2) a theoretical account that is mathematically rigorous — at minimum a two-layer linear network analysis, at best full nonlinear proofs; (3) empirical coverage across multiple benchmarks, architectures, and distribution shift types to establish robustness; (4) a concrete structural constraint that makes the inversion tractable — pure assumption inversion without the enabling constraint is dismissed as incomplete; (5) explicit discussion of scope conditions and failure cases. Papers that provide only empirical observation without theoretical account, or theory without empirical validation at realistic scale, receive 6-7/10. Oral-level papers combine clean theory with strong empirical breadth and a practically simple implementation.

### Oral vs Rejected Gap
Oral papers invert a core assumption with theoretical justification and introduce a complementary structural constraint that makes the inversion tractable, then validate with broad empirical coverage. Rejected papers propose incremental refinements within the existing paradigm (online range learning, statistics-guided merging) without a non-obvious assumption inversion — reviewers can see the idea is a parameter-efficient version of existing approaches rather than a structural departure from them.

### Oral vs High-Cited Gap
Oral papers in this cluster are methodologically precise — they solve specific failure modes in specific sub-domains (continual RL, few-shot transfer, unsupervised RL). High-cited papers (DARE's model merging, Layer by Layer's intermediate representations) reveal universal properties of fine-tuning and representation extraction that apply to every practitioner working with any pretrained model — the scope of applicability drives citations. The HC papers also identify phenomena that practitioners had not tested in their own systems, making them feel discovery papers rather than technique papers.

### Illustrative Examples
- **[Oral]** *Fine-Tuning can Distort Pretrained Features and Underperform Out-of-Distribution*: Proves that full fine-tuning from random head initialization distorts pretrained features, and proposes LP-FT (linear probe first, then fine-tune) to prevent distortion.
  - Lesson: The assumption being inverted is 'more optimization always helps generalization.' The inversion is counter-intuitive because full fine-tuning achieves better in-distribution accuracy, yet LP-FT achieves better OOD accuracy. Oral-level quality comes from the theoretical proof in two-layer linear networks plus empirical validation on 10 distribution shift benchmarks.
- **[Oral]** *Representational Continuity for Unsupervised Continual Learning*: Removes labels from continual learning entirely, showing unsupervised representations forget less than supervised ones, and adds LUMP (instance interpolation) as a lightweight memory anchor.
  - Lesson: The assumption inverted is 'labels are necessary for continual learning.' The inversion is backed by mechanistic insight: labels force task-specific feature specialization that becomes fragile when boundaries shift. The simplicity of the resulting method (LUMP adds one interpolation step) is as important as the theoretical insight.
- **[HC]** *Language Models are Super Mario: Absorbing Abilities from Homologous Models as a Free Lunch*: Shows that 90-99% of fine-tuned parameter deltas are noise-like and can be randomly dropped with rescaling, enabling lossless model merging by simple addition.
  - Lesson: The assumption inverted is 'all fine-tuned parameters carry task-specific signal.' The finding that the vast majority are redundant unlocks model merging at no cost. The HC status comes from universal applicability: any practitioner with multiple fine-tuned models from the same base can immediately exploit this, making it a free performance improvement that demands citation.
- **[Rejected]** *RangeAugment: Efficient Online Augmentation with Range Learning*: Attempts to improve automatic augmentation by learning optimal magnitude ranges online via bilevel optimization, replacing fixed range heuristics.
  - Lesson: This is an incremental refinement rather than an assumption inversion: the assumption that magnitude ranges are fixed is relaxed, but there is no structural insight about why bilevel optimization is the right relaxation or what property of ranges makes them learnable. Reviewers rated it 3/3/3/8 — one reviewer saw value, others saw it as marginal over simply tuning RandAugment magnitude.
- **[Oral]** *Learning Invariant Representations for Reinforcement Learning without Reconstruction*: Replaces the reconstruction objective (encode everything) with a bisimulation metric objective (encode only what determines future reward), making distractor-robust representations tractable in continuous deep RL.
  - Lesson: The assumption inverted is 'auxiliary objectives should reconstruct the full input to provide dense signal.' The key structural constraint is the twin-network stop-gradient regression, which makes the apparently intractable bisimulation metric tractable as a self-supervised contraction. The theoretical proof that this converges, combined with CARLA evaluation demonstrating real-world distractor invariance, achieves oral-level acceptance.

## C12: Loss Function Diagnosis and Principled Redesign
*60 Oral, 3 High-Cited, 5 Rejected*

### Core Principle
Identify a specific, named mathematical flaw in an existing loss function or training objective, then redesign it using a principled substitution (geometry, divergence theory, statistical estimation) that eliminates the failure mode by construction. The key move is shifting from 'does this work empirically?' to 'why does this fail theoretically?', then deriving the minimal fix.

### Step-by-Step
- Step 1: Select a widely-used loss function or training objective that practitioners apply without questioning its mathematical assumptions (e.g., cross-entropy, L2, denoising, label assignment).
- Step 2: Perform a formal decomposition or failure-mode analysis of the loss to identify a specific structural flaw — not a general weakness, but a precise mathematical property that causes systematic bias (e.g., treating all errors as equal despite existing metric structure, mass-covering pressure causing degeneration, positive-only supervision lacking discriminative contrast).
- Step 3: Identify an existing mathematical tool from an adjacent field (divergence theory, Bayesian inference, contrastive learning, combinatorial optimization, information theory) that has the exact property needed to fix the identified flaw.
- Step 4: Derive a tractable approximation or reformulation of the new objective if it is not directly optimizable (e.g., derive upper bounds on TVD, construct a central path proxy, derive closed-form relabeling from influence functions).
- Step 5: Prove theoretically (or show analytically) that the new objective strictly dominates the old one with respect to the failure mode identified in Step 2.
- Step 6: Validate empirically across multiple benchmarks and tasks to demonstrate that the theoretical improvement translates to consistent practical gains, with ablations isolating the contribution of each change.

### Success Conditions (from Oral)
- The flaw diagnosis is mathematically precise: reviewers require that you name the specific property of the loss that fails (e.g., 'KLD forces mass-covering, which explains degeneration on outlier tokens') rather than a vague claim that 'the loss is suboptimal'.
- The fix is orthogonal to architecture: Oral papers like D2GPo and TaiLr were praised for being applicable across models and tasks without modifying architecture, demonstrating the fix is at the objective level, not the model level.
- Theory and practice are both present: papers combining a clean theoretical guarantee (e.g., closed-form proof that relabeling dominates removal, TVD-robustness to outliers) with strong empirical results across diverse benchmarks consistently received top scores.
- The proposed substitution is minimal: the fix should change as little as possible (e.g., replace one-hot targets with geometry-aware soft targets, not redesign the entire training pipeline) — reviewers penalized unnecessary complexity.
- Ablation study isolates the contribution of the new objective: each component of the loss change must be validated independently to prove the mathematical fix — not better hyperparameters or architectures — drives improvements.

### Failure Modes (from Rejected)
- Empirical superiority without theoretical mechanism: PBES (PCA exemplar selection) showed that PCA-based selection may work, but could not explain mechanistically why PCA specifically is the right tool — rejected for lacking justification beyond benchmark improvement.
- Confounding training signal with another variable: Imprecise Label Learning was rejected because performance gains were entangled with data augmentation quality, making it impossible to evaluate whether the unified MLE framework itself provided improvement.
- Inconsistent performance across settings: Input Margins paper improved one benchmark but failed on others, indicating the fix was not principled but rather tuned to specific conditions — a clear red flag for reviewers.
- Incremental extension without new insight: PAC-Bayes bounds for LAEs was rejected as an incremental extension of Shalaeva et al., extending multivariate regression to a constrained model class without providing new theoretical insight about why the bound is tight or when it fails.
- Theoretical contribution without practical utility: the temperature-scaling theory paper was rejected for lacking empirical validation and practical deployment guidance despite a correct analytical result.

### Impact Drivers (from High-Cited)
- Becoming the community's default strong baseline: DINO (2473 citations) became the go-to DETR-family detector because it combined SOTA accuracy with a well-engineered open-source implementation — community adoption requires not just a good paper but a usable artifact.
- Solving a training-vs-inference mismatch that blocks practical deployment: YOLOv10 (3877 citations) became massively cited by eliminating NMS from the YOLO pipeline — it addressed a bottleneck that every real-time computer vision practitioner faced.
- Filling a foundational theoretical gap about ubiquitous methods: Cross-Entropy Loss Functions was highly cited for providing the first H-consistency bounds for cross-entropy, a loss used in virtually every classification system — theory about universally-used tools gets universal citation.
- Providing both theory and SOTA empirical performance simultaneously: all three HC papers combined rigorous mathematical treatment with new state-of-the-art results, making them citable from both theoretical and applied perspectives.
- Generality across problem settings: papers that fix a flaw at the level of the loss function — not a specific architecture or dataset — attract broader citation because every practitioner using that loss function has a reason to cite the paper.

### Cognitive Barriers
- The status quo loss function appears principled: L2 loss, cross-entropy, and standard denoising objectives look mathematically grounded and are theoretically justified in limiting cases — diagnosing their failure requires decomposing them into components and analyzing which component fails under realistic conditions, not just in asymptotic settings.
- Practitioners attribute performance gaps to models, not losses: the field's default response to poor performance is to add capacity, change architecture, or augment data — recognizing that the loss function itself is the root cause requires inverting the standard diagnostic hierarchy.
- The fix seems too simple once articulated: after reading PiCO's EM coupling or Distribution Calibration's feature borrowing, the insight seems obvious — this 'obvious-in-hindsight' effect masks the cognitive difficulty of the original insight, which required recognizing structural equivalences across frameworks.
- Connecting adjacent fields requires dual expertise: importing TVD from robust statistics, H-consistency from learning theory, or contrastive signals from representation learning into loss function design requires being conversant in both the source and target domain simultaneously.

### Reviewer Expectations
Reviewers in this cluster expect: (1) a mathematically precise identification of the failure mode — not 'existing losses are suboptimal' but 'KLD imposes mass-covering pressure on low-probability tokens, causing systematic overestimation'; (2) a principled substitution justified by theory, not just empirical search; (3) proof that the substitution fixes the identified flaw — either a theorem or a formal derivation, not just ablation numbers; (4) broad empirical validation across multiple tasks/datasets to demonstrate the fix is not tuned to one benchmark; (5) ablation studies that isolate the objective change from confounding factors (architecture, augmentation, data). Reviewers penalize: vague claims of improvement without mechanistic explanation, improvements entangled with confounders, inconsistent performance across settings, and incremental extensions of existing bounds without new insight.

### Oral vs Rejected Gap
Oral papers provide a mathematically precise failure-mode diagnosis followed by a principled fix derived from theory, while rejected papers either (a) apply an existing tool (PCA, PAC-Bayes) to a new setting without explaining why that tool is the right one, or (b) show empirical gains whose source cannot be disentangled from confounders like data augmentation or hyperparameter tuning.

### Oral vs High-Cited Gap
Oral papers provide principled loss redesigns that advance theory, but high-cited papers go further by solving a practical deployment bottleneck faced by every practitioner (NMS removal for YOLOv10, DETR baseline for DINO) or filling a foundational theoretical gap about a universally-used loss (cross-entropy H-consistency) — citation impact requires that researchers outside the immediate subfield have a concrete reason to use or cite the paper.

### Illustrative Examples
- **[Oral]** *Tailoring Language Generation Models under Total Variation Distance*: Diagnosed text degeneration as caused by KLD's mass-covering property, then derived a tractable TVD-based loss that is outlier-robust by construction.
  - Lesson: Attribute a failure mode to a specific mathematical property of the loss (mass-covering vs. outlier-robustness), then substitute a divergence with the opposite property — one reviewer gave 10/10 for this precise mechanistic framing.
- **[Oral]** *Resolving Training Biases via Influence-based Data Relabeling*: Recognized that the influence function signal used to diagnose harmful samples already encodes their optimal corrected label, shifting from removal to correction with a provable guarantee.
  - Lesson: When a diagnostic signal identifies a problem, check if it also encodes the solution — the conceptual shift from 'remove bad data' to 'correct it using the same signal' required only recognizing that the diagnostic is also prescriptive.
- **[HC]** *DINO: DETR with Improved DeNoising Anchor Boxes for End-to-End Object Detection*: Added contrastive negative anchor boxes to DETR training, addressing the failure that positive-only denoising does not teach discriminative correction of near-miss anchors.
  - Lesson: HC papers require not just a principled fix but a usable artifact — DINO's 2473 citations came from becoming the community's standard strong baseline with open-source code, not merely from the paper's theoretical contribution.
- **[HC]** *YOLOv10: Real-Time End-to-End Object Detection*: Resolved the training-vs-inference mismatch in YOLO by training dual assignment heads simultaneously, then discarding the dense head at inference — achieving NMS-free real-time detection.
  - Lesson: Papers become massively cited when they remove a bottleneck that every practitioner in a large subfield encounters; NMS removal unlocked true end-to-end YOLO deployment for millions of edge-device applications.
- **[Rejected]** *Imprecise Label Learning: A Unified Framework for Learning with Various Imprecise Label Configurations*: Unified noisy, partial, and multi-candidate label settings under MLE over latent labels, but was rejected because performance gains could not be disentangled from data augmentation quality.
  - Lesson: A unification framework must demonstrate that the framework itself — not accompanying implementation choices like augmentation — provides the improvement; always include ablations showing performance without confounding factors.

## C13: Game-Theoretic Intractability Decomposition and Reduction
*16 Oral, 0 High-Cited, 6 Rejected*

### Core Principle
Convert a seemingly intractable multi-agent or sequential decision-making problem into a tractable one by either (a) replacing an intractable solution concept with a computationally equivalent tractable alternative that preserves meaningful guarantees, or (b) exploiting structural asymmetries in the problem (e.g., cooperative vs. adversarial roles, exponential vs. polynomial structure) to decompose the joint problem into solved sub-problems. The key insight is always identifying which component of the problem is the bottleneck and proving that substituting or decomposing that component restores tractability without losing the essential guarantee.

### Step-by-Step
- Step 1: Identify an established pipeline or algorithm that works in a restricted setting (e.g., 2-player zero-sum games, simple MDPs, single-agent RL) and characterize exactly why it fails to generalize to a harder setting (e.g., N-player general-sum, mixed-motive, exponential action spaces).
- Step 2: Diagnose the specific bottleneck component — is it the solution concept (Nash is intractable), the action space (exponential), the optimization landscape (non-convex), or the coverage assumption (offline vs. online)?
- Step 3: Search for a mathematical tool from an adjacent field (evolutionary game theory, convex geometry, online learning theory, behavioral economics) that has the computational properties the bottleneck component lacks.
- Step 4: Define the precise interface: prove that substituting the new component into the existing pipeline preserves the relevant convergence guarantee, theoretical certificate, or equilibrium property — not just empirically, but with formal proofs.
- Step 5: Establish the boundary conditions — identify when the tractability result applies (e.g., specific game classes, coverage conditions, structural assumptions) and when it fails, providing separating examples.
- Step 6: Validate on appropriate benchmarks that test the harder setting (multi-player poker, recommendation games, congestion game instances) to demonstrate that the theoretical result translates to practical settings.

### Success Conditions (from Oral)
- Closing a concrete, named open problem or filling a well-defined gap: every Oral in this cluster is accepted partly because it fills a gap that the community recognizes as open — 'first polynomial algorithm for X,' 'first human-level performance in Y,' 'first convergence guarantee for Z.' The gap must be specific enough that the community can verify it was indeed open.
- The substitution is provably correct: reviewers require formal proofs that the tractable alternative (e.g., alpha-Rank substituting Nash, Caratheodory decomposition substituting full mixed strategy enumeration, EVI relaxing VIs) preserves the essential guarantee — empirical convergence alone does not suffice in this cluster.
- The structural insight generalizes: papers praised for 'unifying a suite of algorithms' (alpha-Rank-PSRO) or 'establishing a new complexity measure that subsumes prior ones' (coverability/self-normalized coefficient) score higher because they reveal structure beyond the specific problem addressed.
- Theory connects to practice through appropriate benchmarks: papers combining tight theoretical bounds with empirical validation on domain-appropriate games (poker, Diplomacy, congestion games) consistently received top scores; purely theoretical results without empirical grounding are accepted but rarely as Oral.
- The result is surprising: Oral papers in this cluster consistently involve a counter-intuitive finding — that agents benefit from coverage distributions they cannot access, that structured disagreement improves OOD performance, that NMS-free training is achievable through dual heads — the surprise factor drives acceptance.

### Failure Modes (from Rejected)
- Constraint relaxation that undermines practical significance: Contextual Bandits with Knapsacks was rejected because the budget constraint was enforced only in expectation — a relaxation that makes the theory tractable but invalidates the results for most real applications. Always validate that theoretical assumptions hold in the target application domain.
- Good results on one domain with undemonstrated generalization: KrwEmd improved poker hand abstraction via K-recall features, but could not demonstrate that the approach scales beyond Texas Hold'em-style games — top venues expect methods to generalize beyond a single game or problem instance.
- Novelty as a mathematical framework without practical utility: Cayley Maze introduced an elegant group-theoretic framework for RL environments but was rejected for having no conclusion, undefined key terms, and no experiments — a strong mathematical idea must be accompanied by at least one concrete demonstration of what it enables.
- Insufficient differentiation from prior work: the Nash equilibrium via meta-learning paper was rejected for insufficient differentiation from the 'learning not to regret' framework, and for limited experimental scope — when working in a well-populated area (regret minimization for Nash), the contribution must be precisely characterized against the closest prior work.
- Narrow scope limiting impact: the differential game solver was rejected for being limited to 2-player zero-sum with one-sided information under Isaacs' condition — while technically correct, the theoretical contribution was deemed too specialized for a top venue's breadth requirements.

### Impact Drivers (from High-Cited)
- Note: Cluster 13 has 0 high-cited papers, so impact drivers are inferred from what the Oral papers emphasize as their broader significance.
- Providing the first polynomial-time algorithm for a natural and long-studied game class: papers like 'Efficiently Computing Nash Equilibria in Adversarial Team Markov Games' achieve impact by solving a problem that appears in many applications (cooperative robotics, security games, multi-team competition) — algorithmic breakthroughs for natural problem classes are broadly cited.
- Establishing a new complexity measure that predicts practical behavior: 'Bridging RL Theory and Practice with the Effective Horizon' achieved impact by providing the first measure that predicts when deep RL succeeds or fails — tools that make theory practically useful drive broad adoption and citation.
- Opening a new research direction rather than closing one: 'Modeling content creator incentives on algorithm-curated platforms' opened the field of pre-deployment algorithmic auditing via game theory — papers that define a new research agenda and provide the first rigorous treatment attract citations from every subsequent paper in that direction.
- Milestone results with rigorous human-play evaluation: 'Human-Level Performance in No-Press Diplomacy' achieved impact through verifiable milestone setting (first human-level AI in a complex mixed-motive multi-player game) — milestone papers are cited even when their component techniques are adapted from prior work.

### Cognitive Barriers
- The harder problem appears to require fundamentally different techniques: practitioners assume that moving from 2-player zero-sum to N-player general-sum games requires entirely new theory — the counter-intuitive insight that substituting only the meta-solver (Nash → alpha-Rank) unlocks generalization requires recognizing that the bottleneck is the solution concept, not the learning procedure.
- Intractability results are treated as fundamental rather than structural: the existence of PPAD-hardness for Nash equilibria leads researchers to avoid game-theoretic formulations of multi-agent problems — the insight that tractability can be restored by exploiting specific structural properties (adversarial team asymmetry, behavioral economic assumptions, EVI relaxation) requires questioning whether the problem's hardness is intrinsic or a consequence of the specific formulation.
- Geometric tools are not in the standard ML toolkit: applying Caratheodory's theorem to decompose exponential action spaces, or using topological analysis of strategy manifolds, requires expertise in convex geometry that most ML practitioners lack — importing these tools requires knowing both the ML problem and the geometric literature.
- Domain-specific assumptions mask shared mathematical structure: coverage conditions in offline RL, best-response oracles in game theory, and regret minimization in online learning appear as separate toolboxes — recognizing that coverability is a structural property of MDPs that enables online RL (not just offline) required stepping outside the framing of the offline/online RL divide.

### Reviewer Expectations
Reviewers in this cluster expect: (1) a formally characterized gap — the paper must name the specific problem class that was previously intractable and explain precisely why existing methods fail; (2) formal correctness — convergence or equilibrium proofs are mandatory, not optional; (3) non-trivial structural insight — it is not sufficient to apply a known technique; the key theoretical move must require non-trivial adaptation (e.g., proving that alpha-Rank's SSCC structure is compatible with PSRO, not just observing that alpha-Rank computes faster); (4) boundary conditions — which game classes or settings does the result apply to, and what is the tightest such characterization; (5) appropriate empirical scope — multi-player game benchmarks, not toy 2x2 matrices. Reviewers penalize: constraint relaxations that undermine real-world applicability, methods validated only on one domain, and insufficient differentiation from prior work in regret minimization or equilibrium computation.

### Oral vs Rejected Gap
Oral papers close a formally characterized open problem with provably correct substitutions that preserve essential guarantees (convergence, equilibrium, complexity bounds), while rejected papers either (a) make realistic assumptions that simplify the problem enough to undermine practical relevance (budget constraints in expectation only), or (b) demonstrate good results in one specific domain without evidence that the approach generalizes to the broader problem class.

### Oral vs High-Cited Gap
This cluster has no high-cited papers, making direct comparison impossible. Based on Oral paper patterns, high-cited papers in game theory / multi-agent RL typically require either: (a) landmark milestone achievements (human-level in a widely-known game) that non-specialists can appreciate, or (b) tools that become the community's standard approach for analyzing a broad class of problems — the Oral papers here provide important theoretical advances but tend to address specialized game classes that limit citation breadth.

### Illustrative Examples
- **[Oral]** *A Generalized Training Approach for Multiagent Learning*: Replaced Nash equilibrium (intractable, non-unique for N-player games) with alpha-Rank (polynomial, unique) as PSRO's meta-solver, unlocking generalization to N-player general-sum games with proved convergence.
  - Lesson: When a pipeline's bottleneck is a computationally intractable component, identify a tractable alternative with the same structural properties and prove substitution preserves the pipeline's guarantees — the non-obvious move is recognizing that alpha-Rank was an analysis tool, not a generative optimizer.
- **[Oral]** *Semi Bandit Dynamics in Congestion Games: Convergence to Nash Equilibrium and No-Regret Guarantees*: Applied Caratheodory's theorem to reduce exponential mixed-strategy spaces to polynomial sparse combinations, enabling gradient descent over exponentially large action sets in polynomial time.
  - Lesson: Geometric decomposition theorems (Caratheodory, Carathéodory-type dimension reductions) can resolve the curse of dimensionality in game-theoretic action spaces — the key is recognizing that any point in the convex hull of actions has a sparse representation.
- **[Oral]** *Tractable Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning through Behavioral Economics*: Imported behavioral economics' Quantal Response Equilibrium as the target solution concept instead of Nash, converting an intractable equilibrium computation into a tractable fixed-point problem.

- **[Rejected]** *Contextual Bandits with Knapsacks beyond Worst Cases via Re-Solving*: Achieved instance-dependent regret bounds for CBwK via LP re-solving, but enforced budget constraints only in expectation — too weak for most real applications.
  - Lesson: Theoretical tractability gained by relaxing constraints must be validated against whether those constraints are necessary for the claimed application — a budget enforced only in expectation is qualitatively different from one enforced per-round.
- **[Rejected]** *Cayley Maze: Universal Open-Ended Reinforcement Learning Environment*: Proposed representing RL environments as Cayley graphs of finite groups for mathematical complexity control, but paper was rejected for having no conclusion, undefined key terms, and no experiments.
  - Lesson: A strong mathematical framework needs at least one concrete experiment demonstrating what the framework enables — reviewers need to see the framework produce a result that could not be obtained without it, not just a description of what it could theoretically do.
- **[Oral]** *Expected Variational Inequalities*: Relaxed pointwise VI constraints to distributional expectation constraints (EVI), converting a computationally intractable problem into one solvable in polynomial time, while preserving Nash equilibrium recovery.
  - Lesson: Converting pointwise constraints to expectation constraints (or from worst-case to average-case) is a systematic technique for restoring tractability — prove that the relaxation is minimal (i.e., no tractable formulation lies between the strict and relaxed versions) to demonstrate necessity.

## C14: Tight Bound Resolution via Structural Property Exploitation
*52 Oral, 2 High-Cited, 3 Rejected*

### Core Principle
Solve a long-open or previously-thought-hard problem by identifying a structural property (low sensitivity, geometric decomposition, compositional parallelism, lattice structure, information-theoretic dimension) that makes the problem tractable when analyzed directly rather than via generic composition or worst-case tools. The central move is identifying what property has been overlooked and proving that exploiting it either (a) resolves an open gap between upper and lower bounds, or (b) converts an exponential-time or intractable problem into a polynomial one without losing meaningful guarantees.

### Step-by-Step
- Step 1: Identify a problem where the gap between known upper and lower bounds is large, or where the best known algorithm is known to be suboptimal relative to information-theoretic limits — this signals that the right structural property has not yet been identified.
- Step 2: Conduct a failure-mode analysis of existing approaches: why do generic composition theorems, worst-case analyses, or standard reductions yield loose bounds? Name the specific structural property they fail to exploit (e.g., low sensitivity of min-cut values, compositional parallelism of DP mechanisms, geometric approximate orthogonality in high dimensions).
- Step 3: Identify the correct structural property by asking: what does the problem instance actually look like that makes it easier than the worst case? For privacy, look for sensitivity structure; for combinatorial problems, look for lattice or submodular structure; for statistical problems, look for dimension-dependent concentration.
- Step 4: Design a direct algorithm or proof that exploits the identified structural property — avoid composing existing generic tools if the structure enables something more tailored (e.g., apply the exponential mechanism directly to min-cut values rather than composing DP releases).
- Step 5: Prove matching lower bounds to establish tightness — Oral papers in this cluster almost universally provide both matching upper and lower bounds; a result with only an upper bound improvement is accepted as a poster but rarely as Oral.
- Step 6: Demonstrate that the tight bounds have practical implications — connect the theoretical result to concrete algorithmic improvements (e.g., sublinear data access for private top-k, one-training-run privacy auditing, NMS-free real-time inference).

### Success Conditions (from Oral)
- Matching upper and lower bounds: the single strongest predictor of Oral acceptance in this cluster is providing tight bounds — papers like 'Nearly Tight Bounds for DP Multiway Cut,' 'Optimal Mistake Bounds for Transductive Online Learning,' and 'Optimal Learners for Realizable Regression' all achieved Oral by proving the matching lower bound alongside the upper bound, establishing that no algorithm can do better.
- Resolving a named open problem or correcting a prior result: reviewers consistently praise papers that close a 'long-standing open problem' or 'correct a widely-cited impossibility result' — this requires explicitly identifying the gap in prior work and proving the gap is now closed. 'AdaBoost is not an Optimal Weak to Strong Learner' achieved Oral by explicitly showing AdaBoost's sub-optimality against a known lower bound.
- The structural insight is non-generic and surprising: the strongest Oral papers exploit structural properties specific to the problem class — e.g., DP's parallel composition property enabling one-run auditing, or high-dimensional approximate orthogonality enabling architecture-agnostic canary detection. Reviewers explicitly reward novelty of the proof technique, not just the result.
- Algorithmic contribution with demonstrated practical speedup: papers that translate tight bounds into practical improvements (orders-of-magnitude speedup in privacy auditing, sublinear data access, polynomial time for previously exponential tasks) receive higher scores than those with purely theoretical improvements.
- Clean, minimal proofs with broadly applicable techniques: 'Privacy Auditing with One (1) Training Run' was praised for a clean Theorem 3.1 that bridges audit statistics and privacy directly — proofs that introduce a technique reusable beyond the specific paper (e.g., Hilbert-space Freedman inequality, self-normalized coefficients) generate follow-on citations.

### Failure Modes (from Rejected)
- Providing theory for an impractically restricted model class: 'Data Debugging is NP-hard for Classifiers Trained with SGD' was rejected because the hardness result applied only to linear classifiers, which are too restrictive for practical ML — theoretical results must target model classes that practitioners actually use, or explicitly analyze the gap to practical settings.
- Theoretical clarification without algorithmic contribution: 'Avoiding Pitfalls for Privacy Accounting of Subsampled Mechanisms' was rejected as more appropriate for a technical note than a full paper — pointing out subtleties in prior analyses without providing a new algorithm, tighter bound, or constructive technique does not meet the bar for a top venue.
- Unclear motivation and insufficient practical implications: 'Towards a Better Theoretical Understanding of Independent Subnetwork Training' was rejected because the introduction failed to clearly motivate the problem and the practical significance of convergence bounds for IST was not articulated — even technically correct results must explain concretely what becomes possible given the theorem.
- Incremental extension without new structural insight: papers that extend PAC-Bayes bounds to new model classes, or composition theorems to new settings, without introducing a new mathematical technique or resolving a known gap risk rejection as incremental — the contribution must add a structural insight, not just add a case to an existing theorem.
- Tight bounds in a setting too narrow for the venue: the differential game solver (Isaacs' condition, 2-player zero-sum, one-sided information) had correct and novel results but was deemed too specialized — the structural insight must apply to a problem class broad enough to be of general interest to the conference audience.

### Impact Drivers (from High-Cited)
- Resolving a 30-year open problem with an exponentially improved bound: 'Optimal Mistake Bounds for Transductive Online Learning' became high-cited by providing a tight Theta(sqrt(d)) bound — open problems attract citations from every paper that subsequently builds on the resolved characterization, making the citation lifetime very long.
- Unifying apparently distinct optimization problems under a single framework with algorithmic reuse: 'Corporate Needs You to Find the Difference: Revisiting Submodular and Supermodular Ratio Optimization Problems' was cited for showing that densest subgraph, densest supermodular set, and submodular minimization are all instances of ratio optimization — unification papers enable algorithm transfer and become anchor references for entire subfields.
- Providing a technique that is reusable across many subsequent papers: the Littlestone tree dimension reduction technique introduced in the transductive mistake bound paper is a new combinatorial proof tool — papers that introduce a reusable technique rather than solving only a specific problem have much longer citation half-lives.
- Establishing tight bounds that eliminate wasted research effort: tight bounds that close a gap prevent the community from continuing to search for improvements that don't exist — papers that prove lower bounds become mandatory citations for all subsequent upper-bound papers in the area.
- Making privacy guarantees practically achievable at scale: papers that translate formal DP guarantees into practical systems (one-training-run auditing, sublinear data access, architecture-agnostic canary detection) are cited both by theory papers and by practitioners implementing DP systems.

### Cognitive Barriers
- Generic composition is the default first attempt: most researchers approaching a privacy or combinatorial problem apply existing composition theorems or reduction frameworks, which are intellectually accessible — the structural insight that the specific problem has lower sensitivity or better structure than the generic framework assumes requires stepping outside the established toolkit and analyzing the problem from first principles.
- Lower bound proofs require adversarial construction intuition: constructing matching lower bounds requires imagining and formally verifying worst-case inputs, a skill that is qualitatively different from upper-bound algorithm design — many researchers can design algorithms but cannot construct tight adversarial instances, creating an asymmetry in what gets published.
- High-dimensional geometry is counter-intuitive: exploiting approximate orthogonality of random vectors in high dimensions (one-shot privacy auditing), or the concentration of measure phenomenon (distributional TD learning), requires comfort with high-dimensional probability that is not part of the standard ML curriculum.
- Cross-domain structural insights require dual expertise: connecting DP accounting to parallel composition (one-run auditing), or submodular function theory to densest subgraph algorithms (ratio optimization unification), requires deep knowledge of both the source and target domains — the structural equivalence is only visible from a vantage point that requires expertise in both fields simultaneously.

### Reviewer Expectations
Reviewers in this cluster expect: (1) both matching upper and lower bounds — a result without a matching lower bound is accepted as a poster but rarely as Oral; (2) explicit identification of the prior gap — 'this problem has been open since [year]' or 'prior work achieved [bound] but ours achieves [tighter bound]'; (3) the structural property that enables the improvement must be named and proved — not 'we use a better analysis' but 'the min-cut sensitivity is O(1) per edge, and prior work ignored this by composing generic DP releases'; (4) practical implications — even theory-heavy papers must explain concretely what becomes possible (e.g., one training run instead of thousands, polynomial instead of exponential time); (5) broad enough scope — the structural insight should apply to a problem class of general interest, not a single narrow instance. Reviewers penalize: results limited to linear models or toy settings, clarification contributions without new algorithms or bounds, and papers that extend an existing theorem to one more case without introducing new proof technique.

### Oral vs Rejected Gap
Oral papers introduce a new structural insight (low sensitivity, compositional parallelism, geometric orthogonality, dimension-based characterization) that resolves a gap between known upper and lower bounds, while rejected papers either restrict analysis to too-narrow model classes (linear classifiers only), provide clarification without algorithmic contribution, or fail to articulate why the theoretical result matters for practical systems.

### Oral vs High-Cited Gap
Oral papers provide tight bounds and novel proof techniques that advance theoretical foundations, but high-cited papers go further by either resolving a named open problem with decades of history (transductive mistake bounds, 30-year open problem) or unifying an entire family of problems under a single algorithmic framework (submodular ratio optimization) — the citation multiplier comes from becoming the anchor reference for a broad sub-community rather than from advancing one specific problem's bounds.

### Illustrative Examples
- **[Oral]** *Nearly Tight Bounds For Differentially Private Multiway Cut*: Identified that min-cut values have bounded sensitivity, enabling direct application of the exponential mechanism and an exponential improvement over generic composition bounds for k-way cut.
  - Lesson: When a problem has been approached with generic composition tools, analyze whether the problem's specific structure (here: cut sensitivity is O(1) per edge) enables a simpler direct mechanism that strictly outperforms generic composition — the simplicity of the optimal algorithm was the surprise, not the complexity.
- **[Oral]** *Privacy Auditing with One (1) Training Run*: Exploited DP's parallel composition property to audit many canary examples simultaneously within a single training run, reducing auditing cost from O(n) training runs to O(1).

  - Lesson: Resolving named open problems with decades of history generates long-lasting citations — the paper becomes the anchor reference for all subsequent work on the question. The tight characterization (proving both achievability and impossibility) is what makes it definitive rather than just a partial advance.
- **[HC]** *Corporate Needs You to Find the Difference: Revisiting Submodular and Supermodular Ratio Optimization*: Showed that densest subgraph, densest supermodular set, and submodular minimization are all instances of f(S)/|S| ratio optimization, enabling algorithm transfer across seemingly separate problems.

- **[Rejected]** *Avoiding Pitfalls for Privacy Accounting of Subsampled Mechanisms under Composition*: Correctly identified two common misconceptions in privacy accounting for composed subsampled mechanisms, but was rejected as more appropriate for a technical note than a research paper.
  - Lesson: Correctly identifying a practical pitfall is insufficient for a top-venue paper if the contribution is solely clarification — a full paper requires either a new algorithm that avoids the pitfall, a tighter bound that quantifies its impact, or a constructive technique that the community can use going forward.
- **[Oral]** *One-shot Empirical Privacy Estimation for Federated Learning*: Exploited high-dimensional approximate orthogonality to design random canary vectors that are statistically distinguishable from Gaussian noise without any optimization, enabling architecture-agnostic one-shot privacy estimation.
  - Lesson: Leveraging high-dimensional geometry as a resource (approximate orthogonality of random vectors is automatic in high dimensions) can replace expensive optimization procedures — the insight required thinking geometrically about the problem rather than adversarially.

## C15: Variational Reformulation for Intractable RL Objectives
*24 Oral, 2 High-Cited, 8 Rejected*

### Core Principle
Convert an intractable sequential decision-making objective (distribution estimation, constraint satisfaction, or policy evaluation) into a tractable minimax or variational optimization problem by exploiting dual representations, fixed-point characterizations, or extreme-value theory — then provide both theoretical guarantees and empirical validation on non-trivial benchmarks. The key move is reframing 'what do we want to compute' as 'what optimization problem has that as its solution'.

### Step-by-Step
- Step 1: Identify the intractable quantity — a distribution ratio, a value function under partial feedback, a stationary distribution, or a policy update — and ask what mathematical structure characterizes it (fixed-point equation, dual program, statistical limit theorem).
- Step 2: Choose the reformulation tool that exploits that structure — f-divergence variational dual, Fenchel biconjugate, extreme value theory (Gumbel), PAC-Bayes prior, or convex duality — and convert the original problem into a minimax or saddle-point optimization.
- Step 3: Handle degeneracy and edge cases explicitly: add normalization constraints to prevent ratio collapse (GenDICE), identify and block problematic gradient pathways (semi-gradient DICE), or restrict assumptions to match the practical regime (L2 vs L-infinity score accuracy).
- Step 4: Derive formal guarantees — consistency, finite-sample regret bounds, or asymptotic efficiency — that go beyond empirical validation and precisely characterize when and why the method outperforms alternatives.
- Step 5: Demonstrate on benchmarks that go beyond what prior work tested, including offline settings, undiscounted horizons, partial-feedback environments, or safety-constrained scenarios, to establish genuine generality.
- Step 6: Reveal unexpected connections or corollaries (offline PageRank from GenDICE, SAM from Bayesian duality) that show the formulation has broader implications than the original problem.

### Success Conditions (from Oral)
- The reformulation removes a genuine structural obstacle (unknown behavior policy, need for policy samples, vacuous supremum bounds) rather than just offering a different perspective on an already-solved problem — reviewers praised GenDICE for eliminating behavior-policy dependence entirely.
- Formal consistency or convergence guarantees are provided and verified; papers like GenDICE, XQL, and Offline IRL MLE received high marks for combining theoretical guarantees with empirical validation on continuous-control tasks more complex than prior benchmarks.
- The paper reveals a previously unrecognized connection between the target quantity and an analytically tractable object from classical mathematics (EVT's Gumbel distribution for LogSumExp in XQL, Fenchel biconjugate for SAM) — reviewers prize this kind of cross-domain bridge.
- The algorithm derived from the reformulation is clean and practical: GenDICE's saddle-point is solvable by neural networks with a single extra normalization constraint; XQL's Gumbel assumption converts a sampling problem to parameter estimation with no extra inference calls.
- Edge cases that killed prior methods are explicitly handled with theoretical justification — normalization preventing ratio degeneracy at gamma=1, PAC-Bayes priors that remain valid without bounded importance weight assumptions.

### Failure Modes (from Rejected)
- Theoretical contributions are not sufficiently novel relative to existing work: the rejected CORSDICE paper identified a real bug (semi-gradient bias) but overclaimed contributions and did not acknowledge concurrent work, while the distributional OPE paper had solid theory but used realizability assumptions that cannot hold in practice.
- Framing findings as observations rather than a principled solution: 'Challenges of Exploration for Offline RL' raised important questions but presented empirical observations without providing an actionable algorithm, leading reviewers to view it as analysis rather than contribution.
- Theoretical bounds that appear sound but contain large unfavorable constants (density-ratio constant C_sup = p_max/p_min) that make results practically vacuous — reviewers caught this in the distributional off-policy evaluation paper.
- Incomplete literature survey or failure to properly position relative to prior work: the Constrained PPO paper missed Borkar (2005) and prior constrained actor-critic literature, leading to rejection despite numerically reasonable results.
- Theory that does not connect back to practical settings: PAC-Bayesian bounds for deep RL agents or sequential policies that are technically valid but still too loose or restricted to specific conditions (realizability, mixing-time estimation) to inform deployment decisions.

### Impact Drivers (from High-Cited)
- QoQ-Med (domain-aware GRPO) was cited by subsequent clinical AI papers because it tackled a high-stakes real-world problem (multi-modal clinical reasoning) and introduced a principled fix (domain-rarity reward scaling) that the community could immediately adopt in similar imbalanced multi-domain RL training scenarios.
- The adversarial unbiasedness paper (bank loan problem) had community impact beyond its borderline acceptance because it bridged two active fields (algorithmic fairness and online learning) in a way that introduced a concrete anti-pattern to avoid (self-reinforcing feedback loops from biased decisions).
- High-cited papers in this cluster addressed problems that were simultaneously technically rich and societally important, giving them traction in both ML theory and application communities — clinical foundation models and fair loan decisions are adopted by practitioners regardless of marginal theoretical novelty.
- Impact in this cluster comes from providing the first end-to-end solution to a problem class, not just improving on an existing approach — being 'first open generalist clinical model' or 'first framework combining adversarial de-biasing with online regret analysis' creates citation anchors.

### Cognitive Barriers
- The instinct is to search for a better algorithm, not to ask whether the target quantity is itself the solution to a different optimization problem — recognizing that a stationary distribution is the fixed-point of a Bellman flow operator requires stepping back from the algorithmic perspective entirely.
- Dual representations (f-divergence variational, Fenchel biconjugate) are from functional analysis and are not part of standard RL or ML training; the cognitive barrier is recognizing when classical convex duality applies to an RL objective and knowing which dual to use.
- In partial-feedback settings, researchers naturally focus on the estimation problem (how to correct for selection bias) rather than on the structure of what is being estimated — the insight that fixing the distribution being estimated (via adversarial unbiasedness) can break feedback loops is counter to standard estimation thinking.
- Extreme value theory characterizes the distribution of maxima in classical statistics, but RL practitioners think in terms of sampling from policies, not distributional limits; recognizing that LogSumExp is an expected maximum whose distributional limit is Gumbel requires familiarity with a statistics literature outside ML.

### Reviewer Expectations
Reviewers in this cluster expect: (1) A precise statement of which structural obstacle the reformulation removes, not just a new perspective. (2) Formal guarantees (consistency, regret, finite-sample bounds) that are non-vacuous — constants must be characterized and shown to be small in practical settings. (3) Experiments on benchmarks that are strictly harder than what prior work evaluated (offline, undiscounted, partial feedback, continuous control). (4) Acknowledgment of concurrent and prior related work with clear positioning — missing key references (Borkar 2005, DualDICE extensions) is penalized heavily. (5) At least one surprising or unexpected consequence of the reformulation that demonstrates generality beyond the main application (e.g., offline PageRank, Adam-SAM extension).

### Oral vs Rejected Gap
Oral papers provided a genuine structural removal of a prior obstacle with non-vacuous guarantees and tested on harder benchmarks, while rejected papers either identified real problems without offering principled solutions (exploration for offline RL), provided theoretically sound methods with impractically large constants or untestable assumptions (distributional OPE, constrained PPO), or overclaimed novelty relative to existing literature. The single most critical differentiator is whether the paper closes a problem or only frames it.

### Oral vs High-Cited Gap
Oral papers in this cluster established methodological foundations (GenDICE's generalization of DICE, XQL's EVT approach, Offline IRL's MLE framework) that may take years to accumulate citations, while high-cited papers addressed immediately deployable applications in high-impact domains (clinical AI, algorithmic fairness) where practitioners adopt tools quickly regardless of theoretical depth. High-cited papers also explicitly targeted community infrastructure needs (first open generalist clinical model, first fairness-preserving online learner), creating citation anchors that pure theory papers lack.

### Illustrative Examples
- **[Oral]** *GenDICE: Generalized Offline Estimation of Stationary Values*: Reformulates stationary distribution estimation as a minimax f-divergence minimization, eliminating behavior-policy dependence and the gamma<1 restriction.
  - Lesson: A clean variational reformulation that removes a genuine structural requirement (known behavior policy) earns high marks; the unexpected application to offline PageRank demonstrates generality that reviewers prize.
- **[Oral]** *Extreme Q-Learning: MaxEnt RL without Entropy*: Models Q-value distributions as Gumbel and computes LogSumExp in closed form via extreme value theory, eliminating policy sampling in offline RL.
  - Lesson: Importing a classical statistics limit theorem (EVT) to convert a sampling problem into a parameter estimation problem is a paradigmatic example of the cross-domain bridge that oral papers execute — the insight is reframing what kind of problem you are solving.
- **[Oral]** *SAM as an Optimal Relaxation of Bayes*: Applies the Fenchel biconjugate to the Bayesian objective and shows the relaxation exactly recovers SAM's update rule, connecting adversarial perturbation to Bayesian inference.
  - Lesson: Working backward from a known heuristic to find the principled objective that justifies it is a valid and highly-rewarded strategy — the Adam-SAM extension as a byproduct demonstrates actionability.
- **[Rejected]** *The Challenges of Exploration for Offline Reinforcement Learning*: Demonstrates empirically that exploration strategy for offline data collection matters but does not provide a principled solution.
  - Lesson: Framing findings as empirical observations without a principled, actionable algorithm converts a potentially strong contribution into a marginal one — reviewers expect the paper to close the problem it identifies.
- **[HC]** *QoQ-Med: Building Multimodal Clinical Foundation Models with Domain-Aware GRPO Training*: Scales reward magnitude inversely with domain rarity during multi-domain RL training, preventing optimization collapse to dominant modalities in a clinical foundation model.
  - Lesson: High citation impact in this cluster comes from addressing a real-world application domain with a concrete, immediately adoptable fix — the domain-rarity reward scaling is a transferable technique that practitioners can apply in any imbalanced multi-domain RL setting.

## C16: Geometric Reparameterization for Efficient Neural Optimization
*34 Oral, 4 High-Cited, 3 Rejected*

### Core Principle
Identify a geometric or algebraic structure in the parameter or gradient space of a neural network and exploit it to resolve a computational-versus-expressiveness tradeoff — either by constructing an implicit encoding of an expensive operation (preconditioning, constraint satisfaction, data compression) through architecture or parameterization, or by diagnosing a hidden mismatch between sequentially composed operations and fixing it with a targeted algebraic correction. The key move is treating parameter geometry, not loss values, as the primary object of design.

### Step-by-Step
- Step 1: Identify a performance gap or computational bottleneck in an existing method and diagnose its root cause at the geometric or algebraic level — ask whether the gap arises from a parameterization mismatch, an unconsidered structural property, or an implicit constraint that is being violated.
- Step 2: Select the geometric primitive that characterizes the solution space — gradient subspace (GPM), Riemannian manifold (WarpGrad, DoRA, parameter symmetries), convex cone (hidden convex landscape), or Jacobian factorization (Lipschitz parameterization) — and construct the targeted fix around it.
- Step 3: Verify that the proposed geometric operation is both complete (covers all desired solutions) and computationally tractable — the SVD of layer activations, alternating low-rank SVD initialization, or smooth surjective parameterizations must be implementable at training scale.
- Step 4: Provide a theoretical result that characterizes the consequence of the geometric structure — convergence rate improvement (WarpGrad), provable variance reduction (Rao-Blackwellized STGS), global minimum characterization (hidden convex landscape), or generalization bound (parameter symmetries).
- Step 5: Demonstrate practical impact across multiple architectures or tasks, not just the motivating example — SparseGPT on 100B+ GPT models, LoftQ across multiple quantization levels and model families, GPM across diverse CL benchmarks.
- Step 6: Identify and communicate the unexpected consequence or elegant byproduct of the geometric viewpoint — data privacy from GPM's gradient projection, drop-in replaceability, or a polynomial-time optimality check from the convex cone characterization.

### Success Conditions (from Oral)
- The geometric insight resolves a concrete limitation of a widely-used method rather than offering a purely theoretical refinement — DoRA achieves near-full-fine-tuning performance at LoRA efficiency by diagnosing that LoRA conflates magnitude and direction updates; LoftQ fixes the initialization pathology created when quantization and LoRA are composed without compensation.
- The method is computationally tractable with minimal overhead — GPM's SVD is computed once per task; LoftQ's alternating optimization is cheap; Rao-Blackwellized STGS requires no additional function evaluations; reviewers explicitly praised practical efficiency as a prerequisite for acceptance.
- The theoretical contribution is both novel and independently useful — Lemma 5.1 (radial/tangential gradient decomposition) in the implicit bias paper, the Rao-Blackwell theorem applied to straight-through estimators, and the convex cone characterization of ReLU network optima were all cited by reviewers as standalone contributions.
- Results are demonstrated on at least two or three distinct tasks or architectures to establish that the geometric insight generalizes beyond the specific motivating case — GPM tested across permuted MNIST, split CIFAR, and multiple benchmarks; SparseGPT validated on OPT and GPT families.
- The paper explicitly positions itself as providing a complete solution (no residual limitation of the core insight) — the convex cone paper emphasizes that the characterization covers all global optima, not just some, earning uniform 8/10 scores.

### Failure Modes (from Rejected)
- Insufficient differentiation from prior work in the same geometric space — the Transformer Compression via Subspace Projection paper proposed layer-wise SVD compression that reviewers identified as overlapping with existing low-rank approximation methods without adequate empirical or theoretical separation.
- Limited experimental scope that fails to validate the method beyond toy settings — the LN-to-RMSNorm algebraic equivalence paper was sound in theory but only validated on small-scale datasets without measuring actual training/inference speedup, which is the primary claimed benefit.
- Incremental extension of a prior method without sufficient new insight — the Symbiotic Tuning paper for side-network adaptation was viewed as modest augmentation of Ladder Side-Tuning without a principled new geometric or optimization insight, and lacked reproducible experimental details.
- Claiming a benefit that the theoretical analysis contradicts — methods that claim linear speedup but whose convergence analysis shows sub-linear speedup (MALCOM-PSGD) are penalized heavily because this suggests the authors did not fully understand their own result.
- Missing quantitative runtime benchmarks when efficiency is the primary claim — algebraic simplifications or structural tricks that promise computational savings must be validated with wall-clock measurements, not just parameter counts or theoretical FLOPs.

### Impact Drivers (from High-Cited)
- DoRA (1,700+ citations) became highly cited because it directly improved upon LoRA — the most widely-used parameter-efficient fine-tuning method — with a principled, minimal-overhead fix that practitioners could immediately integrate into existing LoRA workflows without architectural changes.
- DeBERTaV3 accumulated 1,700+ citations by providing a stronger open-source NLP backbone that the community adopted as a standard fine-tuning baseline; the gradient-disentangled embedding fix was secondary to the community infrastructure value of a better open model.
- GLM-130B became highly cited as the first fully open 100B-scale model, demonstrating that engineering transparency (documenting training instabilities, releasing INT4 quantization) can create more citation impact than algorithmic novelty — it filled a gap where the community needed a reproducible frontier-scale baseline.
- Scaling and evaluating sparse autoencoders attracted citations from the interpretability community because it established SAE scaling laws — a previously missing foundational result — and the Top-k hard constraint improved practical training stability, making it a reference methodology for LLM mechanistic interpretability.
- High-cited papers in this cluster provide community infrastructure (open models, scaling laws, drop-in improvements to dominant methods) that creates sustained citation demand regardless of acceptance tier — the common pattern is 'the tool everyone needs and no one else has released'.

### Cognitive Barriers
- Weight updates are conventionally treated as atomic scalars added to a matrix; recognizing that magnitude and direction are functionally distinct components that benefit from separate optimization requires a Riemannian differential geometry perspective on parameter space that is not part of standard deep learning training.
- The instinct when two operations are composed in a pipeline is to optimize them independently; recognizing that quantization distortion and LoRA initialization create a joint system with a specific algebraic pathology — and that the fix is initializing the second to compensate for the first — requires modeling the composition as a single system rather than a sequence of independent steps.
- Symmetries in neural networks are studied as theoretical equivalences for understanding; the insight that they define a navigable manifold and that traversal along this manifold (teleportation) reduces curvature is a non-standard operational use of group theory that requires bridging abstract algebra with optimization geometry.
- The conventional strategy for gradient conflict in multi-task or multi-objective learning is to adjust loss weights; the insight that a structural gradient stop (blocking the discriminator's gradient from the shared embedding) resolves a directional conflict without sacrificing parameter sharing requires understanding gradient flow at the mechanistic level, not the loss level.

### Reviewer Expectations
Reviewers in this cluster expect: (1) A precise geometric or algebraic diagnosis of why the existing method underperforms, not just an empirical observation that it does. (2) A proposed fix that has minimal computational overhead and is a drop-in replacement or easy integration — methods requiring new training infrastructure are scrutinized more. (3) Theoretical characterization of the fix's properties (variance reduction, convergence rate, global optimum structure) with supporting lemmas that are independently useful. (4) Experiments across at least two model families or task domains to prevent overfitting to a specific architecture. (5) For compression or efficiency papers: actual wall-clock benchmarks, not just FLOPs or parameter counts. Uniform high reviewer scores (8/8/8) come from papers where every claimed benefit is backed by both theory and measurement.

### Oral vs Rejected Gap
Oral papers diagnosed a specific geometric or algebraic root cause and provided a provably minimal fix with measured efficiency benefits and multi-domain validation, while rejected papers either lacked sufficient differentiation from prior work on the same geometric idea (subspace projection overlapping existing low-rank methods) or had limited experimental scope that failed to demonstrate the practical efficiency gains central to their claims. The single most important difference is whether the diagnosis is precise enough to yield a unique, non-obvious fix.

### Oral vs High-Cited Gap
Oral papers established elegant geometric principles (gradient subspace projection, implicit preconditioning via warp layers, Rao-Blackwellization of gradient estimators) with theoretical depth, while high-cited papers in this cluster primarily created community infrastructure — better open models (GLM-130B), improvements to dominant methods (DoRA over LoRA), scaling laws for tools the community was already using (sparse autoencoders). The gap is between advancing understanding and providing what the community needs right now: high-cited papers in this cluster are tools, not theories.

### Illustrative Examples
- **[Oral]** *Gradient Projection Memory for Continual Learning*: Projects gradient updates onto the orthogonal complement of past-task gradient subspaces (extracted via SVD), preventing interference without storing data or expanding the network.
  - Lesson: Shifting the object of protection from weight space to gradient subspace is a paradigmatic geometric reparameterization — the SVD-based extraction is tractable, the privacy byproduct is a compelling bonus, and the method is a clean drop-in for the standard CL setting.
- **[Oral]** *LoftQ: LoRA-Fine-Tuning-aware Quantization for Large Language Models*: Alternates between finding the best quantized weight and the best SVD-based low-rank residual approximation, initializing LoRA to compensate for quantization distortion rather than starting from zero.
  - Lesson: Viewing quantization and LoRA as a joint system rather than a pipeline reveals a specific initialization pathology with an elegant algebraic fix — this precision in diagnosing the root cause is exactly what earns oral-level recognition.
- **[HC]** *DoRA: Weight-Decomposed Low-Rank Adaptation*: Decomposes weight vectors into magnitude and direction components, applying LoRA only to direction while learning magnitudes independently, recovering full fine-tuning dynamics at LoRA efficiency.
  - Lesson: High citation impact comes from improving the community's dominant tool (LoRA) with a principled fix that requires minimal implementation change — the paper's citation trajectory reflects practitioner adoption, not theoretical novelty.
- **[HC]** *GLM-130B: An Open Bilingual Pre-trained Model*: First fully open 100B+ model with documented training instability solutions (embedding gradient shrinking) and INT4 quantization for accessible deployment.
  - Lesson: Engineering transparency and open-sourcing at a capability threshold the community needs creates more citation impact than algorithmic novelty — the paper's 1,252 citations reflect that it filled a vacuum, not that it introduced a better algorithm.
- **[Rejected]** *Transformer Compression via Subspace Projection*: Projects all transformer weights into a shared SVD-derived subspace for globally consistent compression, but overlaps significantly with prior low-rank approximation work.
  - Lesson: Working in the same geometric space (SVD, low-rank subspace) as prior work without a clearly differentiated contribution and inadequate baseline comparison leads to rejection — the diagnosis of the failure is not specific enough to yield a novel fix.

## C17: Structural Assumption Replacement in Optimization Theory
*51 Oral, 2 High-Cited, 7 Rejected*

### Core Principle
Diagnose that a widely-used theoretical assumption is not a genuine requirement of the problem but an artifact of the proof technique or an artifact of domain conventions, then replace it with a weaker, more structurally appropriate condition — thereby extending theoretical results to the practical regime, recovering explanations for heuristics that already work, or converting intractable landscapes into tractable ones. The key move is distinguishing proof artifacts from mathematical necessities and finding the natural condition that actually governs the phenomenon.

### Step-by-Step
- Step 1: Identify a persistent gap between theory and practice — a widely-used algorithm lacks theoretical grounding under realistic conditions, or theoretical results require assumptions (strong convexity, L-infinity score accuracy, constant smoothness) that empirical systems never satisfy.
- Step 2: Carefully diagnose whether the restrictive assumption is a genuine mathematical necessity or a proof artifact: measure whether the assumption actually holds (empirical verification of (L0,L1)-smoothness on ResNets), or analyze what happens to the proof when it is relaxed (switching from L-infinity to L2 score assumptions in diffusion theory).
- Step 3: Identify the structurally correct replacement assumption — one that is both weaker than the original and naturally satisfied by the target problem class (self-concordance for barrier functions and BFGS, (L0,L1)-smoothness for neural network training, quasi-cocoercivity for minimax optimization induced by the right prediction point).
- Step 4: Develop the new proof machinery that exploits the replacement assumption — switching from L-infinity to L2 requires entirely new analytical techniques, not just tighter constants; the radial-tangential decomposition of gradient flows for homogeneous networks is an independently useful lemma.
- Step 5: Verify that the replacement yields qualitatively stronger or more general results — not just slightly improved constants but a genuinely new regime (from local to global convergence for BFGS, from restricted to general network architectures, from discounted to undiscounted RL).
- Step 6: Provide a clean, reproducible empirical validation that confirms the new assumption holds in practice and demonstrates that the theoretical prediction matches empirical behavior — theory papers in this cluster require at least minimal experiments confirming the key assumptions.

### Success Conditions (from Oral)
- The replaced assumption is one that the community has implicitly accepted as necessary but that empirically or theoretically can be shown not to hold or not to be required — 'Why Gradient Clipping Accelerates Training' directly measured (L0,L1)-smoothness violations on ResNets and LSTMs, and the data confirmed the standard assumption was wrong.
- The replacement yields a qualitative improvement in the strength of the result, not just a constant factor — 'Sampling is as easy as learning the score' replaced L-infinity with L2 accuracy and achieved the first polynomial bounds without log-concavity, which is a regime-level improvement; 'Affine-Invariant BFGS under Self-Concordance' moved from local to global convergence guarantees.
- The new proof technique introduces a mathematical tool that is independently reusable — the radial/tangential gradient decomposition of Lyu & Li for homogeneous networks, the Lie algebra framework for conservation laws, the inverse trajectory pooling coefficient for TD learning — each is explicitly cited by reviewers as an independently valuable contribution.
- The result closes a long-standing open question or resolves a known gap between theory and practice, and the paper explicitly identifies this as such — 'On the Statistical Benefits of Temporal Difference Learning' settled the question of when and why TD outperforms direct estimation; 'Gradient Descent Maximizes the Margin' proved the first result for nonlinear homogeneous networks.
- For minimax and constrained optimization papers: the improved complexity bound matches a known lower bound or achieves the same rate as an easier problem class (single-loop O(ε^{-2}) for NC-NC minimax matching convex-concave rates), establishing optimality of the approach.

### Failure Modes (from Rejected)
- The proposed method is not actually distinct from an existing well-known method — the NGD for PINNs paper was rejected because reviewers identified it as equivalent to Gauss-Newton without a clear differentiation, which is a foundational identity concern that no amount of theoretical cleanup can resolve.
- Experiments deviate from the theoretical setup in important ways, making it unclear whether the theoretical claims support the empirical claims — the PINN blow-up analysis paper had theory for one setting and experiments in a different setting, leaving the key claim unverified.
- The convergence measure used is non-standard for the problem class — using gradient norm instead of duality gap for minimax problems is a fundamental mismatch that signals incomplete understanding of the problem geometry; the shuffled extragradient paper was rejected for this reason.
- Theoretical results that are sound but too limited to be impactful: dimension-free greedy coordinate descent rates are elegant but validated only on synthetic linear least squares, missing the primary application (SVM training) where the result would be most significant.
- Combining two existing techniques without proving that their interaction yields a qualitatively new property — MALCOM-PSGD combined model sparsification with gradient compression but failed to achieve linear speedup, with reviewers noting the claimed improvement in contributions was directly contradicted by the analysis.

### Impact Drivers (from High-Cited)
- The Adjoint Schrödinger Bridge Sampler attracted citations by providing a cleaner and more principled formulation of an active area (Schrödinger bridge/diffusion samplers for Boltzmann distributions) that avoided the instabilities of importance-weighted alternatives — it is a foundational tool paper for an emerging subfield.
- BFGS under Self-Concordance became a reference result for the quasi-Newton theory community because it resolved a long-standing open problem (global convergence without strong convexity) and did so with affine-invariant rates applicable to a broad function class including log-barrier objectives, making it broadly applicable in constrained optimization.
- High-cited theory papers in this cluster succeed because they provide the first rigorous explanation of why an already widely-used tool works — this is distinct from proposing a new algorithm, and it creates lasting citation value as every subsequent paper using that tool needs to cite the theoretical foundation.
- The combination of a surprising or counter-intuitive result (one sample suffices for robust pacing; over-parametrization converts spurious minima to saddle points; self-concordance suffices for global BFGS convergence) with formal proof and empirical confirmation drives both expert adoption and broad citation.

### Cognitive Barriers
- The community has internalized standard assumptions (strong convexity for optimization, L-infinity score bounds for diffusion, constant smoothness for gradient descent) as if they were definitional; recognizing that these are proof artifacts rather than mathematical requirements demands reading prior proofs closely enough to distinguish what the proof actually uses from what the theorem states.
- Identifying when two separately developed algorithmic families are discrete approximations of the same continuous mathematical object (SMLD and DDPM as special cases of a single SDE, Meta-CL's gradient alignment as implicit Hessian estimation) requires holding two literatures in mind simultaneously and looking for structural isomorphisms rather than surface-level similarities.
- Converting a known non-convex optimization problem into a structurally tractable one (lifting spurious minima into strict saddle points, constructing a smooth surjective parameterization of an SDP constraint set, finding the natural dual variable that makes the problem minimax) requires facility with convex and differential geometry that is not part of standard ML training.
- The instinct for theory papers is to add stronger assumptions to get stronger results; the opposite direction — weakening assumptions and showing the result still holds, or identifying the minimal assumption that makes the result true — is counter-intuitive and requires a different analytical posture.

### Reviewer Expectations
Reviewers in this cluster expect: (1) A precise identification of which assumption is being replaced and a careful argument (empirical or theoretical) that the original assumption is not a genuine requirement. (2) New proof techniques that are independently useful, not just tightened constants from prior proofs — the novelty of the analytical approach matters as much as the strength of the result. (3) For optimization convergence papers: results that are optimal or near-optimal relative to known lower bounds, covering the full parameter range, not just an improved constant. (4) Experiments that directly test the key theoretical assumption (e.g., verifying (L0,L1)-smoothness, measuring conservation law predictions) — sparse or absent experiments are acceptable only for pure theory papers, and even then must be accompanied by an explicit statement of why experiments would not add information. (5) Proper differentiation from closely related prior work: conflating the proposed method with an existing one (NGD vs Gauss-Newton) is a fatal flaw; the paper must demonstrate that the replacement assumption and resulting analysis are genuinely new.

### Oral vs Rejected Gap
Oral papers replaced a standard theoretical assumption with a structurally appropriate weaker one and developed new proof machinery that yielded qualitatively stronger results (global vs local convergence, polynomial vs exponential bounds, realistic vs unrealistic assumptions), while rejected papers either confused the proposed method with an existing one at a foundational level (NGD vs Gauss-Newton), used a non-standard convergence measure for the problem class (gradient norm for minimax), or validated in settings that diverged from the theoretical setup. The single most important differentiator is whether the theoretical contribution is genuinely distinct from what already exists in a way that the authors can clearly articulate.

### Oral vs High-Cited Gap
Oral papers in this cluster are predominantly theoretical foundations that establish correct, rigorous results and may take years to accumulate widespread citations, while high-cited papers tend to either resolve long-standing open problems that the entire field was waiting for (global BFGS convergence under self-concordance) or provide a cleaner formulation of an emerging practical tool (adjoint Schrödinger bridges for Boltzmann sampling). The key distinction is whether the result is needed right now by a large community (BFGS theory is needed by constrained optimization practitioners) or provides a theoretical foundation that is primarily important for specialists — high-cited papers serve immediate community needs even within pure theory.

### Illustrative Examples
- **[Oral]** *Why Gradient Clipping Accelerates Training: A Theoretical Justification for Adaptivity*: Replaces constant-smoothness assumption with gradient-norm-dependent (L0,L1)-smoothness, empirically verified on ResNets and LSTMs, proving clipped GD converges arbitrarily faster than fixed-step GD.
  - Lesson: Measuring whether the standard assumption empirically holds before trying to build theory on it is an underused strategy — finding that it systematically fails on real networks unlocks a new theoretical regime and explains a decades-old practical mystery.
- **[Oral]** *Sampling is as easy as learning the score: theory for diffusion models with minimal data assumptions*: Replaces L-infinity score accuracy with L2 accuracy (which networks are actually trained with) and proves polynomial convergence for DDPMs without log-concavity, providing the first realistic theoretical foundation for the most successful generative model family.
  - Lesson: The gap between what networks are optimized for (L2 regression) and what theory required (L-infinity accuracy) was a proof artifact, not a mathematical necessity — identifying this gap and closing it with entirely new proof machinery constitutes a foundational contribution.
- **[Oral]** *Over-parametrization via Lifting for Low-rank Matrix Sensing*: Proves that lifting hierarchy converts spurious local minima to strict saddle points — bad solutions do not disappear but become escapable, which is a counter-intuitive and previously unanticipated mechanism.
  - Lesson: The prior community focused on proving spurious solutions vanish or on avoiding them; asking the opposite question ('what structural property do they acquire under over-parametrization?') yields a richer and more actionable theoretical landscape.
- **[HC]** *Affine-Invariant Global Non-Asymptotic Convergence Analysis of BFGS under Self-Concordance*: Replaces strong convexity with self-concordance — the natural condition for barrier functions — yielding the first global non-asymptotic linear and superlinear convergence guarantees for BFGS from arbitrary initialization.
  - Lesson: Self-concordance is not a weakening of strong convexity but a structurally different condition that is naturally satisfied by log-barrier functions; recognizing this required seeing the problem from interior-point methods theory rather than convex optimization — the cross-field perspective is what unlocked the result.
- **[Rejected]** *Stochastic Extragradient with Flip-Flop Shuffling & Anchoring*: Combines shuffled sampling and anchoring for minimax optimization but uses gradient norm as convergence measure instead of the standard duality gap, undermining the theoretical contribution.
  - Lesson: Using a non-standard convergence measure for a well-studied problem class signals that the authors have not fully absorbed the field's methodological standards — duality gap is standard for minimax, and deviating from it requires explicit justification that this paper did not provide.
- **[Rejected]** *Convergence Analysis of Natural Gradient Descent for Over-parameterized PINNs*: Proves NGD achieves O(1) learning rate and quadratic convergence for PINNs, but was rejected because reviewers identified the proposed NGD as equivalent to Gauss-Newton without clear differentiation, and no experiments were provided.
  - Lesson: A foundational identity concern — is your method actually different from an existing well-known method? — must be resolved before any theoretical results can be evaluated; unresolved foundational concerns are fatal regardless of the quality of downstream analysis.
