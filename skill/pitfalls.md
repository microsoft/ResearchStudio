# Common Pitfalls by Innovation Pattern

Failure modes extracted from rejected/low-scored papers within each innovation cluster.
Use this as a checklist after generating an idea.

## C00: Adversarial Inversion and Security Diagnostic Frameworks

1. Proposing a creative but theoretically loose approximation (e.g., NTK-based single-level GAN objective) where the approximation gap invalidates the main claim in practice — reviewers penalize loose theoretical surrogates heavily when empirical performance does not compensate.
2. Claiming stealth or efficiency without validating against adaptive defenses that are aware of the specific strategy — stealth claims that rely only on non-adaptive evaluation are uniformly rejected.
3. Poor writing quality and organizational clarity, including undefined concepts, unexplained figures, and missing motivation — 7 uniform low-score rejections for Certified Copy illustrate that technical motivation must be stated explicitly even for mechanistically sound ideas.
4. Insufficient empirical scope: evaluating only on small/standard benchmarks (CIFAR-10 only, one or two datasets) when claiming broad applicability to a practical safety or robustness problem.
5. Lack of differentiation from prior adaptive attack work — rejected papers in this cluster shared the failure of not positioning their innovation relative to the closest existing evasion strategies.

**Oral vs Rejected Gap**: Oral papers provide a named structural diagnosis with causal evidence and validate against aware adversaries, turning an empirical pattern into an actionable mechanism. Rejected papers in this cluster either relied on theoretically loose approximations that didn't transfer to practice (NTK-GAN), or proposed valid-sounding mechanisms without positioning them against the closest prior adaptive attacks — leaving reviewers unable to assess novelty.

## C01: Structural Decomposition and Cross-Domain Algorithmic Transfer

1. Missing comparison with the most relevant prior work that uses the same structural idea — rejected papers in this cluster were consistently penalized for missing compatibility-matrix methods, prior sheaf network applications, or existing coarsening-based approaches that made novelty claims impossible to evaluate.
2. Framework-without-algorithm: assembling existing techniques (clustering + coarsening + structure learning) into a pipeline without a fundamentally new algorithmic component, yielding insufficient novelty despite addressing a genuine engineering problem.
3. Theory-practice gap in the core approximation: proposing a theoretically motivated assumption (fixed-precision arithmetic model, NTK discriminator approximation) that does not map convincingly to the actual deployed systems reviewers know about — the burden is on the paper to close this gap explicitly.
4. Evaluation limited to toy or simple benchmarks (MNIST-only deep clustering, CIFAR-10-only adversarial training, synthetic data only for topological features) when the method claims broad applicability — reviewers require performance on the hardest standard benchmarks.
5. Claiming efficiency without hardware or latency benchmarks: proposing a lookup-table inference scheme (ClusComp) or a novel inference mechanism without measuring actual GPU latency, cache pressure, or throughput makes the 'efficient' claim unverifiable and leads to rejection.

**Oral vs Rejected Gap**: Oral papers in this cluster provide a formal guarantee (expressiveness proof, convergence bound, necessary-and-sufficient characterization) alongside empirical SOTA results, and position against all closest related methods. Rejected papers either miss the critical prior-work comparison that makes novelty evaluable, or assemble existing techniques into a pipeline without providing a new algorithmic core — they solve real problems but cannot demonstrate sufficient novelty over the combination of the individual components they use.

## C02: Causal Structure Exploitation for Identifiable Inference

1. Identifiability not established for the proposed latent causal model — the most common rejection reason in this cluster: claiming to jointly learn causal representation and graph structure without proving the result is identifiable from the data generating process, leading to results that cannot be distinguished from an arbitrary solution.
2. Mathematical presentation errors or poorly defined notation in theoretical proofs — even a conceptually valid extension (soft interventions in causal representation learning) was rejected when key definitions were incorrect and proofs contained reviewer-identified errors.
3. Methodological combination without theoretical innovation: assembling representation learning with existing IV bounds (CATE partial identification) as a pipeline without new theoretical guarantees, producing marginal empirical improvements that do not justify publication as a theoretical contribution.
4. Evaluation limited to synthetic or toy settings (simple toy images, synthetic IVs) when theoretical claims cover broad real-world applicability — causal inference reviewers require validation on real-world datasets from the target domain.
5. Assuming a weaker assumption holds without rigorously verifying it: claiming that the exclusion restriction is preserved by the learned instrument representation, or that soft intervention switching is identifiable, without a formal proof that the construction actually satisfies the required causal independence conditions.

**Oral vs Rejected Gap**: Oral papers establish formal identifiability or optimality results with correctly stated and verifiable assumptions, and demonstrate a counterintuitive consequence that justifies the causal framing over simpler alternatives. Rejected papers in this cluster failed at one of three points: they could not establish identifiability for their proposed latent model (Learning Latent SCMs), they contained mathematical errors in key definitions (Implicit CRL), or they combined existing techniques without new theoretical guarantees (instrument representation learning for CATE bounds).

## C03: Cross-Modal Transfer and Representation Reframing

1. Claiming novelty for combining existing components (e.g., TCN + TGN + self-attention) without proposing any new module or principled design rationale — reviewers explicitly called this 'too incremental for ICLR' even when results improved over baselines.
2. Presenting only qualitative or abstract claims without quantitative benchmarks — the stochastic interpolants rejection shows that even theoretically elegant ideas are rejected when FID/LPIPS scores or direct comparisons are absent.
3. Failing to respond to or address specific reviewer concerns in the rebuttal phase — multiple rejections in this cluster note that authors did not engage with gradient analysis challenges, missing baselines, or undefined terminology.
4. Applying existing methodology (LoRA, VQ-VAE tokenization) to a new domain without any methodological novelty and without demonstrating that the pre-trained model cannot already handle the domain adequately.
5. Testing only on synthetic or rendered data without validating on real-world inputs — reviewers consistently flagged this as limiting real-world applicability, particularly for 3D editing and medical imaging papers.

**Oral vs Rejected Gap**: Oral papers in this cluster validate their core innovation with multi-domain ablations that isolate the transferred component, while rejected papers either lack quantitative experiments entirely or combine existing components without any new module, making it impossible to determine what produces the gain. The single most discriminating factor is whether the paper demonstrates that the target domain property (quality, structure, invariance) could not be achieved without the specific transferred mechanism.

## C04: Sequential Decision-Making via Self-Improvement and Objective Reframing

1. Testing only one narrow instantiation of a broad general framework — the ToT rejection explicitly noted that 'only one narrow implementation was tested despite the general architecture proposed,' making it impossible to assess generality.
2. Missing critical baselines: rejected papers uniformly failed to compare against the strongest recent concurrent method (ACT for imitation learning, strong multi-agent RL methods for coordination), making claims of improvement unverifiable.
3. Mismatch between the stated problem and the actual evaluation: the slow-hardware RL paper was rejected because 'the approach seems to target high action frequency rather than perceptual delay,' revealing a disconnect between the framing and the solution.
4. Over-reliance on simple environments for evaluation (Minigrid only, two task types, single simulator) — reviewers in this cluster repeatedly penalized limited evaluation scope, with explicit concern about generalization to StarCraft II, complex planning, or real-world settings.
5. Incremental contribution framed as a novel framework: assembling existing components (off-policy RL + diffusion + filtering) without identifying and addressing the specific failure mode that makes the combination necessary yields rejection for insufficient novelty.

**Oral vs Rejected Gap**: Oral papers provide both a formal theoretical motivation and multi-environment empirical evidence, while rejected papers either test only on a single narrow setting or fail to compare against the most relevant recent baselines. The most discriminating failure in this cluster is evaluation scope: every rejection cited insufficient breadth, while every Oral paper included at least three distinct evaluation domains.

## C05: Symmetry and Geometry as Architectural First Principles

1. Proposing a symmetry-aware architecture for an incremental improvement on a domain-specific architecture where a simpler existing method already works adequately — the Hodge-aware simplicial complex paper was rejected for 'limited applications and no evaluation on widely-used benchmarks,' while the Canonic Spike Coding paper failed for insufficient novelty in the core coding scheme.
2. Claiming a new theoretical result that is actually subsumed by or derivable from existing literature using simpler techniques — the universal approximation paper was rejected because 'a stronger result could be proved using well-known simpler techniques the authors appear unaware of,' indicating insufficient literature coverage.
3. Testing the symmetry approach on only one narrow task (single country-capital task, single evaluation domain) without demonstrating that the structural insight generalizes — scope limitation is the primary rejection reason for cluster-5 papers.
4. Proposing a bio-inspired mechanism (predictive coding for GNN robustness, evolutionary search for SNN feedback) without rigorously testing it against strong adversarial baselines or showing a clear advantage over standard methods under challenging conditions.
5. Providing ablations that do not isolate each symmetry component's contribution — the Hodge-aware paper was rejected partly because 'ablation study does not clearly isolate each Hodge component's contribution,' preventing assessment of which symmetry constraint actually matters.

**Oral vs Rejected Gap**: Oral papers provide complete theoretical characterizations (full symmetry groups, formal proofs, completeness results) combined with multi-domain empirical validation, while rejected papers either provide incremental theoretical extensions without awareness of stronger existing results, or test narrow single-domain applications without demonstrating that the symmetry insight generalizes. The single most discriminating factor is theoretical completeness: partial equivariance results or narrowly tested architectures do not reach oral level in this cluster.

## C06: Domain-Grounded Structural Bias for Scientific ML

1. Domain motivation without performance advantage: 'Contrastive Learning of Molecular Representation with Fragmented Views' is chemically well-motivated but rejected because performance gains over existing molecular contrastive learning baselines 'were not significant enough to justify the contribution'. Merely arguing chemical plausibility is insufficient — gains must be clear and consistent across tasks.
2. Applying existing algorithm to new domain without algorithmic novelty: 'Identifying key amino acid types using Shapley values' was rejected explicitly for 'applying an existing algorithm to a new domain without significant algorithmic novelty'. The insight must be both domain-specific and technically novel — domain application alone does not meet ML venue standards.
3. Failing to generalize beyond the specific domain: Rejected papers like trace reconstruction with language models fail when the approach requires dataset-specific fine-tuning and cannot generalize to different error probabilities. Reviewers specifically identify this as a fatal flaw — domain-specific methods must show principled generalization.
4. Metaphorical framing masking trivial mechanism: The 'Social Hierarchy-Guided NAS' paper is rejected because the social hierarchy metaphor 'reduces to fitness-weighted selection, which is standard in evolutionary algorithms'. Structural innovation must be mechanistically novel, not just rebranded standard techniques with a creative name.
5. Insufficient evaluation breadth: Multiple rejected papers are criticized for 'limited downstream tasks', 'only one model size tested', or 'evaluation domains not sufficiently diverse'. Domain-focused papers must demonstrate that the domain bias generalizes across multiple benchmarks, not just a single cherry-picked evaluation.

**Oral vs Rejected Gap**: The single most important difference is that Oral papers achieve clear, significant performance gains that justify the domain-motivated complexity, while rejected papers have sound conceptual motivation but marginal or context-specific improvements — reviewers in this cluster accept the premise that domain knowledge helps but demand proof that this specific encoding of domain knowledge provides a qualitatively different capability or substantially better results, not incremental gains on limited evaluations.

## C07: Probabilistic Reformulation for Generative Model Design

1. Prior work has already used the key mathematical tool: 'On Fitting Flow Models with Large Sinkhorn Couplings' is likely rejected because OT-based couplings for flow matching were already used in prior work (Lipman et al.) — the paper did not differentiate its contribution sufficiently. In this cluster, the mathematical tool must be either genuinely new to the problem class or applied with a non-trivial modification that enables a new capability.
2. Theoretical framework disconnected from practical contribution: 'Conditional Lagrangian Wasserstein Flow for Time Series Imputation' is rejected because 'theoretical elements feel disconnected from practical contribution' and reviewers identified potential implementation errors. A reformulation paper must show that the mathematical framework *causes* the empirical improvement, not just accompanies it.
3. Architectural modification without theoretical justification: 'Jakiro' applies MoE to an existing speculative decoding framework and is rejected for 'incremental nature' and 'insufficient theoretical justification'. In this cluster, adding components without a principled mathematical rationale (why does this formulation guarantee better diversity?) is insufficient — the contribution must be the formulation, not the architecture.

**Oral vs Rejected Gap**: The critical difference is that Oral papers demonstrate that their mathematical reformulation enables a *structurally new capability* — enforcing distributional constraints, reducing parameters by 10x via algorithm-as-encoder, amortizing hyperparameter search into a single training run — while rejected papers apply mathematical tools (optimal transport, Lagrangian mechanics, MoE) to existing formulations without demonstrating what is now possible that was previously impossible.

## C08: Decomposition-Based LLM Capability Elicitation

1. Writing quality and depth insufficient for venue: 'Lucy: Think and Reason to Solve Text-to-SQL' is rejected because 'the paper lacks depth and writing quality does not meet NeurIPS standards'. In cluster 8, the LLM+tool decomposition idea is familiar to reviewers; a paper needs rigorous evaluation, strong writing, and clear contribution differentiation — not just a working pipeline.
2. Incoherent or AI-generated methodology: 'A Dual-Modal Framework Utilizing Visual Prompts for Enhanced Patch Analysis' receives all four 1/10 ratings as reviewers unanimously identify it as 'likely AI-generated with incoherent methodology'. In a cluster where the community is expert in LLM outputs, papers with disconnected mathematical formulations and ambiguous methodology are rapidly identified as lacking genuine scientific content.
3. Limited performance vs. baseline methods despite principled framing: 'ARC-AGI Without Pretraining' is likely rejected for achieving only 20% on ARC-AGI compared to pretraining-based approaches, and for 'insufficient theoretical grounding of why MDL enables the observed generalization'. Principled alternatives (MDL as compression) without competitive performance cannot justify rejection of the dominant paradigm.

**Oral vs Rejected Gap**: The single most important difference is execution quality and contribution specificity: Oral papers identify a precisely named capability gap, design a decomposition with a clear mechanism that addresses it, and demonstrate results across multiple tasks with honest ablations — rejected papers propose pipelines for legitimate problems but lack depth, rigorous evaluation, or clear differentiation from existing approaches, and in the extreme case (AI-generated paper) lack genuine scientific content entirely.

## C09: Diagnostic Metric Design for LLM Reasoning Evaluation

1. Insufficient novelty over existing approaches: adding a process reward model to outcome-based RL (SophiaVL-R1) without a principled new diagnostic or a theoretically grounded new objective — reviewers see it as engineering without insight.
2. Interesting phenomenon identified but methodological rigor insufficient: 'underthinking' (Thoughts Are All Over the Place) was genuinely novel but the evaluation was insufficiently robust and the proposed fix (TIP decoding) was ad hoc without theoretical justification.
3. Evaluation scope too narrow to support general claims: a phenomenon measured on a single model family or single reasoning domain raises doubts about whether the finding generalizes.
4. The metric or intervention is not clearly motivated by theory: rejections occur when diagnostic design choices (switching penalty magnitude, threshold values) lack principled selection methods.
5. Treating a symptom rather than diagnosing a root cause: applying a penalty at decoding time without understanding why the problem occurs — reviewers demand mechanistic understanding alongside the fix.

**Oral vs Rejected Gap**: Oral papers introduce a principled diagnostic that reveals a theoretically motivated failure mode invisible to standard metrics, then demonstrate the failure with broad empirical evidence across multiple models and tasks. Rejected papers either identify interesting phenomena but lack methodological rigor and principled justification for their interventions (underthinking paper), or apply existing PRM frameworks to a new modality without a novel diagnostic insight.

## C10: Cross-Domain Knowledge Import for AI Systems and Evaluation

1. The structural mapping is implicit or incomplete: CAAP imports visual perception framing for computer control but fails to articulate why this framing is better than existing approaches, leaving reviewers unconvinced of novelty over GUI agent baselines.
2. The design choices in the import are not justified: PiCO imports peer review for LLM evaluation but the elimination threshold (60%), aggregation method, and other design parameters lack principled selection, making the import seem arbitrary rather than validated.
3. The empirical validation is too narrow: testing only on a specific application domain (single software company workflows, single medical benchmark) raises generalizability concerns that rejected papers do not adequately address.
4. The analogy is metaphorical rather than structural: papers that merely use source domain vocabulary without formally instantiating the source domain's mechanisms are rejected for superficial framing without technical depth.
5. The import adds complexity without demonstrable benefit over simpler alternatives: rejected papers fail to show that the additional machinery (bilevel optimization, multi-agent coordination overhead) yields improvements justifying the added cost.

**Oral vs Rejected Gap**: Oral papers provide a rigorous structural mapping from a non-ML domain to the AI problem and validate the import with comprehensive experiments on realistic benchmarks, often introducing a named concept or evaluation environment that the community adopts. Rejected papers provide shallow engineering integration (CAAP) or unjustified design parameter choices (PiCO), making the import seem arbitrary rather than principled — reviewers cannot distinguish the design from alternative implementations without a theory of why the specific choices were made.

## C11: Assumption Inversion and Structural Constraint in Transfer Learning

1. Incremental improvement without assumption inversion: RangeAugment learns magnitude ranges online instead of fixing them, but this is a refinement within the existing augmentation paradigm rather than an inversion of any core assumption — reviewers see 6/10 or lower when the core idea is 'learn this hyperparameter adaptively' without a structural insight.
2. Insufficient differentiation from prior merging techniques: StatsMerging uses teacher statistics to guide model merging, but fails to clearly articulate what assumption prior merging methods make that this inverts, or why statistical overlap is the right proxy — reviewers cannot evaluate whether the design choices are principled.
3. Marginal empirical gains that do not justify additional complexity: RangeAugment's bilevel optimization overhead makes it slower than RandAugment without commensurate performance improvement — the assumption inversion must produce gains large enough to motivate the structural change.
4. Theory that does not connect to the empirical setting: papers providing theory for two-layer linear networks but experimenting with deep nonlinear networks face skepticism about whether the theory explains the observed phenomenon or is merely illustrative.
5. Evaluation scope that raises generalization concerns: papers evaluated on a single domain (only NLP sequence tagging, only visual domains) without discussing where the assumption inversion would or would not hold.

**Oral vs Rejected Gap**: Oral papers invert a core assumption with theoretical justification and introduce a complementary structural constraint that makes the inversion tractable, then validate with broad empirical coverage. Rejected papers propose incremental refinements within the existing paradigm (online range learning, statistics-guided merging) without a non-obvious assumption inversion — reviewers can see the idea is a parameter-efficient version of existing approaches rather than a structural departure from them.

## C12: Loss Function Diagnosis and Principled Redesign

1. Empirical superiority without theoretical mechanism: PBES (PCA exemplar selection) showed that PCA-based selection may work, but could not explain mechanistically why PCA specifically is the right tool — rejected for lacking justification beyond benchmark improvement.
2. Confounding training signal with another variable: Imprecise Label Learning was rejected because performance gains were entangled with data augmentation quality, making it impossible to evaluate whether the unified MLE framework itself provided improvement.
3. Inconsistent performance across settings: Input Margins paper improved one benchmark but failed on others, indicating the fix was not principled but rather tuned to specific conditions — a clear red flag for reviewers.
4. Incremental extension without new insight: PAC-Bayes bounds for LAEs was rejected as an incremental extension of Shalaeva et al., extending multivariate regression to a constrained model class without providing new theoretical insight about why the bound is tight or when it fails.
5. Theoretical contribution without practical utility: the temperature-scaling theory paper was rejected for lacking empirical validation and practical deployment guidance despite a correct analytical result.

**Oral vs Rejected Gap**: Oral papers provide a mathematically precise failure-mode diagnosis followed by a principled fix derived from theory, while rejected papers either (a) apply an existing tool (PCA, PAC-Bayes) to a new setting without explaining why that tool is the right one, or (b) show empirical gains whose source cannot be disentangled from confounders like data augmentation or hyperparameter tuning.

## C13: Game-Theoretic Intractability Decomposition and Reduction

1. Constraint relaxation that undermines practical significance: Contextual Bandits with Knapsacks was rejected because the budget constraint was enforced only in expectation — a relaxation that makes the theory tractable but invalidates the results for most real applications. Always validate that theoretical assumptions hold in the target application domain.
2. Good results on one domain with undemonstrated generalization: KrwEmd improved poker hand abstraction via K-recall features, but could not demonstrate that the approach scales beyond Texas Hold'em-style games — top venues expect methods to generalize beyond a single game or problem instance.
3. Novelty as a mathematical framework without practical utility: Cayley Maze introduced an elegant group-theoretic framework for RL environments but was rejected for having no conclusion, undefined key terms, and no experiments — a strong mathematical idea must be accompanied by at least one concrete demonstration of what it enables.
4. Insufficient differentiation from prior work: the Nash equilibrium via meta-learning paper was rejected for insufficient differentiation from the 'learning not to regret' framework, and for limited experimental scope — when working in a well-populated area (regret minimization for Nash), the contribution must be precisely characterized against the closest prior work.
5. Narrow scope limiting impact: the differential game solver was rejected for being limited to 2-player zero-sum with one-sided information under Isaacs' condition — while technically correct, the theoretical contribution was deemed too specialized for a top venue's breadth requirements.

**Oral vs Rejected Gap**: Oral papers close a formally characterized open problem with provably correct substitutions that preserve essential guarantees (convergence, equilibrium, complexity bounds), while rejected papers either (a) make realistic assumptions that simplify the problem enough to undermine practical relevance (budget constraints in expectation only), or (b) demonstrate good results in one specific domain without evidence that the approach generalizes to the broader problem class.

## C14: Tight Bound Resolution via Structural Property Exploitation

1. Providing theory for an impractically restricted model class: 'Data Debugging is NP-hard for Classifiers Trained with SGD' was rejected because the hardness result applied only to linear classifiers, which are too restrictive for practical ML — theoretical results must target model classes that practitioners actually use, or explicitly analyze the gap to practical settings.
2. Theoretical clarification without algorithmic contribution: 'Avoiding Pitfalls for Privacy Accounting of Subsampled Mechanisms' was rejected as more appropriate for a technical note than a full paper — pointing out subtleties in prior analyses without providing a new algorithm, tighter bound, or constructive technique does not meet the bar for a top venue.
3. Unclear motivation and insufficient practical implications: 'Towards a Better Theoretical Understanding of Independent Subnetwork Training' was rejected because the introduction failed to clearly motivate the problem and the practical significance of convergence bounds for IST was not articulated — even technically correct results must explain concretely what becomes possible given the theorem.
4. Incremental extension without new structural insight: papers that extend PAC-Bayes bounds to new model classes, or composition theorems to new settings, without introducing a new mathematical technique or resolving a known gap risk rejection as incremental — the contribution must add a structural insight, not just add a case to an existing theorem.
5. Tight bounds in a setting too narrow for the venue: the differential game solver (Isaacs' condition, 2-player zero-sum, one-sided information) had correct and novel results but was deemed too specialized — the structural insight must apply to a problem class broad enough to be of general interest to the conference audience.

**Oral vs Rejected Gap**: Oral papers introduce a new structural insight (low sensitivity, compositional parallelism, geometric orthogonality, dimension-based characterization) that resolves a gap between known upper and lower bounds, while rejected papers either restrict analysis to too-narrow model classes (linear classifiers only), provide clarification without algorithmic contribution, or fail to articulate why the theoretical result matters for practical systems.

## C15: Variational Reformulation for Intractable RL Objectives

1. Theoretical contributions are not sufficiently novel relative to existing work: the rejected CORSDICE paper identified a real bug (semi-gradient bias) but overclaimed contributions and did not acknowledge concurrent work, while the distributional OPE paper had solid theory but used realizability assumptions that cannot hold in practice.
2. Framing findings as observations rather than a principled solution: 'Challenges of Exploration for Offline RL' raised important questions but presented empirical observations without providing an actionable algorithm, leading reviewers to view it as analysis rather than contribution.
3. Theoretical bounds that appear sound but contain large unfavorable constants (density-ratio constant C_sup = p_max/p_min) that make results practically vacuous — reviewers caught this in the distributional off-policy evaluation paper.
4. Incomplete literature survey or failure to properly position relative to prior work: the Constrained PPO paper missed Borkar (2005) and prior constrained actor-critic literature, leading to rejection despite numerically reasonable results.
5. Theory that does not connect back to practical settings: PAC-Bayesian bounds for deep RL agents or sequential policies that are technically valid but still too loose or restricted to specific conditions (realizability, mixing-time estimation) to inform deployment decisions.

**Oral vs Rejected Gap**: Oral papers provided a genuine structural removal of a prior obstacle with non-vacuous guarantees and tested on harder benchmarks, while rejected papers either identified real problems without offering principled solutions (exploration for offline RL), provided theoretically sound methods with impractically large constants or untestable assumptions (distributional OPE, constrained PPO), or overclaimed novelty relative to existing literature. The single most critical differentiator is whether the paper closes a problem or only frames it.

## C16: Geometric Reparameterization for Efficient Neural Optimization

1. Insufficient differentiation from prior work in the same geometric space — the Transformer Compression via Subspace Projection paper proposed layer-wise SVD compression that reviewers identified as overlapping with existing low-rank approximation methods without adequate empirical or theoretical separation.
2. Limited experimental scope that fails to validate the method beyond toy settings — the LN-to-RMSNorm algebraic equivalence paper was sound in theory but only validated on small-scale datasets without measuring actual training/inference speedup, which is the primary claimed benefit.
3. Incremental extension of a prior method without sufficient new insight — the Symbiotic Tuning paper for side-network adaptation was viewed as modest augmentation of Ladder Side-Tuning without a principled new geometric or optimization insight, and lacked reproducible experimental details.
4. Claiming a benefit that the theoretical analysis contradicts — methods that claim linear speedup but whose convergence analysis shows sub-linear speedup (MALCOM-PSGD) are penalized heavily because this suggests the authors did not fully understand their own result.
5. Missing quantitative runtime benchmarks when efficiency is the primary claim — algebraic simplifications or structural tricks that promise computational savings must be validated with wall-clock measurements, not just parameter counts or theoretical FLOPs.

**Oral vs Rejected Gap**: Oral papers diagnosed a specific geometric or algebraic root cause and provided a provably minimal fix with measured efficiency benefits and multi-domain validation, while rejected papers either lacked sufficient differentiation from prior work on the same geometric idea (subspace projection overlapping existing low-rank methods) or had limited experimental scope that failed to demonstrate the practical efficiency gains central to their claims. The single most important difference is whether the diagnosis is precise enough to yield a unique, non-obvious fix.

## C17: Structural Assumption Replacement in Optimization Theory

1. The proposed method is not actually distinct from an existing well-known method — the NGD for PINNs paper was rejected because reviewers identified it as equivalent to Gauss-Newton without a clear differentiation, which is a foundational identity concern that no amount of theoretical cleanup can resolve.
2. Experiments deviate from the theoretical setup in important ways, making it unclear whether the theoretical claims support the empirical claims — the PINN blow-up analysis paper had theory for one setting and experiments in a different setting, leaving the key claim unverified.
3. The convergence measure used is non-standard for the problem class — using gradient norm instead of duality gap for minimax problems is a fundamental mismatch that signals incomplete understanding of the problem geometry; the shuffled extragradient paper was rejected for this reason.
4. Theoretical results that are sound but too limited to be impactful: dimension-free greedy coordinate descent rates are elegant but validated only on synthetic linear least squares, missing the primary application (SVM training) where the result would be most significant.
5. Combining two existing techniques without proving that their interaction yields a qualitatively new property — MALCOM-PSGD combined model sparsification with gradient compression but failed to achieve linear speedup, with reviewers noting the claimed improvement in contributions was directly contradicted by the analysis.

**Oral vs Rejected Gap**: Oral papers replaced a standard theoretical assumption with a structurally appropriate weaker one and developed new proof machinery that yielded qualitatively stronger results (global vs local convergence, polynomial vs exponential bounds, realistic vs unrealistic assumptions), while rejected papers either confused the proposed method with an existing one at a foundational level (NGD vs Gauss-Newton), used a non-standard convergence measure for the problem class (gradient norm for minimax), or validated in settings that diverged from the theoretical setup. The single most important differentiator is whether the theoretical contribution is genuinely distinct from what already exists in a way that the authors can clearly articulate.
